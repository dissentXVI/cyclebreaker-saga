## Description
The Dustway is the largest residential district in [[Tarwa'kem]]'s lower city, sprawling across the ground between the foot of the [[Kemtabi Shelf]] and the city's outer walls in a dense press of tenement blocks, shared courtyards, and narrow lanes that rarely see direct sunlight. The name comes from the persistent fine dust that settles here — carried down from the [[Amrakebir Mountains]] by wind, stirred up by foot traffic, and never quite cleared away. It coats windowsills and doorframes and the hems of clothing, and residents stop noticing it early.

The buildings of the Dustway are functional and layered, constructed from the same pale sandstone as the rest of [[Tarwa'kem]] but without its ornamentation — walls patched where they have cracked, upper stories added where the original structure would bear them, shared cisterns and communal ovens occupying the larger courtyards. Families have lived in the same rooms for generations. The streets are irregular, formed by use rather than plan, and the district is easier to navigate by landmark than by direction. Most residents could walk it in full dark without difficulty. Visitors become turned around quickly.

Quiet shrines to [[Petra]] appear throughout the Dustway with a frequency found nowhere else in [[Tarwa'kem]] — tucked into wall alcoves, maintained on windowsills, placed at the foot of stairwells. They are kept by nurses, midwives, and mothers, and tended with the particular care of people who have reason to ask for protection and cannot afford more formal devotion. The shrines are small and personal, rarely more than a carved figure and a dish for offerings, but they are rarely neglected.

## Significance
The Dustway is [[Tarwa'kem]]'s labor reservoir — the district that wakes earliest, walks furthest, and returns last. Its residents work the [[Ash Ward]]'s forges, staff the [[Sekhet Markets]]' stalls, maintain the estates of [[Providence]], and fill the lower tiers of [[Meninos' Theater]] on fight days. The city depends on them in the way that cities depend on their foundations: completely and without much acknowledgment.

The arrival of D'yadyan workers from [[Misrata]] has sharpened the Dustway's existing tensions into something more volatile. Refugees willing to work for less are displacing Dustway residents from positions they have held for years, and the resentment this generates is directed overwhelmingly at the D'yadyans rather than at the employers — chief among them [[Ngor Azizi]] — who set the terms. The derogatory term *saltheads* is heard more frequently in the Dustway's lanes than anywhere else in the city, and the mood in its tea houses and shared courtyards has grown harder to read in the past few years.

[[Tarik Tazi]] is a figure of genuine weight in the Dustway — not because he visits, but because his story is known here better than anywhere. A man who arrived in [[Tarwa'kem]] with nothing and climbed to General is the kind of story this district holds onto. [[Sultana Neema Karo'kem|Neema]]'s reform platform is watched here with cautious hope, tempered by generations of experience with promises that did not reach the lower city. The Dustway does not trust easily. It remembers too well.
