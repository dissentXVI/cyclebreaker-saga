## Description
The Ipet District occupies the middle band of [[Tarwa'kem]] in every sense — geographically between the lower city and the [[Kemtabi Shelf]], economically between the labor of the [[Dustway]] and the inherited wealth of [[Providence]], and socially in the space that neither fully claims. It is the city's most functionally varied district: a dense patchwork of storefronts, workshops, professional offices, and residential buildings stacked above commercial ground floors. The streets are wider than the [[Dustway]] and more worn than [[Providence]], maintained well enough to suggest investment but not so immaculately as to suggest excess.

The buildings of the Ipet District reflect the aspirations of the people who built them — sandstone facades with modest decorative flourishes, carved lintels bearing family names or trade symbols, awnings in the merchant colors of their owners' guilds. Many properties have been in the same family for two or three generations, expanded incrementally as fortunes allowed: a second story added here, a workshop extended there, a courtyard enclosed to make a proper room. The result is an architecture of accumulation, each building a material record of its family's climb.

Commerce in the Ipet runs differently from the [[Sekhet Markets]]. Where the Sekhet is loud and transient, the Ipet favors the established relationship — the apothecary whose family has supplied the same households for decades, the tailor who knows every measurement without asking, the notary whose discretion is his primary product. Specialty trades and professional services predominate. The district's tea houses and modest dining rooms serve as informal meeting places where deals are made over shared meals between people who know each other well enough not to need witnesses.

## Significance
The Ipet District is [[Tarwa'kem]]'s most politically unstable ground, though it rarely announces itself as such. Its residents have enough to lose to care deeply about the city's direction and enough daily economic pressure to feel every policy shift before it reaches [[Providence]]'s awareness. [[The Monetarist Council]]'s tariffs, guild licensing fees, and trade restrictions land hardest here, where margins are thinner than they appear and a bad season cannot be absorbed the way it can in the upper city.

[[Sultana Neema Karo'kem]]'s reform platform has found genuine support in the Ipet — more cautious than the enthusiasm of the [[Dustway]], more substantive than the polite interest of [[Providence]]. Business owners here want stability and fair access to trade, two things the current council structure does not reliably provide for those outside its inner circle. Whether that support translates into meaningful political weight is a question the district is still working out. The Ipet has no representative on [[the Monetarist Council]] and no formal mechanism for collective voice. What it has is economic indispensability — and the slow, growing awareness that this might be leverage enough, if it were ever organized.
