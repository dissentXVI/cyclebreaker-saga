**Location:** Outside the northeastern walls of [[Tarwa'kem]]
**Affiliation:** None

## Description
The Shai Fields occupy the outermost band of [[Tarwa'kem]]'s settled territory, spread across the relatively flat ground at the mountain's base where the city's walls give way to cultivated land. The terrain here is dry and demanding — not naturally fertile, but made workable over generations by the dwarven irrigation engineering that channels mountain water through a network of stone-lined channels, cisterns, and distribution gates maintained by the farming families themselves. The system is old and reliable when it is maintained, and the labor of keeping it so falls entirely on the people who depend on it.

The fields themselves are divided into plots of varying size, growing staple grains, root vegetables, drought-tolerant herbs, and the date palms that line the irrigation channels in long, regular rows. Interspersed among the working land are the low, thick-walled farmhouses and communal storage structures that house the district's residents — plain sandstone buildings with small windows and flat roofs used for drying crops and sleeping in summer heat. There is little ornamentation here. What resources a family has go into the land and the tools that work it.

The people of the Shai Fields are among [[Tarwa'kem]]'s oldest residents in terms of unbroken presence — farming families whose connection to this specific ground predates several of the merchant dynasties now seated in [[Providence]]. They are not a politically organized community and do not present themselves as one. They know each other, settle disputes among themselves where possible, and share equipment and labor during planting and harvest seasons in arrangements that have no formal name and require none.

## Significance
The Shai Fields feed [[Tarwa'kem]], a fact the city acknowledges minimally and economically. Produce from the fields moves into the [[Sekhet Markets]] at dawn, sold at prices set by market demand and guild intermediaries rather than by the farmers who grew it. The margin between what the fields produce and what their families can live on is thin enough that a poor season or an unfavorable tariff adjustment is felt immediately in the district. Neither circumstance prompts much concern in [[Providence]] or among [[the Monetarist Council]] until supply actually falters.

The Shai Fields sit at the bottom of [[Tarwa'kem]]'s class structure in a way that differs from the [[Dustway]] — not the desperation of urban poverty but the specific invisibility of rural labor that the city could not function without and prefers not to think about. The farmers here are not refugees, not displaced, not agitating for reform. They are simply working, as they have always worked, and watching the city above them grow wealthier in ways that do not reach the irrigation channels. [[Sultana Neema Karo'kem]]'s reform platform has not made significant inroads here — not because the farmers oppose her, but because no one has thought to ask them.
