## Description
Misrata sits on the far bank of the [[Tefuti Stream]], outside Tarwa'kem's walls and beyond the reach of its civic infrastructure. What began as a temporary encampment for D'yadyan refugees fleeing the devastation of [[The Siege of the White Sands]] has grown, over years of displacement, into something that functions like a permanent settlement without ever having been built as one. The structures here are improvised and layered — canvas and salvaged timber, mud-brick walls raised over earlier foundations, lean-tos attached to lean-tos. The streets, such as they are, follow the logic of foot traffic rather than any plan. Water comes from the stream and from charity; sanitation is managed collectively and imperfectly.

Despite the material conditions, Misrata has the texture of a community that has decided to persist. Cooking fires burn at consistent hours. Children attend informal lessons run by elders. Shrines to D'yadyan traditions and displaced devotions are kept with care that the surrounding structures do not suggest is possible. The settlement's inhabitants call it Misrata as-Suhada — the City of Martyrs — a name that carries both grief and defiance, honoring what was lost at the Siege while insisting that the people who survived it are still a people.

From the streets of [[Tarwa'kem]], Misrata is visible across the stream as a low, irregular skyline of smoke and makeshift rooflines. Most Tarwa'kin who reference it do so by the name the city gave it: the Sift — a dismissal wrapped in metaphor, implying that the D'yadyan people who ended up here are what remained after everything of value was strained away. The D'yadyans are aware of the name and what it means.

## Significance
Misrata is the most visible pressure point in [[Tarwa'kem]]'s current social tensions. The refugees who live here cross the [[Tefuti Stream]] daily to work in the [[Ash Ward]], the [[Sekhet Markets]], and the estates of [[Providence]], accepting wages that native Tarwa'kin workers cannot sustain themselves on and will not match. The resulting resentment is directed overwhelmingly at the D'yadyans rather than at the employers who set the terms — among them [[Ngor Azizi]], whose [[Kustara Consortium]] has been the most deliberate and the least apologetic in exploiting the wage differential. The derogatory term *saltheads*, used by Tarwa'kin for D'yadyans, has become more common in the lower districts as economic friction sharpens into something uglier.

[[Tarik Tazi]], himself a D'yadyan refugee who crossed into Tarwa'kem with nothing and rose through [[Meninos' Theater]] to become General of the [[Gilded Legion]], is the settlement's most powerful if unofficial advocate. He visits Misrata regularly and in secret, funding initiatives that move skilled D'yadyan workers into trade schools and temple apprenticeships under assumed names. His presence in the settlement is known to its inhabitants and to very few others.

[[Sultana Neema Karo'kem]]'s reform agenda includes provisions that would extend basic civic protections to the settlement's residents — a position that has made her popular in Misrata and deeply unpopular among the Tarwa'kin workers who see those protections as legitimizing their competition. The stream between Misrata and the city walls is narrow enough to cross in a few steps. The distance it represents is considerably wider.
