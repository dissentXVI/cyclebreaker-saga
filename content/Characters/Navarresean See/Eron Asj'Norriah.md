## Appearance

Asj'Norriah is painted not as the imperious sovereign her successors styled themselves, but as something quieter and more watchful. Dark brown hair braided loosely back, threaded through with small pink and white blooms and a modest gold coronet. Freckles across her nose and cheeks — an unusual detail for imperial portraiture, which typically idealized its subjects beyond recognition. Her expression is composed but genuinely attentive, as though she is looking at you rather than through you. Her green and gold gown is rich but her bearing is not stiff with it. Of all the Navarresean rulers, she is the one who looks like she might stop and listen.

## Personality

The greatest magical mind of the Second Radiant Age, and possibly of any age. Asj'Norriah was defined by an insatiable, rigorous curiosity — one that sat in quiet tension with the empire she served. She mastered and surpassed every Navarresean scholar of her era, pioneered breathstone teleportation, and created the [[Garden of Asj'Norriah]] as both a scientific achievement and a living work of art.

Her contemporaries recorded her habit of inviting representatives of lesser races to study alongside Navarresean scholars with audible condescension — framing it as a personal quirk, a kind of charitable folly. Her own writings, largely lost or destroyed, may have told a different story. The nostos flower she chose for her garden dedication — an extinct bloom with no magical or alchemical value, known only for responding to the emotions of nearby living creatures — is the one surviving clue to what she privately believed.

## Narrative Relevance

Asj'Norriah's legacy is physically present in [[The Cosmic Pillar]] through the [[Garden of Asj'Norriah]] and the [[Arboretum Wing]], which the party has encountered directly. Her breathstone beacon network forms the pneumatic architecture underlying much of the Tower's systems.

She is also a quiet historical counterpoint to the empire's worst impulses — a figure who seems, in the gaps of the record, to have understood that the people Navarre called lesser were not. What she did with that understanding, and why so little of her personal writing survives, are questions the historical record does not answer.
