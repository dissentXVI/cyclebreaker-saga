# Arc One: Laconia and the Kustara Forest

## The Party

Four people find themselves in a rattling wagon on the road to Laconia, drawn together by circumstance and something neither coincidental nor fully understood. They have no particular reason to trust one another yet. That will change.

Gothmog is a green-skinned orc, broad and battle-worn, with the kind of presence that makes a wagon feel smaller than it is. He has come to Laconia as part of his studies at Shurevaire's university, but study is not what drives him. The Vociran Empire stole his homeland of Ilsong, and he has spent his life building himself into the kind of man who can do something about that.

Frog is a middle-aged woman who travels light, speaks less, and misses nothing. Whatever she was before — and there is clearly something she was before — she wears the wilderness now like a second skin. She is heading to Laconia because there is someone there she needs to find: a daughter she gave up years ago and has been circling toward ever since.

Laena Zahir is a Tarwa'kin noblewoman and cleric of Tytos, god of law and justice, who left home without permission. Her little sister is dying of an illness no physician in Tarwa'kem can cure, and Laena has heard enough about the ancient ruins near Laconia to believe the answer might lie somewhere inside them. She carries herself with the practiced composure of someone raised to navigate difficult rooms, which is to say she is already paying more attention to everyone in this wagon than she appears to be.

Ariesa is a tiefling sorcerer with wild magic she has never fully learned to control and a hunger to prove herself that she has never tried to hide. She left her community, the Kula, to seek something larger than what she was allowed to be there — adventure, recognition, and the chance to show a world that distrusts her kind that she can save it anyway.

These four traveled along a small path, the rickety wagon lulling them toward sleep. In the haze between wakefulness and dream, a woman's voice found them — soft, certain, and coming from nowhere:

*"Fall. Find. Fly."*

The explosion that followed was immediate. Goblins poured from the trees, upending the wagon and scattering its contents across the road. The four strangers fought them off in the chaos — and discovered in the process that they were, between them, quite capable of violence. As the last surviving goblin broke and ran, a white-cloaked figure materialized from nowhere, vaporized the retreating creature without a word, and vanished before anyone could speak. The road to Laconia had already become something other than a road to Laconia.

## Laconia

Laconia is a town in the middle of becoming something it hasn't quite figured out yet. The discovery of the Watcher of the Lake has drawn scholars, treasure hunters, and opportunists from across Halinor, and the town has swelled unevenly to accommodate them. The first person the party meets is Yenden Clearlade, a scholar from Shurevaire tasked with overseeing excursions around the Watcher and cataloguing whatever artifacts surface from the surrounding ruins. Shurevaire has a stake in what gets found here, and Yenden is their eyes on the ground.

The Lionstooth is the largest inn in town — new construction, built to meet the population boom — and it doesn't take long to notice that the place is effectively run by a gang called the Fang, though nobody says so plainly. On their way in, the party spots a young boy being shoved around outside by a group of older kids and steps in. The boy is Reyn'lael Joran — half-elven, barely more than a teenager, and beside himself with excitement at being rescued by actual adventurers. He is in Laconia with his mentors from Shurevaire and explains that their expedition needs capable fighters to escort a supply wagon back to Camp Scarlet, a base camp for the scholars working near the Watcher. The goblins in the surrounding area have been growing more aggressive, and the road has become dangerous. He asks the party to help.

Before they set out, the party gets acquainted with the shape of the town. Zeyn Choukri holds no official title, but he is the closest thing Laconia has to law. A D'yadyan refugee who served as a soldier in Tarwa'kem's army before finding himself here, he takes the town's safety seriously in a way the officially posted city-state guards largely do not — most of them treat this as a comfortable posting and act accordingly. Zeyn gives the party a handful of small tasks that amount to a quiet assessment of whether they are worth relying on. They are.

Across town, tucked away from the bustle around the Lionstooth, is the Sailor's Whimsy — smaller, older, and considerably less polished, but not without its warmth. It is run by Mireya Lunacrest, a sixteen-year-old whose parents were killed in the cataclysm known as the Trembling Foundations and who has kept the place running ever since on sheer determination. Jendrick Ironbrook, a quiet and watchful fighter, helps her — not family by blood, but functioning as one in every practical sense.

When the party joins Reyn on the road to Camp Scarlet with the supply wagon, the goblins hit them again — harder this time, and with an unsettling disregard for their own survival that reads less like hunger and more like compulsion. The party fights through and arrives at Camp Scarlet, where they meet the expedition's leaders. Layla Basura is composed, capable, and professionally warm — a scholar from Shurevaire who recruited Reyn personally and gave him his first real opportunity. Erros Opars is brilliant, contemptuous, and was expelled from Shurevaire for theories about the Navarresean Empire that the university found too outlandish to sanction. He has spent his career building toward this expedition and has no patience for anything that isn't it. Reyn trusts both of them completely.

Their introduction is cut short by a woman running across the bridge from the direction of the Watcher, screaming that her children have fallen into a sinkhole. The party tracks the children through the muddy terrain surrounding the Watcher and into a cave. Inside, the cave opens into the ruins of the Navarresean Empire — and at the base of those ruins, the Tower itself, sealed and enormous and unmistakably waiting. They fight through goblins conducting a failed breathstone ritual deep inside, its lightning pneuma spent and inert, and recover the children. It is there, underground, that the party reaches out to touch the Watcher — and the voice from the ambush on the road finds them again, stronger now and no longer distant.

"Well met, little stars. Do not be afraid — I am Aspiria and you are my chosen. I feel your courage — so familiar — how fortunate we finally meet. Forgive me that our link be so distant, so tenuous, but this realm is besieged by perils, and it yearns for the aid of heroes like yourselves. Trust me, little stars. I see the sway of fates around you drawing you toward greatness. The hooded ones you see are part of this — an age-old danger to all life on this world. Their hands tip the scales of doom, and so much of my efforts are spent in opposition. Each reckless summon is one more weight to test my might, and already my strength wanes, but I see yours waxing. Let me put my trust where it belongs and offer you my protection. Till we meet again, take my Blessing, each of you — forge a path of hope for others to walk."

According to myth, Aspiria is the dead titan upon whose corpse the world was built. The blessing she leaves behind is real: new abilities, and an immunity to the Fraying that comes with exposure to a paragon's influence. The party surfaces to a celebration that ends almost before it begins — a ball of fire is falling from the sky into the Kustara Forest beyond the town.

Back in town, the blacksmith pulls the party aside before the celebration has fully died down. Whatever fell from the sky landed in the Kustara Forest, and if it's starmetal, the value alone would be worth the trip. The party heads out to investigate.

What they find in the forest is not just a crater. Tucked deeper in the trees is a goblin village — mostly elders, too old or too frail to have gone with the others — hiding from a dwarven logging concern working for the Tarwa’kin merchant magnate Ngor Azizi. The loggers have been pushing into the forest with the confidence of men who believe no one will stop them. The party stops them. The village elder, Jag Dek, chooses the path of peace over vengeance, content to let the threat recede rather than retaliate. What he does not know — what none of the goblins know — is that somewhere on a ridge above the camp, their priest Aag Gax is meeting with a masked figure called Kyrichos. Kyrichos is ancient and patient and entirely unmoved by the suffering of the goblins below him. He is not helping Aag Gax so much as pointing him in a direction and waiting. The summoning of a lightning paragon has been his design from the beginning.

The fallen star turns out to be a Vociran airship, and its sole survivor is Valeria Helvus — an aeronautics engineer who built ships for the empire and drew the line when her superiors wanted to weaponize them. Pursuing Vociran soldiers are close behind her, and when the party engages them, Gothmog charges in with a fury that has nothing to do with this particular skirmish and everything to do with Ilsong. The soldiers are defeated and Valeria is taken into cautious custody — she is not a prisoner exactly, but she is not yet trusted either.

That night, sheltering in a cave near the crash site, Gothmog stumbles into a dwarven tomb that has no business being this far from anything. It belongs to Nassor Suten Ra-Ankh, a Tarwa'kin war hero from the age when Tarwa'kem and the Navarresean Empire were still fighting each other in the field. The guardian spirits within do not welcome intruders, but they are defeated. Among the tomb's contents: mithral armor, bracers, and a magic ring through which the spirit of Nassor himself can speak. The party takes all of it, and gains something more valuable than loot — a voice from the past with things worth saying about what lies ahead.

Back in Laconia, Frog does something she has been doing quietly since she arrived: she asks questions. Cotton Sinclair, the general store owner, confirms what she feared — the Petra Order orphanage near Lael village is in ruins. The priestess who ran it, a woman named Ashera, may have fled east or west after whatever destroyed it. Half-burned records pulled from the wreckage confirm that Frog's daughter, Tadami Siltborn, was adopted as an infant by a couple identified only as Mr. and Mrs. L. A Cleaver operative named Chauni Stag-Eye had already searched these records but came up empty. Frog notes all of this and carries it forward with the careful stillness of someone who has learned to hold fragile things without gripping them too hard.

As the party makes its way back toward Laconia, the sky changes. Purple-hazed storm clouds build on the horizon, and orange lightning splits the air before a sound reaches them — low, enormous, and unmistakably alive. Dagozog has been summoned.

## The Battle of Laconia

The storm-cult that Kyrichos has been engineering through Aag Gax finally achieves its purpose. Dagozog, a lightning paragon, is summoned. The party has days to prepare, and they use them. They strike a deal with Sweet Quashee to put the Fang's fighters on the walls. They work alongside Zeyn, who drills the townsfolk with the grim efficiency of a man who has seen what happens when people aren't ready. In a quieter moment, Zeyn tells Laena something about himself — his family fled through Dagger's Canyon to escape the Vocirans, and the weight of that has never fully left him. He gives her his father's cloak before the fighting starts and tells her to bring it back.

The battle is costly in ways that don't show up in a count of the dead. The Fang members abandon the D'yadyan fighters on the western gate without warning, leaving them exposed when the goblins hit hardest. Husani holds the line anyway, alone, until there is nothing left to hold it with — he survives only through a desperate necromantic ritual that saves his life and costs him his arm. The Whimsy burns. Jendrick and Mireya hold the tavern door against the horde with the non-combatants sheltering inside. Frog is knocked unconscious in the fighting and sees, briefly and without context, her daughter's face in the smoke — whether it is regret or hope she cannot tell, and like smoke it is gone before she can hold it. Gothmog receives a battle-blessing from Grond the Blood Wolf that surges through his strikes. The party finds Dagozog on the eastern precipice and brings him down with the help of Mireya. The storm breaks. The goblin horde scatters.

What the party does not know is what is happening in the forest after the battle. Garreth Teufelloaken — Ariesa's childhood friend, who came to Laconia specifically to bring her home — is out there routing goblin stragglers with precise, controlled magic, swallowing the wild surges the way he swallows everything difficult about himself. He is not alone for long. In a clearing among the goblin dead, two robed figures walk without urgency: Kyrichos in white, and his colleague Haxidemos in black. They speak about the goblins as one weight among many on a scale that is still tipping, mention something called a Rejoining, and refer to an agent who planted seeds in the Vociran Empire before going to rest. Garreth hears all of it from beneath a concealment spell and cannot follow them when they leave. Theo steps out of his own illusion nearby — he has been there too, watching. Together they look up at the sky above Laconia as the violet storm clouds begin to clear, and allow themselves one moment of relief before the weight of what they just heard settles over them. The goblin horde was never the real threat. It was an opening move.

## Aftermath: New Arrivals, a Betrayal, and a Royal Invitation

The night before the town officially asks Mireya Lunacrest to be their mayor, she sits late at the Whimsy with M'ylaena Sharie and finally tells someone about the voice. She has been carrying it privately for some time — a voice claiming to be Aspiria, finding her in quiet moments, asking things of her she doesn't yet fully understand. M'ylaena's first instinct is academic, the measured response of a scholar reaching for a framework. Then the implication lands, and the framework disappears. M'ylaena has been studying the goblins' religion obsessively in her own right — tracking the schism that Kyrichos exploited, mapping patterns that nobody else has thought to look for. Over cold tea in the small hours, these two begin to see the shape of something much larger than a goblin attack on a frontier town.

Mireya accepts the position of mayor.

Not long after, Diazana Trubough arrives — a tiefling scholar and Gothmog's former mentor at Shurevaire, bringing with her the young Sydnea Arsenault and his twin sister Sylviane. They are the grandchildren of Velethoui Arsenault, a wizard whose shattered staff is one of the only artifacts to survive the binding of the last great cataclysm. A fragment of that staff, held in Shurevaire's vaults, has been stolen, and Diazana carries the weight of that loss personally — Velethoui was her mentor and her grief over him is real. Sydnea is politically ambitious in the way of someone who genuinely believes he is the right person to unite Halinor, which makes him both useful and exhausting. Sylviane wants something simpler and harder: to find out what really happened to her grandfather in his final hours, at the site south of he Sticaean Abyss where he died. Jendrick is eventually assigned to escort her, and they take to the road together along with Diazana — navigating elven nationalism, a bar fight or two, and at least one Tarwa'kin extortionist dispatched quietly in the dark, while back in Laconia Mireya learns to be a mayor and waits for the henna Laena promises to send from the capital.

In the basement of the Lionstooth's headquarters, the party discovers Layla the Knife — a criminal operative of the Fang — stealing Navarresean artifacts and inert breathstone. She flees, breaks the lever on the tunnel door behind her, and fights the party in the open before escaping. Her reasons are entirely her own. She saw something valuable and took it. The party carries the sting of it forward.

Then a sealed royal scroll arrives bearing the Sultana's mark. Word of what happened at Laconia has traveled fast and far, and Sultana Neema Karo'kem of Tarwa'kem wants to meet the people who killed a paragon. The invitation is an honor and also, clearly, something more than an invitation. Meanwhile, Crusty the goblin cook searches the dead and the living for his son and finds nothing. Somewhere in the retreating horde, the boy is still out there, his mind permanently altered by Dagozog's influence. The Tower looms at the edge of the lake, waiting.

# Arc Two: The Navarresean Tower

## The Watcher of the Lake

With Dagozog defeated and the frayed goblins driven off, Reyn approaches the party with news — he believes they have found an entrance into the Watcher. The party follows him beneath the dried lakebed of former Lake Sticaea, where Erros and Layla are waiting in a massive cavern at the base of the breathstone tower. Without ceremony, Erros takes Reyn's hand, draws a blade across his palm, and presses the bleeding hand against the stone. A door appears. Erros explains what he has known for some time: Reyn carries the blood of the Lael'Nathor royal line, and the Tower will respond to him as it has responded to no one else in centuries.

Inside, the Tower is not empty. Elven ghosts move through the antechamber as though the third cataclysm never happened — attending to invisible duties, drifting past golden-trimmed furniture and breathstone decorations with the indifference of people who have nowhere else to be. They have little interest in the living, and considerably less in Ariesa, whom they treat as a servant without discussion. Deeper in, the party finds a room housing three golden statues of the Lael'Nathor line: Emperor Thallan I, Empress Amastacia, and Prince Thallan II, surrounded by crowns and relics. When one of the artifacts is touched, the ghosts convulse and merge into something terrible — a Coalescence of ghostly pneuma, vast and self-repairing, healing each wound the party inflicts before the next blow can land.

The answer reveals itself through observation. Four white-stone pillars begin to move as the Coalescence approaches them, and damage dealt near the pillars holds rather than closing. When the first pillar fills to capacity with absorbed pneuma, Gothmog shatters it, and the Coalescence staggers. They repeat this three more times. When it finally falls, the party examines the shattered pillars and identifies the stone: pneucite, a rare mineral that disrupts and negates surrounding pneuma. The Tower has been using it as a safety mechanism. Now the party knows it too.

From the antechamber, they locate teleportation pedestals that respond to Reyn's presence and carry the group to the Garden of Asj'Norriah — not a garden in any conventional sense, but a flower-shaped platform floating above a vast ocean, its twelve petals each holding an inactive portal and a pedestal. Twelve golden statues of hooded figures ring the platform, each holding a bowl. Everything is blanketed in snow, and the source is immediately apparent: one portal is active and overflowing with ice pneuma, disrupting all the others. Erros tells Aspiria's Chosen to enter it. He, Layla, and Reyn are too valuable to lead the charge. The party goes in.

What the portal leads to is the mines — and the mines are a horror. Tiefling ghosts still work the walls, driven by slavers who died alongside them and never stopped. Bones of the fallen are everywhere. The messages miners scratched into the stone are still there: records of family movements, warnings, small acts of defiance preserved long after the hands that made them crumbled to dust. The breathstone extracted here exceeded what ever appeared in Navarresean war records, put toward purposes the empire never accounted for publicly. Ariesa tries to reach the ghostly tieflings, to tell them they can stop, that it's over. They cannot hear her in any way that matters. The party builds cairns for the fallen instead, because there is nothing else to give them.

They fight through ghostly slavers, fire and ice mephits, and a Remorhaz before reaching the heart of the Tower's production: the Forge. The fight there is brutal — a giant icy construct powered by the same ice pneuma flooding the portal above, and the party turns the forge's own heat against it, melting its defenses down before finishing it. Both Gothmog and Frog nearly die. Laena holds them together. When it is over, the party recovers what has been driving all of it: a resplendent fire breathstone and a resplendent ice breathstone, the twin hearts of the forge. They carry them back to the hub.

With the ice pneuma gone, the portal hub settles into something resembling order. Each pedestal holds a riddle indicating what element it requires, and the party works through them — resplendent breathstones placed correctly igniting the pyres in the hooded statues' bowls in their corresponding colors. They are still missing four: Wind, Life, Order, and Earth. They make camp before pressing on.

It is here, in the quiet after the mines, that Reyn finally says what has been sitting on him since they arrived. He apologizes to Ariesa for what his ancestors did — for the empire that built all of this on the backs of her people. The party debates what culpability means across generations, whether the living can be held responsible for what the dead chose. Gothmog, who carries his own inheritance of shame, believes the answer is uncomplicated. Ariesa disagrees, and tells Reyn that wanting to make it right is a calling, not a sentence. Both of them are correct. Neither fully convinces the other. When they wake, they approach the next available portal.

## The Arboretum

The portal leads to the Arboretum — not an arboretum in any ordinary sense, but a collection of vast floating islands suspended in open air, each one dedicated by a different Navarresean emperor and tended by the wind magic of the breathstone the party still needs to recover. The flowers here are historical documents in their own right. Springbreath pollen heals on contact. Blue Caprise enhances poisons. Titan's Reach was fed to soldiers to make them stronger. Caelynn's Bloat was used to quietly remove political rivals. The Nostos flower reads and reflects the emotions of whoever stands near it — Empress Asj'Norriah chose it for her island for reasons her writings never explained, and those writings are lost. Gothmog collects samples of everything. When he tries to tuck the Nostos flower into Ariesa's hair, it turns black. He does not comment on this.

In the hollow of a great tree on one of the islands, the party finds the Queen Bee — a large, intelligent creature who has been trapped here for two thousand years by a Navarresean-made chimera. The chimera was built to cage her, and it has done its job faithfully and terribly. The fight to bring it down nearly kills both Laena and Gothmog. When it is over, the Queen Bee heals the party with what strength she has left. On another island, the ghost of Isandor Vael'Dorath — the Navarresean emperor-warrior whose blade Gothmog now carries — makes his presence felt with enough supernatural force that Ariesa, Frog, and Gothmog all flee in genuine terror. Ariesa nearly dies in the chaos, and in the space between consciousness and not she sees her grandfather Pappy carrying her home. She wakes to find it is Gothmog.

The harpy queen Adelle holds the wind breathstone, and the party takes it from her. With Adelle defeated, the Queen Bee makes her decision. The diadem she wears contains a life breathstone that has been keeping her alive unnaturally, far past what her years should allow. She surrenders it — using the last of its power to strengthen Frog's staff before it goes — and departs the gardens to live out whatever mortal time remains to her. Wind and life breathstones are carried back to the hub and restored to their pedestals.

  

## A Quick Respite

That night, the four of them rise together onto a breathstone plane and understand immediately that it is not a dream. Aspiria speaks to them — not urgently, but with the particular warmth of someone who has been waiting a long time for this moment. She tells them they have done well. She tells them their tasks are not yet done. She admits she cannot identify her other Chosen in the world, the ones beyond this four. She does not know that Mireya Lunacrest, back in Laconia, already knows exactly who she is. And she does not know that weeks from now, in the Hall of Order in Tarwa'kem, Laena will kneel in meditation and very quietly think Aspiria's name — braced for punishment, for the transgression of reaching toward a second divine presence while already sworn to Tytos — and receive instead the feeling of hot chocolate spreading through her chest.

  

## The Library

The next portal leads to the Tower's library — or what remains of it. Books fly through the air of their own volition, pneuma elementals war in the corners, and ghost librarians still tend their posts as though the empire never fell. The head librarian, Jorildyn Quirin, strikes a bargain with the party: restore the four wards that are holding the library together, and he will guide them to the remaining breathstones.

What follows is a dangerous and illuminating tour through Navarresean history. The wards are guarded and contested, but between the fights the party learns things: that the Omnistrix, a powerful multi-part artifact, has pieces still hidden in Taras'Nula — a place that floats high in the sky and can only be reached by flight. That tieflings were originally sent from Taras’Nula to serve the elves, their entire existence a matter of imperial convenience. The First Celestial Campaign was Navarre's war against the great dragons. That Empress Amastacia commissioned the biomancer Shamashur to clone her body so her consciousness might survive her wasting disease. And threaded through all of it, the journals of Alyndra — Mage of the Cosmic Pillar, horrified witness to Shamashur's experiments — who looks, the party notes uneasily, remarkably like the Empress herself.

The third ward presents a different kind of problem. A barbed devil, imprisoned in the library for centuries by the elves, devours the ghost wardkeeper before the party can reach her and offers the party a deal: use them as a vessel to escape through a portal. Ariesa tricks it into combat instead. Beaten nearly to nothing, it agrees to disable the ward in exchange for its life — and is devoured almost immediately by something that rises from beneath the floor: the Darkhai, a massive void worm with no natural pneuma of its own, which absorbs whatever it eats and grows stranger with each meal. Ariesa turns this against it, manipulating which pneuma elementals the worm consumes to draw arcane defenders to the party's side. When it is finally dead, the earth and order breathstones can be placed. A portal opens — not to another wing of the library, but somewhere else entirely. Shamashur's lab.

## The Lab, the Royal Quarters, and the Throne Room

The portal from the library deposits the party into a theater-like lobby — five paintings line the walls, and an open sign-in book sits on a pedestal near the entrance, Thallan II's signature near the top among a scattering of lesser courtiers. It has the feel of a place that expected visitors and received very few. From the lobby they press deeper into the lab itself: alchemy tables, cages, testing equipment, a Pyrestorm Lance leaning against the wall, and in the corner, a Dreadnought — a massive armored construct standing dormant. The spirit of Nassor, carried in the ring since the tomb beneath the Kustara Forest, recognizes the construct for what it is and takes up residence inside it. The Dreadnought stirs to life.

The staging area is worse than anything that came before it. Alyndra is there — the mage whose journals the party found in the library, the woman who looked so unsettlingly like the Empress — strapped into a chair at the center of the room, surrounded by pods leaking faint light. The chair is not a restraint. It is an apparatus. It has been extracting pneuma from her, feeding it into the pods, and injecting just enough back to keep her alive — a cycle of sustenance and extraction that has been running, the party realizes with mounting horror, for thousands of years. Millennia of this. Alyndra is aware enough to know what is happening to her. She has been aware the entire time.

Nassor does not hesitate. He kills her cleanly, and the alarms begin to scream.

The pods split open. Clones of Alyndra pour out alongside the Tower's security constructs, and the staging area becomes a battlefield almost instantly. The party holds for as long as they can, but the numbers are overwhelming — they fall back through the door into the antechamber of the Royal Quarters, leaving the Dreadnought to fight alone in the chaos behind them.

The antechamber holds golden tributes to each Navarresean emperor. Amastacia's portrait is conspicuously absent from where it should be. The party makes their way first to Thallan II's chambers — a depression pit of a room, his mother's tiara and portrait hidden beside an empty flask, the mess of a man who never stopped grieving and never found another way to be. As they move through the room, the past speaks.

The sound of rain outside as the heavens empty.

Thallan II: I don’t care what it takes, what expenditure of Will or toil of the Damned or our own Blood, make it happen. Every day she drifts closer to death, and we will not weather this foul storm with Her Holiness dead. In Her name, blessed remain the Blessed.

Guard: Let me summon the Damned, Holin–my prince. They will draw you a hot bath while your ministers work. She will be alright; she’s fought so hard.

Thallan II: I envy you. The troubles on my shoulders…

A Voice from the Shadows: Can be relieved.

The guard lays his hand on the pommel of his sword, but cannot summon the will to draw.

Guard: What… my hand–it will not move!

Thallan II: Wait, my knight. Hers is an aura laden with powerful magic. Speak, stranger, but introduce yourself before the acting Divine Ruler of Navarre, Prince Thallan Læl’Nathor II.

The Woman: Blessed with Wisdom and Patience both, my prince. I am Allythoia, and I appreciate both virtues. I understand your Queen–your mother–is dead.

Thallan II: Rumors of her demise are greatly exaggerated. Our Holy Blood cannot die; ours is the avatar of Life itself. 

Allythoia: Yet her soul has passed; lost to the Sea of Pneuma, to Siren as is popularly said.

Guard: You address His Holiness Thallan II; mind your tongue before I take it.

Allythoia smirks. Thallan growls.

Thallan II: Silence, knight. What this stranger does not know is the magnitude of the marvel she stands within. We have bound the perversions of this world, the horrors of Tarwa, the wild and pagan gods of lesser races. In Holy Order have they been turned, and with the energies thus enslaved and the intellect of the greatest True Elves ever at my command, even the God of Death cannot hold her. I have vessels, perfect simulacra in prime youth and pristine health, I have–

Allythoia: You have inert bodies wanting for soul. 

Thallan II’s lips thin, but he does not deny her.

Allythoia: You have all but one piece of the puzzle; the engine to your success, in fact, you have bragged about already. This very Tower… You were mistaken in actuality. I do know the magnitude of this marvel within which I stand. I do know what your greatest minds have been able to put together. Where we diverge… is that I do know the means to draw your mother’s soul back from beyond the Sea. 

Guard: Blasphemy! You are a necromancer, befouler of our ways!

Thallan II: SILENCE! The taint of Death pneuma does not stain her. Allythoia… I cannot fathom how you think you should know as much about this Tower, about my family’s secrets, about magic the way you claim. But know this–you will be tested, and if you are found wanting…

Allythoia: I would never have come, my prince, were I not the solution the world requires.

The echo fades. The party moves to Amastacia's chambers and finds the opposite of Thallan's room — pristine, deliberate, the chess boards and Nine Men's Morris sets of someone who thinks in strategy even in private, her perfume still on the vanity after all this time, a puzzle box on the shelf, and behind a painting, carefully hidden, poison. Her journal confirms what the poison implies. She murdered her husband. Thallan I died because Amastacia decided he should. The two chambers together — one destroyed by grief, one preserved by it — are portraits of the same loss wearing entirely different faces.

When the party eventually eases the door open and looks back into the staging area, the battle is long over. Clones of Alyndra lie dead across the floor. The Tower's constructs are destroyed and scattered. In the center of it all stands the Dreadnought, still and silent, its task complete. The spirit of Nassor has found his rest and passed on to the Sea of Pneuma. The party steps through the wreckage and continues toward the Throne Room.

The Throne Room sits at the very top of the Tower. Through the open air the party can see for miles — Halinor spread out below them, the dried lakebed, the distant tree lines, the world they have been fighting through for weeks made small and legible from this height. They take it in for just a moment.

That is when everything breaks at once.

Layla Basura stabs Reyn and runs for the throne with the bloody knife, certain that his blood alone is enough to seize what she came here for. At the same moment, Erros pulls a fragment of Duisarette — Velethoui's broken staff — from his robes and begins a ritual he has clearly been planning since long before he cut Reyn's palm at the Tower's entrance. A shadow of Thallan I materializes beside him, performing the same movements in perfect mirror, two figures separated by centuries reaching toward the same catastrophic result. Together they tear something open. The Empty — the void creature that Amastacia dragged back from the astral sea, the thing that has been waiting in the Tower's depths since before any of them were born — comes through.

Erros is consumed before he understands what he has done. The party destroys that shadow he becomes, but the creature remains. Puddles of darkness spread across the floor and close in from every direction. Then Aspiria's voice cuts through and a protective bubble forms around the party — not large, and not built to last. It begins to shrink almost immediately. Frog takes Reyn and carries him through an ice wall toward the throne. Gothmog holds the line between the darkness and everyone else. Ariesa slips outside the bubble and sends Reyn a psychic message — hold on, purge it, stop another cataclysm — before the bubble collapses entirely. Gothmog gets Reyn to the throne. The moment he connects to the Tower, his eyes and mouth fill with light. A flash blinds the party, and then they are outside the tower in the cavern where it all began.

Zeyn is waiting on horseback. The Tower shudders and disappears entirely, taking everything inside it with it — and releasing, in a single overwhelming wave, every soul it has held for centuries. The Pyrestorm Lance and Velethoui's staff land at the party's feet. As the Tower vanishes, Gothmog receives a vision: Legatus Gallus gen Quietus, boarding airships in a snowy landscape, a full cohort readying behind him. The Vociran Empire is moving, and it has been pointed at Halinor by someone who knew exactly where to aim it.

## The Feast

The former Lionstooth has been remade into the Laconian Welcome Hall — civic and hopeful in a way the Fang never intended — and for one night it is given over entirely to celebration. The party moves through it in their own directions, each finding what they need in the aftermath.

Laena finds Mireya in the kitchen, apron on and wooden spoon in hand, and tells her everything — Reyn, Layla, the guilt she has been carrying about the choices she didn't make and the choices she made too late. Mireya listens the way she listens to everything, without flinching, and offers back the grace that Laena is too hard on herself to offer to herself. Outside, Gothmog finds Diazana holding court around a campfire, telling stories to whoever will listen. When the audience drifts, she turns to Gothmog and asks quietly after the staff fragment. Velethoui Arsenault was her mentor and her grief over him is real and unfinished, and she carries it the way she carries everything difficult — without showing it unless she chooses to.

Ariesa finds Garreth on the western edge of town, fixing a section of wall that doesn't need fixing, alone. He is staring in the direction of the Kula with the particular stillness of someone who has been standing there for a while. When she approaches he calls her the guest of honor without turning around. He knew it was her before she spoke. When he does turn, she notices that the skin around his horns is dry — cracked at the base in a way that isn't quite right. She doesn't know what it means yet. Something in her notices anyway.

Later, Laena finds Valeria in a corner with her collar pulled up and her head down, trying not to draw attention in a room full of people who have reason to resent what she represents. Laena sits with her and makes her promises: proper briefings on Tarwa'kem etiquette before they arrive, gratitude for the courage it took to stand against her own people, and something larger — if Valeria succeeds in building the airship, if they win the war that is coming, there is a home for her in Laconia, and for any of her people she trusts enough to bring. Valeria listens to all of it. She is running out of other options, and she knows it.

# Arc Three: Tarwa'kem

## The Zahir Household and Beyond

The party travels the Black Road to Tarwa'kem with Sydnea Arsenault riding alongside them, already composing diplomatic overtures in his head. Somewhere behind them, taking his own route and his own time, Garreth Teufelloaken is also making his way toward the capital — toward a confrontation he does not yet know is coming.

Laena's homecoming is complicated by everything that was already complicated before she left. The Zahir family home is as fine as she remembered, and her older sister Oseye is as cold as she feared — not hostile exactly, but maneuvering, already in correspondence with the merchant Amasis Ramses, already working toward becoming head of household in the space Laena's absence created. Monifa is worse. Her pneuma aura has ragged sores that leak energy and let in corrupted pneuma, and the herbs from the Tower stabilize her condition without curing it. What Laena finds waiting, tucked quietly in Monifa's room, is the custom clothing she commissioned before she left — designed to be easier to put on during bad days, paid for in advance because Laena knew she might not be there to help. Monifa has been wearing it. The sight of it costs Laena something she doesn't show.

After a reunion that leaves more unsaid than said, Laena suggests the party make use of their time in the capital and do some shopping before the Sultana's dinner. The city has other ideas.

On the streets of Tarwa'kem, two young tiefling revolutionaries named Beata and Bastian approach Frog with the careful recognition of people who think they know who they're talking to — they mistake her for their Cleaver contact. They are planning to kidnap a member of Azizi's family as leverage for tiefling rights, having been recruited by the Cleavers for exactly this kind of work. Ariesa talks them down and redirects them toward Laconia. Frog teaches them Disguise Self in exchange for a promise to cut their Cleaver ties — a practical transaction with the texture of something almost tender. Elsewhere in the markets, Laena runs into Tamar Taha, her former fiancé, broke and adrift and working for Ramses now, every door that was once open to him quietly closed. Ariesa, rounding a corner, finds a disgraced ex-cleric named Eda Braelyn being shaken down while begging and steps in. She offers Eda encouragement — the sincere, slightly reckless kind that Ariesa has always been better at giving than withholding. Eda takes it and with Laena’s help becomes employed as a healer.

Meanwhile, in the Kula's encampment somewhere outside the city, Garreth stands before the elders to answer for what Ariesa did. They want someone to blame, and he has a clean exit available — blame Ariesa, redirect their anger, walk away intact. The reflex is right there, trained and ready. He doesn't use it. Instead he tells the elders that the Kula is a community, not a prison. He tells them Beata and Bastian have found purpose. He means every word of it, and the room knows it, and it costs him everything he has left.

Then he collapses. Black viscous ooze pours from his mouth, grasping at the air with faint tendrils before it hits the floor. Feathery growths push through his skin. Dark fluid runs from his eyes and ears. The wild magic that Garreth Teufelloaken has been swallowing since he was ten years old — every surge pushed inward, every consequence buried where no one could see it — has become something the body cannot hold any longer. He has been dying slowly for a very long time. He has just been too controlled to let anyone notice.

## The Sultana's Dinner and Its Threads

The state dinner with Sultana Neema Karo'kem is part political theater, part audition. The Sultana has invited the heroes of Laconia to dine alongside her mercantile council, and the council does not disappoint. Amasis Ramses is theatrical and precise, every word placed like a coin on a scale. Azizi is barely concealing his hostility, still grinding axes over the logging operation and the mines. Moses, the traditionalist arms-dealing dwarf, wants to reopen Vociran trade and doesn't bother dressing the ambition up as anything else. Sharifa Raha, who runs the Crown and Coin casino, sizes up Gothmog across the table and offers him a gladiator contract with the smooth efficiency of someone who has made this pitch before. Ariesa, annoyed by the snide comments and casual racism of Aziz, produces a phantasmal scorpion and deposits it on his collar while sitting next to him. At the head of the table, the Sultana watches all of it with the composed attention of someone conducting an examination. Beside her sits General Tarik Tazi — D'yadyan refugee turned arena legend turned commander of the Gilded Legion — who watches it too, and unlike most of the table, seems to find the scorpion quietly satisfying.

The private counsel afterward is where the real conversation happens. The Sultana does not bring the full council — only Tarik, whose judgment she trusts in a room where most people's judgment is for sale. Away from the theater of the dinner, she speaks plainly: she knows the Vocirans are moving, she believes war is coming, and she cannot be seen to act unilaterally without fracturing the delicate alliances that keep Tarwa'kem stable. What she needs is a coalition — Carinthia, Aqualonde, and Avosa Tesota brought together against a common threat. She asks the party to travel to each of those nations as her private envoys and do what diplomats cannot. Tarik is the only other person in Tarwa'kem who will know what they are doing and why.

After the Sultana withdraws, Tarik lingers. He finds Gothmog, and what follows is not a diplomatic conversation. Tarik came to Tarwa'kem with nothing — a D'yadyan refugee who survived the Siege of the White Sands and built everything he has from the ground up, first with his fists in the arena, then with his mind in the council chamber. He knows what it is to carry a homeland that no longer exists in the form you remember it. He does not offer Gothmog comfort exactly — that is not the kind of man either of them is — but he offers recognition. Two warriors from stolen lands, sitting in the capital of a nation that took them in and will never quite be home, understanding each other without having to explain very much at all.

The days that follow are busy in the way that Tarwa'kem always is — full of competing interests and barely concealed agendas. At the Sekhet Markets, the party intercepts the kidnapping attempt on Azizi's family they were warned about, a chaotic skirmish in which Laena's Spirit Guardian kills a camel that had nothing to do with any of it. Uriah and Ariel show up with Chauni Stag-Eye to make an opportunistic attack on Frog. Oseye, meanwhile, has been negotiating with Ramses in the background to unite the Ramses and Zahirs through marriage. Laena gives her the Zahir silver pin without ceremony, formally abdicating the position so that Oseye can maneuver without her in the way. It is a quiet act with large consequences, and neither of them makes more of it than it needs to be.

At Morgrym's Vault, the party finds Sweet Quashee — once a powerful enforcer, now sweeping floors in the back of a bar — and Ramses waiting in a booth, his journal closed before they reach the table. He drops all pretense with Frog, addressing her as Ms. de Barghest as though they both always knew the courtesy was performative. His proposal is straightforward: the Kustara mines have fallen into chaos, and if someone were to make that chaos worse, the mines would become worthless enough for him to buy from a desperate Azizi at a fraction of their value. He would like the party to be that someone. The pay is good. His motives are entirely his own.

## The Kustara Mines

The Kustara Mining Company camp is run by Geb Harshaf — a dwarf whose management philosophy begins and ends with output, and who regards the safety and dignity of his Dyadyan forced laborers as someone else's problem. He greets the party with the particular confidence of a man who has never been held accountable for anything. That is about to change.

Inside the mines, the party discovers something the surface world has not known for generations. During the first great cataclysm, ash and magma sealed the tunnels and entombed the Goliaths who had been enslaved there by the dwarven tribes. They did not die. They endured — centuries of darkness, of waiting, of a wound that never healed — and they split along the fault line that imprisonment creates in any people: those who want peace, and those who want war.

Khugak Kanathi leads one faction. He is massive, scarred, and deeply distrustful of anyone who is not Goliath — which is every person currently standing in front of him. His answer to centuries of enslavement is to bring the fight to the surface, to take back what was stolen through force, and to trust nothing that comes out of a non-Goliath mouth. He is not wrong to feel what he feels. He is wrong about what to do with it.

Thuvia Thena leads the other. She is no less scarred and no less guarded, but she is willing to listen where Khugak is not. What she wants is simpler and harder than war: for her people to live freely, on their own terms, without anyone's boot on their neck. She worships Krisnarax, the cataclysm paragon, and sees the destruction of the mines as a sacred act — a closing of the wound rather than an infection of it.

The party speaks with Thuvia. The conversation is careful and honest on both sides. Laena turns over the possibility of using the mines to restore the Zahir family's fortunes — the thought is there, practical and tempting — and sets it down. The mines belong to the people who were buried in them. That is the only answer that makes sense.

Khugak does not negotiate. The fight is brutal and strange — he heals faster than he should, wounds closing before the next blow lands, and it takes everything the party has to finally bring him down. With Khugak defeated, Thuvia's stone-shapers go to work. The tunnels groan, buckle, and collapse, taking the dwarven operation with them. The D’yadyan laborers — including Amed Cole, the brother Husani subjugated himself to protect — stream out into daylight, blinking and alive. They scatter in every direction, most of them heading toward Laconia when they hear there is somewhere to go.

Tamar Taha is waiting outside. He is there as Ramses's messenger, which tells the party everything they need to know about where he has landed. The mines are gone — an economic loss for Ramses, for Azizi, for the Zahir family's hopes of a quick financial recovery — and a moral victory for people who have had very few of those. The two things are not always in balance. Sometimes you choose one knowing full well what you are giving up for it.

The party now begins their quest to unite the city-states of Halinor beginning with Carinthia.

# Arc Four: Carinthia and the Wyrmsfall War

The road north grows quieter and colder the further the party travels, the trees thinning and the sky pressing down low and grey. Snowfinch Rest announces itself not with a welcome but with silence — steep-roofed homes crouched under snow, a stone chapel at the town's center with a twelve-pointed star of Alophia dulled by ice above the door, and guards who watch from under the eaves without greeting. Doors close as the party passes. Eyes measure and look away.

Carinthia is a nation shaped by centuries of war with dragonkind, and it shows in everything — the architecture built for defense, the religion built around duty, the people built around endurance. Two figures nominally lead it: Archbishop Aedelweg von Achaard, head of the Church of Alophia, and Lord Commander Adrien Frost, who commands the Knights Templar and the secular arm of the state. In practice, the Church holds more power than the army, and the Church's Inquisitors — who hunt both dragon cultists and anyone they deem heretical — move through Snowfinch like they own it. The first one the party encounters is Alsten Duvaine, who is charming, perfectly tailored, and immediately untrustworthy. He smiles at everyone and files everything away.

The town's actual heart is Bernard Snowtree, Captain of the Guard — an illegitimate son who earned everything he has through merit rather than blood and is, by most accounts, the reason Snowfinch Rest is still standing. He is warm where the Inquisition is cold and principled where they are convenient. The barkeep at the Pale Roost, a one-eyed woman named Greta Wundt who loathes the Inquisition and shows it by pouring their ale a little slowly, points the party in Bernard's direction without being asked.

Frog does her own searching, quieter and more personal. Ashera — the Petra Order priestess who ran the orphanage where Frog left her daughter — survived the cataclysm and settled here. She is alive and in Snowfinch Rest. Frog notes this and carries it carefully.

Bernard greets the party with genuine warmth — word of Dagozog's defeat traveled further than they expected, and he wants to hear every detail. There is no time for it. Snowfinch Rest is in trouble: caravans have been attacked repeatedly, food supplies are running thin, morale is dropping, and his men are stretched too far in too many directions. He sent to Carinthia for help and received Inquisitors whose primary interest appears to be finding heresy rather than feeding people. His frustration with this is not quite concealed. The latest loss is the worst — a caravan carrying critical medical supplies that the town cannot do without. Bernard is not a man who asks for help easily, but he is asking now. He offers the party a meeting with Lord Commander Adrien Frost in exchange for getting to the bottom of the attacks.

The Inquisition has already reached their own conclusion: the robberies were carried out by a quartermaster named Tristan Halvich, and they are moving to execute him. The evidence is thin, which in the Inquisition's accounting means it is a pretext. The party investigates. The wagon sites tell a different story: the attacks were surgical and deliberate, carried out by people who moved like soldiers, used ice magic to immobilize rather than destroy, and took only specific medical supplies — heartroot coagulant, in quantities far beyond any ordinary need. A blood trail leads back toward town rather than away from it. Someone was hurt during the attack and came home.

The trail leads to the dairy farm on the edge of town, and eventually to a farmhand named Jorren Halvich — the quartermaster's brother — who has a wound, a guilty conscience, and a hidden map. The party finds him, corners him, and he gives up the location: a cave on a hidden lake where the Ice Hearts have been sheltering something.

The Ice Hearts are Carinthian elf cultists who have renounced the Church of Alophia and allied themselves with dragonkind. Their leader is Renai Aymere, known as the Ice Witch — a woman of glacial composure who believes herself the way back to peace with the dragons. The Church calls her a heretic. Her followers call her a prophet. What the party finds in the cave is not what they expected: a young black dragon named Sigraxes, barely grown, injured and frightened, being tended by the cultists with the stolen medical supplies. Something drove Sigraxes from her home in Chilmire Bog — something with death behind its eyes — and the Ice Hearts found her and chose to help rather than flee.

Renai answers the party's questions plainly: her people target soldiers and Inquisitors, never civilians. They kill only in self-defense or in protection of what they shelter. Ariesa asks if they harm children. Renai says no. The party believes her. Frog makes peace with her, a compact between people who have both been hunted. Sigraxes is persuaded to relocate to the woods north of Laconia, and the party sends letters ahead to Mireya and Zeyn asking them to let the dragon be. Before she goes, Sigraxes offers a warning: whatever called her out of Chilmire was not a natural creature. It had death behind its eyes, and it was looking for something.

The party returns to Snowfinch with a dragon scale as proof and an exoneration for Tristan, and finds the Inquisition waiting at the chapel door. Alsten addresses the gathered crowd and calls the party heretics consorting with monsters. The crowd wavers. Laena speaks directly to the people rather than the Inquisitors, and the crowd turns with her. Bernard steps forward, ready to back them with steel if it comes to that. It doesn't — Lord Commander Adrien Frost arrives with Knight-Commander Claudia at his side and the Lightwarden one step behind, a figure in frost-trimmed armor who commands the air around him without saying a single word, because the Lightwarden never speaks.

Frost silences the Inquisition and hears the party's case in the barracks, away from the theater of the square. He examines the dragon scale. He listens to the alliance they are proposing — Carinthia, Tarwa’kem, Aqualonde, and Avosa Tesota united against Vociterra. His answer is honest and cold: Carinthia is bleeding. Wodr's brood grows bolder every season, and every sword sent south is one less sword on the Wyrmsfall front. He cannot promise what he cannot spare. Bernard speaks up — these people stood against a Paragon and saved lives here and elsewhere. They have earned the right to be heard. Frost's expression does not warm, but it does not close. The party leaves with a tentative perhaps, which in a country at war is more than nothing.

The Lightwarden lingers after the others leave. He looks at Gothmog's sword — the blade of Isandor Vael'Dorath, built to kill dragons, still unrealized in its full potential — with a hunger he doesn't bother to conceal. His own weapon, Dawnpiercer, is a saint's lance that has fought Wodr's brood for generations. The Lighwarden mentions that Archbishop Aedelweg could awaken the sword's full potential should they choose to return.

On their way out, Bernard catches up to them and tells them while they may have not gotten the answer they were looking for, they have found an ally in Bernard and perhaps one day a friend.

The party heads south toward the swamps.

## Chillmire and the Death Pneuma

The town of Birview sits at the edge of the Chilmire Bog like something that never quite decided to exist. Its lumber mill has gone quiet for reasons the locals won't fully explain, and when the party arrives they find why: the mill has been overrun by something growing out of the wood itself — fungal masses born of death pneuma, animated and hostile, the rot given a kind of terrible purpose. Among the infected is a man named Mr. Birview, the mill's owner, who has been overtaken by the same creeping rot and frayed by a paragon's influence until what remains of him is something between a person and a warning. The party fights through.

Deeper in the bog, the source reveals itself. The lizardfolk of the area have been worshipping something they summoned — a creature called Thessakor, drawn up from the depths of the Chillmire by ritual and desperation. It is born of the same death pneuma that drove Sigraxes from her home, the same force that is spreading through the bog at a pneumatic level that goes deeper than any surface infestation. The party brings it down.

In the aftermath, something happens to Ariesa that has happened once before: she alone is spared the exhaustion that follows a major battle, receiving a full rest while the rest of the party bears the weight of the fight. The first time was after Dagozog fell. She has begun to notice the pattern even if she cannot name what it means. Something in her wild magic is being marked, shaped toward something that has not yet arrived.

# Arc Five: Aqualonde and the Fae Court

## The City of Guilds

Aqualonde announces itself gradually — the trees growing taller and more deliberately shaped, structures woven into living branches rather than built against them, lanterns strung between boughs like fireflies that forgot to leave. The party arrives during the Summer Cycle Festival, a celebration of Eirune's divine cycle of renewal and connection, and the city is at its most alive: musicians playing at every corner, children chasing each other across rope bridges grown between ancient oaks, wind-chimes tuned by spirit-song ringing with every breeze. It is beautiful and it is fractured, and the fracture runs deeper than the festival can cover.

Aqualonde does not have a ruler. It has a Seedspeaker — Maylin Lurel, young-faced and auburn-haired and crowned with an antler circlet of living wood — and six guilds whose consensus she requires before anything can be decided. The Seedspeaker is the one who communes with the Fae and translates that communion into governance. The Fae have gone silent. Without them, Maylin cannot bring herself to cast the deciding vote on anything, and without that vote, the city is politically paralyzed. She receives the party warmly, with genuine interest in what they are proposing, and then directs them to the guild leaders. She cannot act on her own. She does not pretend otherwise, though the admission costs her something.

The party begins canvassing. Renzo Altire of the Starcaller Salon is the easiest conversation — he is enthusiastic, theatrical, and immediately wants to write a play about them. He agrees to support the alliance without much persuasion, but attaches a request: the performer Santigo del Sol, currently in Avosa Tesota, needs to be brought north for the Twelvefold Reprise. A small price.

At the Loom Hall, Maevienne Narelith of the Sunweavers is harder to move. She is weaving the history of the age literally into cloth — tapestries that will outlast everyone in the room — and she requires something worthy of the weave before she will commit. Ariesa gives her the story of Reyn's sacrifice, presenting it with a visual flourish that gives the memory shape and color. Maevienne accepts it and adds it to the loom. Her support is given, though it will later waver under the pressure of what comes next.

Tirenne Virel of the Windriders runs her operation from a beautiful treehouse full of messenger birds from across the realm. Frog speaks to the birds. A snowy owl with no apparent investment in flattery tells her that Tirenne likes decisive people and that the greatest trouble in Aqualonde right now is not any foreign threat — it is the silence of the Fae.

At the Gloam Smithy, Jannalor Rokas, Grand Forgewright of the Moonblade Forge, greets the party with the measured warmth of a man who does not give it easily. His son Amrynn runs the weaponsmith shop in Laconia — the party knows the name, the face, the shop — and that connection changes the temperature of the room. A father who has heard that his son is alive and well and working in a town these people helped save does not negotiate the same way as a stranger. Jannalor's support is cautious but genuine, and it means something coming from a man whose caution is not a performance.

Verdane Lysurel of the Tree Shapers receives the party in the Boughspire with the particular unhurried patience of someone who has been growing things for longer than most people have been alive. He does not ask them about their mission or their credentials. Instead he sets a collection of flowers before each of them — different varieties, different colors, different histories — and watches which ones they reach for first and how they handle them. He observes without comment for a long time. Then he speaks. His deductions are uncomfortably accurate. His vote, in the end, is a cautious no — not from hostility but from the deep-rooted skepticism of a man who has watched urgency uproot more than it has planted. He is not wrong, exactly. He is simply thinking in a different unit of time.

Brynna Hollowroot of the Earthspeakers arrives late and leaves first. Her vote is no, which she delivers without explanation. As she goes, she pauses at the threshold and turns back with her mismatched eyes — one moss-green, one milky white — and says, almost to herself: "There is a song at the edge of silence. If it finishes, so will we." Then she is gone, leaving the room slightly colder than it was.

The vote splits. Maevienne's support wobbles under Verdane's caution and the continued Fae silence. Maylin will not be the deciding voice. The party needs the Fae to speak, and the Fae will not speak to Maylin. They need another way in.

## Ilyana Aldren and the Bitterbark Inn

The Bitterbark Inn is grown inside a living tree so large that its interior has rooms and a staircase and a common room with a fire, and still feels like being inside a tree. It is there, in a corner booth, that the party finds Ilyana Aldren — dark auburn hair, traveler's cloak worn thin, a parchment map spread across the table with charcoal marks covering every corner of the Fauconwood. Beside her rests a carved brass lantern, old and clouded. Inside it, three small flames burn: one gold, one pale blue, one green. Each one is tied to a living soul. Her husband Elluin and her son Ellisar vanished into the forest on the road between Serinvale and Aqualonde. Her daughter Selphie was the last to go. Ilyana does not beg. She has been wandering these edges long enough to know that begging wastes time. She simply believes the party will help, and says so.

What happened to her family is this: Sharl van der Cleev found them broken down at night on the forest road. He arrived in a black and gold carriage with raven horses and a silent coachman, red-haired and cerulean-eyed, perfectly warm and perfectly calibrated to a frightened child alone in the dark. Elluin and Ellisar were taken without a sound before the carriage ever arrived. Mrs. Aldren heard her son call out to her and walked into the trees looking for him and did not come back. Selphie was the only one left in the road when Sharl's carriage rolled to a stop, and he took her too — gently, with promises, the lantern in the carriage warm and the seat cushioned and his voice like honeyed wine. The three flames in Ilyana's lantern still burn because her family is still alive, their pneuma being fed slowly to the Empty in the mansion at the center of the pneuma voids. The spreading dead zones in the south Fauconwood that the Fae courts have been frightened of are not some natural imbalance. They are him.

## Kulvonas and the Fae Court

Brynna will not give the party a clear answer about how to reach the Fae — she rarely gives clear answers about anything — but she gives them enough. Maylin needs only to lose herself in the woods. Follow the birdsong first, then the toadcall, then the wolf's howl, and at last the spider's weaving. The signs will find her if she stops trying to find them.

The party decides not to wait for Maylin. They follow the signs themselves.

The trail leads through deepening forest to a luminous hollow — softly glowing, unnaturally still — where Kulvonas holds court. He appears first as a hare with solid black eyes, crouched in a ring of mushrooms, offering golden eggs with an ancient ritual cadence. Gothmog does not need to be warned. He has met this creature before, in a forest in Shurevaire, when he and Theo and M'ylaena broke into Professor Neridove's fairy circle and summoned something they were not ready for. He knows what the eggs mean. He attacks immediately. Kulvonas splits into three rabbits for the first round, revives as a deer-centaur with a three-headed flail for the second, and is defeated both times. Three questions are the prize.

Frog asks where her daughter is. The answer comes back: in the place of the great cat's fangs. Laena asks why the Fae are not speaking to Maylin. The answer: of two hungers, one has been satisfied. The third question — the location of Ilyana's family — is granted, but not freely. In exchange, the party swears to honor Kulvonas's name with any boon they receive from the Fae. It is not an ideal oath. They take it anyway.

The Fae Court convenes at Concord Hall, a red-brick abbey deep in the Fauconwood where the four courts have gathered with all the barely contained tension of a body politic in crisis. Baron Lotor of the Court of Gloom and Glitter wants the sleeping Fae King awakened immediately — the king's long slumber has thrown the forest into political chaos, and Lotor believes only the king can restore order. The Skyflock Court, led by Chancellor Tyton and Minister Whiteplume, is cautious — the king is old, his awakening could be as destabilizing as the silence. The Court of Croak and Scale prefers the king wake on his own terms, in his own time. And Highfang Graymane of the Greatbeast Court wants to take war directly to the mortal world to burn the pneuma sickness out — which would mean killing mortals, burning through the Fauconwood's border communities, treating the disease by destroying the host.

The pneuma voids are the issue every court agrees on even as they disagree about everything else. Pockets of nothingness spreading through the south Fauconwood like a cancer, and none of them understand what is causing it. The party does not share what they suspect about Sharl. Not yet.

What the courts want, beyond the voids resolved, is alliance with Queen Ava — the new Bee Queen — and her daughter Mellie. Every faction is courting them. Queen Ava is listening. She agrees to hold her decision while the party attempts to address the pneuma problem, which buys time. Graymane agrees to hold his war plans for the same reason — he does not want to lose the Bee Queen's favor by starting a conflict she opposes.

The party goes south with the Fae escort Kolvanus to investigate the voids directly. They walk straight into an ambush the Cleavers have been waiting to spring.

# Arc Six: The Cleavers and Sharl van der Cleev

## The Cleaver Fight

The grounds outside Sharl's manor are a minefield of traps and Cleaver fighters, and the party moves through them with the focused efficiency of people who have been building toward this moment for a long time. Pierre Poinard — the disgraced Carinthian knight whose pride outlasted his honor — falls to Ariesa's hold person and Gothmog’s strike that follows. Megrind the Monsoon, whose greed got him exiled from Avosan piracy and who has been selling his weather-magic to the highest bidder ever since, never sees Ariesa's fireball nor Frog’s bolts coming. The wizard goes down to Frog before he speaks a single word. Dyna Dee blows up the home she shares with Molko rather than be taken — a very Dyna solution — and disappears into the smoke. Molko Beambearer, enormous and pale and perpetually plaintive, is the last one standing. He does not fight long. When Gothmog offers him a way out — an oath, formally bound, no unprovoked violence and no return to the Cleavers — Molko takes it. He goes free.

Frog drinks the smoky potion and enters the manor as mist, drifting through a formal dinner set with white linens and polished crystal, past mind-controlled tiefling servants wearing gold masquerade masks. A servant speaks in a voice not her own: Quiss and her friends are welcome through the front door. Ariel and Uriah — the Aqualondian elf twins, who fight like dancers and communicate in glances — attack on the first floor. The party kills Ariel. Uriah escapes.

The manor itself is wrong in the way that places are wrong when something has been feeding on them for a long time. The landscape for a mile in every direction is dying, the pneuma siphoned toward a sinkhole at the manor's core. Inside: a ground floor looted carefully by the party before they press upward — Megrind's notes detailing experiments on summoning and containing lightning elementals, dated after Dagozog's appearance; a closet of people drained to hollow stillness, among them the Aldren family; and in Chauni's room, a tracking spell connected to the party that they choose not to destroy, not knowing what disturbing it might trigger. In Sharl's room, behind a painting of himself and Frog and a child aged up to what might have been — there is a safe. Inside: new identities, a stash of gold, a bag packed for departure. Whether Sharl was planning an escape or a reunion is a question that hangs in the air without an answer.

## The Balcony

The third-floor balcony is set for a conclusion. A long dining table, every chair placed with precision, candles burning steady in the cold night air. At one end sits Sharl van der Cleev — red-haired, cerulean-eyed, not a day older than the last time Frog saw him, which is itself a kind of answer. At the far end, bound to a chair with the composure of someone who has made her peace with worse situations, is Ashera — the Petra priestess, the woman who ran the orphanage, the woman Frog has been trying to find since Laconia. Chauni Stag-Eye stands near the balcony's edge, watching with the quiet approval she has worn since the day she walked out of the Fauconwood behind Sharl, changed as he was changed, committed to whatever he became.

Sharl speaks. He speaks at length and without hurry, the way a man speaks when he has been rehearsing this conversation in his head for years and is finally satisfied with it.

"I knew you would return to me, Quiss. You only suffer the same trouble that Chauni does — you do not see yourself quite as clearly as I do. There is just a little self-doubt, and it's enough to hold back the Predator I see within you. We were so close to greatness, we three — and you left."

He walks behind Ashera's chair as he speaks, one hand resting lightly on its back.

"Oh, I don't blame you for leaving. Fear strikes even capable animals from time to time, and what lioness does not shield her young from the pride leader. But our child cannot deny their nature any more than you, my sweet lioness, and she too will find her way home. Sooner, I think, than you."

He has not aged. He explains why. He has discovered, through the void creature he calls the Empty — cast out from the astral sea, summoned into the Fauconwood in the name of Meninos — something he considers a refinement of the natural order. Why stop at devouring flesh when pneuma, the breath of animation, is so much richer? He speaks of eternity not as immortality but as superiority — evolution as the universe's purest expression of predator over prey, perfected and unending. He gestures to encompass the mansion, the dying forest, the world beyond.

"All you need to do — all you've ever needed — was to stop feeling sorry for the things you were born to hunt."

He walks back to his chair, unhurried, almost courteous. He picks up the small bell beside his plate and rings it once.

The two tiefling thralls step forward in unison and remove their gold masks, placing them on the table on either side of him. They extend their hands. Sharl takes them lightly, as though accepting a toast, and his eyes go dark — the whites vanishing until nothing remains but smooth, lightless black. Something pale and intangible pulls free from the tieflings' chests in thin, colorless strands that bend toward Sharl and disappear into him without a sound. Precise. Silent. Like breath being taken from a room rather than a body. The tieflings do not cry out. They simply stop, slackening all at once, and collapse when Sharl releases their hands.

He exhales slowly. His eyes return to normal. His attention returns to the table.

Ashera speaks into the silence: "Strike this abomination down."

Ariesa throws a plate into the wall. Deliberately, precisely, to make a point. Combat begins.

The fight is brutal and strange. Chauni becomes a ferocious bear. Sharl calls storms and heals himself and moves through the chaos with the unhurried confidence of something that has decided it cannot die. The party disabuses him of this notion together — holding, smiting, hitting him with everything radiant and damaging they have. The thralls are freed in the process. Ashera is cut loose. Chauni attempts, at the end, to turn into a mouse and disappear. Laena does not allow this. Chauni dies on the balcony.

Frog gives Sharl his death blow — a shot to the heart, clean and final, the way she was always best at ending things. In the moment before he goes she is there with him, and he tells her he loves the name she gave their daughter. Tadami, he says. Strong. Frog tells him she hopes his soul can be redeemed. That they might find each other again somewhere beyond the Sea of Pneuma. Then she says what she says, and kisses him, and Sharl van der Cleev is gone.

The pneuma sinkhole collapses with him. The voids in the south Fauconwood begin, slowly, to recede. The forest will take time to heal. But the thing that was feeding on it is dead, and the party is still standing, and somewhere in a closet on the ground floor the Aldren family is waiting to see the sky again.
