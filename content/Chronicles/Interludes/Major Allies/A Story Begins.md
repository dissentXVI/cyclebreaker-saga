# A Story Begins

*A young half-elf named Reyn, prone to daydreaming about heroes, boards a boat from Shurevaire on his first scholarly expedition abroad under the eccentric Professor Opars — with the world at his feet and his head still in the clouds.*

---

A loud clang echoed through the stormy night air as the hill giant’s club crashed into the Paladin’s golden shield.

“Where is he?!” the dwarf shouted, straining to hold the massive wooden club from crushing him.

“I don’t know! He should be here by now!” the woman responded while firing a volley of magic missiles at the giant to no effect.

Just then, a dire wolf leapt from the shadows and sank its sharp teeth into the back of the giant’s leg. The giant wailed out in pain and flung the wolf into a nearby boulder. The wolf morphed back into an elven druid who then shouted, “Well, by the gods, he better hurry!”

In that moment, as if he heard the pleas from the adventurers, a cloaked man atop a steed raced towards the great giant. Lightning flashed, and thunder crashed. Some onlookers claimed the thunder came from the hero’s horse as its gallop met the earth.

Without fear or delay, the hero placed his feet on the saddle of his horse and, in one fluid motion, jumped into a backflip. With a flourish, he fired a trio of arrows into the giant’s underarm. The giant recoiled in pain, alleviating the pressure from the dwarf. He turned towards the hooded man and smiled with a snicker. The hooded man let down his cowl just as a flash of lightning brightened the sky.

“It’s about time, Reyn!” the woman shouted, “Are you ready?”

The half-elf boy gave a stoic nod before setting off in a sprint towards his paladin companion. With a hop, Reyn landed on the dwarf’s shield and, with all his holy might, the paladin launched Reyn into the air where he was caught by a giant eagle. Soaring over the hill giant, the eagle dropped the boy before landing and reverting back to its elven form.

Reyn grasped at the creature’s hair, making sure he had a firm grip before climbing to the top of its head. The half-elf held strong with one hand as he raised his sword in the other. The wizard muttered a spell, and with a wave of her hand, Reyn’s sword was ignited in magical fire. Without hesitation, Reyn plunged his sword into the giant’s head, killing it. He gracefully dismounted the falling creature and made his way back to his team as the storm subsided.

After a moment of camaraderie, the group approached the frightened villagers to assure them the danger had passed. Relieved, the villagers surrounded the group of brave warriors and began thanking them. Then one after another, they began chanting his name, “Reyn! Reyn! Reyn!”. The chant grew louder and louder until *SNAP!*

Layla snapped her fingers in front of Reyn’s face to jolt him out of his imagination.

“Listen up and grab your things. The boat is about to leave,” she said, beginning to pick up his books he had scattered around him. The port had been so quiet since Shurevaire closed its borders. No one knew why, and it was best not to ask. It was no matter to Reyn; he had his first assignment abroad, and his excitement led him to sit at the dock all day, looking forward to setting sail. He began packing his books away as Layla handed them to him.

“You were daydreaming again, weren’t you? I swear Reyn, if you spent half as much time studying magicks as you do reading this nonsense, then you’d be a wizard rivaling even the great Velethoui,” she said, glancing at the titles of his books. *The Tale of Y’Set’s Vanquishers,* *The War of Enchanted Anvils: A Soldier’s Journal,* and* Legends and Lore of Aspiria* were among the collection of spine-worn tomes the young man–boy, really–had brought with him.

Reyn smirked downward, avoiding eye contact, “Ha, yeah. I mean, I know this is an educational trip, but…”

“But nothing. That’s exactly what this is, nothing more. If I were you, I’d keep your head out of the clouds and focus on where we’re going. I vouched for you because I know how passionate you are about history. No matter how fictitious it is,” her words dripped with contempt as she handed him his last book, *Pre-Navarresean Cataclysms: Gods Born of Tragedy.*

“Of course. I am…I mean, I will. I mean,” he sighed in frustration as the words failed to leave his mouth, “What I mean to say is, you can count on me. I promise you won’t regret giving me this opportunity.”

“That’s good to hear,” Layla said with encouragement, “Because as you know, Professor Opars is…eccentric. Hence the reason this isn’t officially sanctioned by the University. But his knowledge and theories on Navaressean civilization far surpass anyone on this island so we are going to follow his lead–and with his good favor,” she notes with a glance as she speaks, “You can get your scholarship duties confirmed and join the University properly..”

“Yes, ma’am.” Reyn picked up his things and nodded.

Reyn and Layla boarded the ship and quickly realized how vacant it was. Reyn was looking forward to swapping tales with some Collettore about their forays into the broader world on the hunt for rare samples. No matter, a quiet boat and the fresh sea air still made for a great first step towards the excitement that assuredly awaited them; he needed to practice his sea-legs after all, in case Avosan pirates set upon them as they had Velethoui himself in the copper-talebook he picked up on the way over. The boat pushed off and began sailing open seas towards Elesatha.

Layla was busy writing in her journal, documenting the first stages of their trip. Reyn took this opportunity to take a step up onto the main deck and appreciate the salt and sea wafting to his nose, the unrestrained rush of wind around him. Each cresting wave was new whites and greens reflecting the glimmer of sunlight, dulled by water or here and there dazzling off a fish’s scales. He quieted his mind and took a deep breath, stirring whatever artistic instinct he could for the canvas of memory.

* * *

The weight of his bags proved to be too much for his slight shoulders. *Twelve, save me. Why did I pack those books?* The tumble of one from his grip caused him to dawdle again on the docks, this time in an unfortunate place. 

“Is that the professor? He looks mad,” Reyn asked in a hush from his kneel, looking up to a grumpy old man stalking with evident annoyance toward them.

“He just looks like that,” Layla replied quickly as she extended her hand down towards the old man and in a much brighter–and louder–voice greets him, “Professor Opars! It’s so exciting to finally be here. I worried this day would never come.”

The old man raised his brow and pushed up his spectacles, “Only through another’s failure, Layla; never doubt mine own will.” His chastisement stung twice, since she was the only ‘another’ he had. He prattled on, sidestepping her to approach Reyn. “You must be Layla’s prize. Let’s have a look at you.”

Reyn’s heart lodged in his throat.

“She tells me she sees great potential in you,” Opars stared into Reyn’s eyes. One at a time, then both. Reyn hoped his vision was motion-based.

“Yes.” The abrupt break in silence startled Reyn, “I see it too. Well Reyn, you may not be a complete waste after all!” Opars turned his back and began walking towards land, hands clasped behind his back as he continued, “You will learn very quickly that this is not a field trip, and you will come to realize that I am not one of your professors–as you are not a student, of course, and therefore the rules are mine own.”

“Rule the First: I will not repeat myself. Rule the Second: This is not a treasure hunt and this is not an adventure. Layla tells me you enjoy history, mostly that of *heroes*, cavorting about like vagabonds and making off with whatever’s not nailed down,” he sneers. “Do not mistake this trip as a bard’s vulgar song or a sailor’s drunken tale. THIS is an expedition for truth. Academic, you know.  One that I’ve been planning for a very long time and I do not want some child enchanted by exaggerated, vainglorious, and meritless tales to spoil it. Rule the Third: I am not a babysitter. I will not hold your hand, nor track your every move. I will not repeat myself, so if I have a task for you, I expect it to be done correctly the first time.” 

He turns about face, “So if you can abide by these rules, do as you are told, and follow directions to the jot and tittle, then I expect this expedition to glean great results - results that will restructure the very foundation of pneuma and pneuma-craft that we know.”

Layla nodded her head in total deference to the old man and with the professor began making their way towards the port registry. Reyn could hear them still chatting, but now that he was by himself, he let wide the smile he’d been suppressing. Every grand tale had its curmudgeonly call to action, after all, so Reyn asserted quietly, “So my story begins…”
