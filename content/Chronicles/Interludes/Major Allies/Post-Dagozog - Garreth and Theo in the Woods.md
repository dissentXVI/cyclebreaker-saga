# Post-Dagozog — Garreth and Theo in the Woods

*After the battle of Dagozog, Garreth hunts goblins in the forest outside Laconia while Ariesa fights inside. He overhears a chilling exchange between two robed figures — Kyrichos and Haxidemos — and meets Theo, who was watching too.*

---

Garreth had practically lost himself in these unfamiliar woods. Out there was practically peace; sure the bands of goblins might not seem like they'd qualify, but they were periodic and fleeting nuisances. A line of frost here, a scorch of flame there, and a disappearance into the trees. It was one against many but Garreth was very good at hiding himself. Back in the village the invaders had been shaken off the fleeing D'yadyans; out here they were dissuaded from ever coming back.

Back in the city there was chaos and out here was only guilt. It helped to imagine each goblin was a small piece of his cowardice, but Garreth couldn't shake the feeling that he should be in the city. Sure, Ariesa made the point plainly, she and her half-elven friend from Shurevaire. Being psychically rewritten hadn't sounded great to him, and as he stalked like a hunting cat across the arbor's shadows for her foes, he had to reason he was still serving both his goals and Ariesa's. The sooner she found the center of this mystery, the sooner she'd return home with him.

Worry ate him up from the inside no matter how much he let himself trust. Worry and pneuma--they curled in his stomach like a rotten dinner, distracted him--the goblins heard and rounded toward him; their crashing feet and clattering weapons would be here in a second. Garreth whirled from behind the tree, outstretched a red hand and felt the ripple of magic move toward his palm. There was no time to think about steadying his breath, about aiming, about gripping tight the fraying edges of the woven spell--it was all practiced autonomic processes. His breath was still; his aim locked at the heart of the goblins' group; and he swallowed the chaotic leftovers of his spell like so much bile after too much brandy.

They had no chance; unlike his insides, the spell was immaculate in all aspects, the carefully honed pinpoint of the explosion perfectly placed. The goblins pulled inward toward that point like they were wind in an inhale, then exploded out as it made a disasterous sneeze. The sound rippled through the forest with a sharp crack, and the clatter of fallen goblins and their equipment settled just as quickly. None stood up again.

Out here there was no one to maintain a level expression for. Garreth grimaced as he loomed over the corpses. "We're all out of place, aren't we?" he whispered to them.

Alone though he was, he would not allow the after-effects of his spell out into the world. He took to a knee as he recovered from the way it twisted inside him. Out here he was safe; Ariesa had somehow threatened to open him up and make him spill everything. In the brush the consequences of magic were easy to bury deep inside.

Coincidentally, it was easy to bury himself there as well. It was a useful talent for hunting--or avoiding angry Tarwa'kin--but here it proved useful for the robed figures moving through the forest. Garreth hadn't heard them himself, and they appeared not to have noticed him.

"Your fruits weren't ripe; you plucked too early," the black-robed one opined as he toed a goblin's corpse. His robes were silk, his mask and effects like obsidian; his voice was similarly dark and dripped with an arrogance Garreth could easily mark.

His companion was even more nobly attired in white and gold, and this one's voice seemed wholly unconcerned with the waste of his harvest. "Oh Haxidemos, my short-sighted friend."

There was humor there, echoed as the black-robed one answers, "Kyrichos, my obfuscatory friend."

They shared a laugh, but Kyrichos did not elaborate but to say, "You’re missing the forest for the trees. The goblins are one weight of many upon the scales that *will* tip when the time is right. Keep close and watch as we close the book on this world at last."

“The reptilians and gnolls I understand, primitive as they are they possess a fervency and predilection for sacrifice. What I fail to grasp is your time with the Vocir–” Haxidemos was cut off.

Kyrichos cut in, “Valuable seeds have been sewn there and their planter has gone to rest. The field will not lay in ruins. What you fail to grasp is an appreciation for all things unfolding. Continue readying your pawns.”

Garreth's stomach turned cold; they carried themselves with all the entitlement, all the disregard of the worst of Tarwa'kem. What had he done to claim such responsibility here?

He's missed what Haxidemos had started to say, only finding the words again as the black-robed man said, "...trouble with your playthings. Whatever role you'd hoped for these creatures is evaporating."

"Patience, patience," answered the unperturbed Kyrichos. They continued their walk through the woods as he assured his companion, "Their old guard is gone, and those Frayed by their devotion will play the role I need. You worry too much, Haxi; have we erred yet?"

Their speech continued but Garreth was too far, and there was not cover enough to trail after them. The hidden tiefling started to exhale in relief as silence took the woods again, but he cut it off with a strangled noise when a voice called from the forest.

"It's good you didn't try casting near them," the voice tried to sound helpful, "If you saw the white-robe work pneuma like I did, you wouldn't want to risk it." The tree shimmered faintly where the gently spoken, well-mannered voice came from. Beyond the veil of illusion stepped a tall, bespectacled elf.

"Hello again, Garreth," smiled Theo, the gesture not quite reaching his eyes. It wasn't rude, just hard to smile after eavesdropping on such an exchange. "Did you come to rescue me?"

"In part," Garreth grunted as he rose from his knee and the brush, "In part the D'yadyans. I expected to find goblins, not... whatever they were."

Theo looked back off toward the figures' departure. "Architects of much sorrow, according to their own admission. They seemed to be searching for something--survivors, perhaps? The goblins I was entrusted with are safe," he added quickly, "But the one in white came by the village they'd been in. I don't think it was for wholesome purpose."

That earned a snort of laughter from Garreth. He mostly sobered as he said, "No, I don't think so. Why do you think he called it a Rejoining?" he asked, but the last syllable, the questioning tone, all faded as he watched the sky above the village. The violet clouds were clearing. "Twelve above, they did it."

Theo joined Garreth in a moment of wonder. They had both hoped, both trusted their respective friends; they had both also feared most terribly. Theo nodded once. "I don't think Dagozag was what he meant by that. He's setting up something else; in the village I watched him beneath this same spell. He mused to himself, intimating some other groups. Nothing useful, but we do know now we have more to fear than just goblins."

"Of course we do," accompanied the long-suffering sigh. Garreth was already looking back toward the village. "We should check on them. You can handle your goblins' safekeeping?" he checked with Theo.

The elf remained patient and offered instead a hand on the tiefling's shoulder. "Rest easy, Garreth; if she were in danger, the clouds would still be sparking." Garreth opened his mouth to clarify, but Theo continued to speak. "Come with me; walk them back to the village. Between the two of us, they'll make it safely."

Garreth couldn't deny some sympathy, but with the mind-screwery gone from Laconia, he knew where he should be. Besides, he pointed out, "The villagers will never accept the goblins amongst them. You'd be leading them into a slaughter."

Theo only chuckled. "I've had a few drinks with your kula-mate, Mister Teufeloaken. If anyone will win these goblins amnesty..." His humor turned to warmth as he promises Garreth, "And she will."

Not for goblins, Garreth didn't say; he refused to think about what he truly feared. "They'll be better off fending for themselves. It would take..." he struggled for the words, wearied from the fights and the sneaking, "A monumental change. They've been enemies too long; how could so much conflict be forgotten? What could she do to change so many minds?"

Theo only parted his hands in a sort of shrug as he suggested, "Killing a Paragon might be a fair way to start that process."

Garreth didn't have enough pessimism to weigh against that. He remained silent as he followed Theo.
