# Tavern Tales

*On the road with Diazana and Sylviane, Jendrick handles a drunk picking a fight at a tavern. Later, alone at the campfire, he burns a letter he was going to send back to Laconia.*

---

“I’m beginning to think you might have more luck if you just keep your mouth shut,” opined the scholar when Jendrick returned to their table. The group had quickly put together that matters went smoother when the warrior did the ordering at the bar and Diazana held down the table with Sylviane. In the corner (the one cast in shadows; you know the one).

	“What?” Jendrick asked with an arched brow, setting down flagons and mugs before the ladies at the table. He sat before Diazana. It didn’t block her completely from the room’s view, but again–things tended to go more smoothly.

	Sylviane reached for her mug, stretching across the table as she teases, “We saw that woman approach you.”

	“And retreat just as fast,” Diazana snickered. Jendrick had noted the scholar wasn’t the motherly type, but what the two girls had bonded over quickly was teaming up against him.

	This bit was fine though. He just shrugged and noted smugly, “Plenty of them, just one of me. Besides, we have important things to discuss.”

	Sylviane rolled her eyes, but Diazana was willing to push back. The tiefling couldn’t keep the amusement out of her one or her eyes as she pointed out, “Ylaena says you have lost plenty of opportunities. I don’t think you’ve won even once–not according to my spies.”

	That much was true, Jendrick knew–not once, not in a long time, and not undeservedly so. That wasn’t the point he wanted to push, though, so he was quietly grateful when Sylviane spoke up.

	“Ylaena spies for you?”

	“Oh, not without some bargaining. She doesn’t have fun with gossip like Gothmog or Theo. She’s definitely like Yenden in that way. But no,  I had to make sure our escort could be trusted, right? Our grim arcanist agrees with the young mayor–he may flunk his flirting courses, but he can be trusted,” notes the red-skinned woman as she eyes him over her lenses. 

	The elf girl’s eyes turned to him, and some flustered warmth crept onto his neck. He steered the carriage of this conversation to point out, “Not that you need to worry. Sylviane’s learning fast with that sword, and your sorcery–”

	Maybe he didn’t need to be so hasty to deflect the conversation. The southern road around what had been Lake Sticaea in his boyhood was littered with small villages and smaller minds, and one of them had just gotten drunk enough to wander over. 

	“I thought you were one of *them*, hiding over here,” the big man slurred as he loomed behind Jendrick’s shoulder. This was the trade-off, he knew. His arm shifted and his feet planted–the jackass behind him was focused on Diazana, not the subtle movements Jendrick took to put his elbow in better striking position. Based off shadow, sound, and peripheral cues, the dark-haired fighter figured he could drop a strike between the drunk’s leg if things escalated.

	Sylviane looked angry, tightening her small hand around the handle of her mug. Too long cooled, if there was indeed liquid left inside, Jendrick thought. Some small piece of him was amused–her twin seemed to instinctually believe he could beat anyone in a contest of wit, while Sylviane herself looked eager to prove herself in a fight. Couple that with Diazana’s love of needling people, and the elf might just find the fight she was itching for.

	True to form, Diazana looked up, all innocence, and asked, “Do women often hide from you here?”

	A leg moved up, confrontational and bumping into the back of Jendrick’s chair. His angle would be worse, but deescalation had to be on the table still. He held up his hand, empty and non-threatening, and said, “Maybe we’ve camped too long at this table. It’s late,” he began, and paused briefly while a heavy hand landed with the intent to discomfort on his neck.

	“Shut up, demon-lover. We got enough for you too,” promised the drunk, grip grown tight on Jendrick’s shoulder. Was that weight all menace, or did the brute behind him trust his balance to that arm too?

	“We? Good for you, making friends.” Diazana’s words could not be mistaken for sincere, even by the drunk. 

	Jendrick put his hand on the stranger’s hand, underselling his own strength. If this man was going to push trouble, Diazana was going to push back. Sylviane was already murmuring curses in elvish. He might as well put himself in the best position to fight back…

	“Sir, your hand–it’s digging in,” Jendrick grunted, making his shoulder shift ever so slightly as if to try and give. His acting was rewarded with a doubling down.

	“None of you should have fu–uhwup!” the drunk started menacing but ended with a fall as Jendrick dropped his shoulder and turned. The chair went out, the drunk started going down, and Jendrick stood up, shoving out with his hands as he stood. The brawny drunk hadn’t been brave enough to start shit on his own, just as Jendrick had feared, but now instead of rushing around their ringleader, his two cronies had to dodge the slow stumble of the instigator. 

	Sylviane flung her mug like a sling’s shot into the face of one of the two lurking villagers. Diazana stayed seated and sipping from her flagon.

	“Bitch!” cried out the second stranger, clutching his nose. The mug had broken, spilling a little blood and less tea. 

	Jendrick had to hand it to her–Sylviane had stopped the advance of the one most open to do so. The other had a brawny sprawl of a friend and a fallen chair to navigate. Jendrick squared his shoulders and brought up his hands, ready to guard or jab, and invited the men, “Go back to your seats. We’ll take our leave and let you digest your lesson in peace.”

	They weren’t too drunk to consider the offer. One spat, sure, but he was far from hitting anyone; the three men stumbled back to their table and amidst a sea of hard stares Jendrick, Diazana, and Sylviane slid out of the tavern. They weren’t likely to get the deposit back on their rooms for the night, but they had finished supper, so if one considered it a trade of coin for dinner and entertainment, well, it wasn’t so bad.

Even Sylviane wasn’t so miffed about another night camping. She was reenacting her pitch around the campfire, all excited animation as she noted, “SMACK, right in the face! Those *connards *slunk away so fast; gods, how funny! Di, you must be annoyed–you didn’t even get a chance to throw a spell,” she empathized, but right on the heel of that sentiment she asked, “Did you see how smooth I charged that mug with a spell?”

“Very subtle,” Diazana agreed with light praise, “And no, they got a good lesson about starting trouble and I got a little more time to think. Why should I be annoyed?”

Sylviane shrugged. “I wanted to hit him. I assumed you’d want to too.”

Even Jendrick had to chuckle at that, joining in on the pair’s light laugher over the recap. He stayed watch over the fire while they went to ready for camp, chatting as they went. Left alone, he turned over his letter, considering it before lofting it into the flames. They would turn off the road soon and not see another chance to send a message back to Laconia for a while. The observations of the towns and wilds he had made since leaving with Diazana and Sylviane hadn’t found any other hints of paragon-related activity anyway. Mireya would assume no news was good news–and be far too busy to worry regardless. 

Let me be the one to worry, Jendrick resolved as he watched the flames crack the sticks they fed on. Worry felt bad in a good way, like picking at a not-quite-healed scab. Worry over what he could have done back in the tavern, both prior to and after the fight. What might have gone wrong; where he could improve; what might have gone wrong–all swirled in the flames and smoke before him, clouding his senses such that he didn’t even realize someone had snuck up behind him.

“Jumpy, aren’t you?”

He had jumped in fairness, but hadn’t done anything to defend himself once he had realized–quickly–that it was just Diazana. He just asked, “Sylviane down for the count?”

“Getting there,” the tiefling shrugged as she reached for some of her things left at the fire. She waved dismissively at his move to make room, “Don’t worry, I’m not going to intrude. You get first watch.”

	“You surprised me,” Jendrick admitted as he settled, “I thought after pushing that guy you were going to want to smack him around a little bit. The kid wouldn’t have blamed you.”

	Diazana smirked, bright teeth reflecting the firelight. “You two had it handled. Besides, if we’re going to turn this world around, people like him are going to have to learn that their behavior isn’t welcome by people like you.”

	Fair point. “And you get to look uninvolved,” Jendrick pointed out. “Probably for the best. If they thought you were really dangerous, they might have called the guard.”

	“Not worth the effort over a skirmish,” the scholar chimed back with full agreement. 

	She lingered, watching him. Jendrick tried to turn, assuming the conversation was over, but he could feel the studying eyes prickle the back of his neck. 

	“I’ll wake you for your watch,” he promised, then added with good-natured mirth, “On time.” She had gotten cross when he tried to take on more of the watch burden when they first started out.

	“You’re an odd person, Mister Ironbrook, but I don’t think a bad one. Enjoy your watch,” she wished him before disappearing to her own tent.
