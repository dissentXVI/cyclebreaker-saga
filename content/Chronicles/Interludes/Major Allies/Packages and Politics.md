Packages and Politics

*This story takes place shortly after the party’s arrival in Tarwa’kem, prior to their productive meeting with the Sultana. Outside of the scholars’ mail post in Laconia the sixteen year old mayor, Mireya Lunacrest, runs into the sheriff, Zeyn Choukri, who holds himself at arm’s length from the community that Laconia offers. Mireya tries to breach his shields, while Zeyn tries to get away for a mission most secretive.*

	“Last stop of the day,” the Sheriff of Laconia explained from atop his stallion. Staring up his way and catching him for conversation was the dark-haired mayor, young Miss Lunacrest, waiting outside the scholar’s cottage which had turned into a sort-of post office. “And Chisisi won’t mind the break.”

	The horse snorted and shook his mane beneath the D’yadyan’s gloved hand. He seemed ready to ride to Mireya’s eye, even if the townsguard had been training all day. 

	She was not ready to call them soldiers by any means. Instead she addressed the knight by name, sharing a smile. Her hand raised too, not in a salute but a cover for her eyes against the afternoon sun. “Zeyn, well met. I trust you’re happy with the way your recruits are learning? Walls are back to good shape, and we’ve turned inward toward the public buildings now. You might not realize we were attacked by end of summer.”

	“You might not realize it, but your people wear it in the face. Buildings repaired or not, every man, woman, child… Laconia joins rank with my own people, and those of the goblins and tiefling as well,” he observed to her coolly. 

	Pessimist. Mireya had dwelled in such places before, but her dreams of late had been more uplifting. It was hard not to feel empowered when one dreamed of visits from dead Titans. “Then we’ll make for good housemates, won’t we? And we’ll be better prepared for the future at that. Not,” she added with a thin smirk. He’d been the one to bring up the tieflings, after all, “If you keep shoving a spear in every newcomer’s hand and drilling them before they’re settled. Before they’ve even had a chance to meet the Mayor–who I might remind you, Sheriff, has some ideas for the good of the whole community. We are a community, after all, not an army camp.”

	Optimist. Zeyn was unfamiliar with the territory; prudence taught him to plan for the worst. “So you are. But at least your home will be safe, and your people armed against disaster. And next time,” he allowed with a steel edge to his voice, “They ride in war, it will be against all the evils of this world.”

	“My home? Not ours?” wondered the girl stood below him.

	A brief shake of the head no was the answer she got at first. He looked out westward, hand on the horn of his saddle. “My home lies beyond the mountains. Where I was born, as this place is for you.”

	“The point is this place, Laconia–it can be your home. It doesn’t mean you have to forget D’yadya or any of your people. It’s here for you–not just as a place to train and drill, but to rest and maybe even settle roots,” the young woman offered, and she offered it without looking away from the soldier’s eyes above. She had bristled in these early days as the sheriff had stood obstinate against her admittedly uncertain hand as mayor. Yenden agreed–he didn’t think Zeyn meant to be dismissive, but he did think Mireya needed a firmer stance if she wanted to lead and not dread this D’yadyan. 

	Zeyn stared down at her, his own visage not yielding an inch of his thoughts. He thought her own guard gave way too easily. She offered it honestly, believed what she was saying even–that was the problem with youth. The world lacked nuance through their eyes, he reasoned. It was not even her age; he had seen the truth of this world when he was eight, and she had to be twice that or so. No, the Vocirans had plunged this continent into shadow, and it was the light of his oath that provided home and guidance.

	Still, one couldn’t just linger on the memory of that day, just as one could not stare impassively at someone trying to be nice. The Sheriff painted a smile that passed for sincerity and assured Mireya, “So it is, Miss Lunacrest. And I am here for Laconia. You’ll be pleased to know we turned the last of the cells out earlier. Not that they’ll stay that way for long, but I figure they’re learning. This last one was causing some trouble for one of the tiefling youth. He’s headed back to Tarwa’kem as we speak.”

	She was pleased, that much was true. All the same she wasn’t pleased that he thought he could plant himself so stubbornly alone. Laconia was full of so many new faces, and those familiar to her had taken on a different note since she’d picked up leadership of the town. *And the ones that pushed me there up and left soon after,* she thought, and she could feel that give her smile the shading of a smirk. There wasn’t anger at them for it, though initially the quarreling with some of the more prickly townsfolks over being led by a sixteen year old had naturally pushed her toward mad at those heroes-turned-friends. With their support, though, and the dreams she’d had of late, she couldn’t say they’d been wrong.- She would get this right; they all would.

	“Well,” she allows at last with a resting of her hand on the scholars’ front door. Scholar. Yenden was the only one left. “I’d best drop this letter off. I’m hoping Laena’s shipment has arrived.”

	Zeyn’s expression lightened in a funny way, as if even his normally dour outlook could not help but poke a little fun, “The noblewoman travels for weeks to meet with the most powerful and vulnerable political force in Halinor, and you anticipate… the henna?” For a moment the young mayor felt like she could see the line open, the promise of developing the professional standoffishness to something more akin to friendship.

	Mireya cracked up briefly and admitted, “Yes, henna. And indigo, otherwise the red stays in the undertones.” Instinct drove her to share, “My parents were both dark of hair. Maybe it’s silly, but… I got so much good from them, like this home and a love for the locals and travelers, a sense of all that’s good in this world, but when I look in the mirror? It’s tough to see them unless I use the dye. I just miss seeing them,” she finished, feeling a little hollowed out for the oversharing. She had to reason that in a way friendship came about from reciprocal vulnerability, but it was hard to imagine the Sheriff vulnerable.

It remained difficult. The insight that Mireya felt open between them was choked off as the awkward silence settled. He cleared his throat, looking away for a moment, and supposed, “It’s grown late. You’d best see to your package.”

“Rest well, Sheriff. I pray you find some time to do good for your spirits and not just Laconia,” Mireya wished with a bow of her head toward the mounted soldier. It would hide the grimace she felt bubbling to the surface. Stupid.

	Grateful for the send-off, Zeyn offered a salute back and wheeled Chisisi’s hooves back to the road, off in the direction of his hut on the town’s outskirts…

	…And past it, beyond the posted guards at the gate and beyond the first reach of the woods. His horse moved at a steady pace, but like his master he gnawed at the bit on the westward road. Not more than a couple miles out waited two of his trusted deputies and a lean figure knelt over between them. Ragged and dressed in rags, he showed plain signs of exhaustion, bare feet dirty and beaten. Zeyn dismounted cleanly, approached the group. His men, good D’yadyan youth, wore no smiles as they dipped their heads in deference. They looked well-rested but a little confused.

	“This one still has talk in him,” one of them offered up to Zeyn with a half-shrug, speaking in some saltlander dialect wholly dipped in Tarwa’kin accent. It grated Zeyn’s nerves, but you couldn’t blame the boy.

	The other picked up, “But he doesn’t seem like, you know, any sort of big deal.” They sounded samey, probably from the same parts of D’yadya. Well, their parents had been anyway. 

	“That’s the problem with evil men,” Zeyn informed them as he joined them in their little thicket, using the steel of his boot’s toe to nudge the Tarwa’kin man in the ribs. The Sheriff continued, “There’s a lot of overlap with ‘normal’.” He swapped from D’yadyan over to Tarwa’kin and barked down at the captive, “Look up, friend. I’m the law around here. You Maladhi?”

	Hearing his own language gave the wretch on his hands and knees a glimmer of hope. “Yes, yes! By the Gods, I am from Maladh, yes. Please, I am a simple merchant, come to peddle goods for the treasure-seekers–I am guilty of no crime!”

	“Maladh is famous for their entrepreneurial spirit,” Zeyn explained to the two deputies, still in Tarwa’kin. They showed no slowdown in swapping between the two languages, listening attentively as Zeyn explained, “Even I learned, at young age no less, how shrewd the Maladhi are at trade.”

	Something in the friendliness of his voice slipped and showed the steel beneath. The hope drained out of the Tarwa’kin.

	“Any guesses as to the going rate of safe passage, refreshed supplies, a very basic welcome even to the eastern half of Halinor? Keep in mind we had the hounds of death nipping at our heels, the slings and arrows of Vociterra raining behind us. To a compassionate man that may seem like impetus to act quickly; think like a *merchant*,” Zeyn implored the two deputies. 

	They weren’t dumb boys, just displaced. Their hands gripped and their jaws clenched, and Zeyn could see their imaginations strive to match the cruelty of reality. The merchant at Zeyn’s feet gripped the bottom of the sheriff’s pant leg, pleading hasty whispers for mercy. By the time Zeyn finished telling his tale to the youths, death became a mercy–and one late to arrive.

	The lawmen rode back to Laconia in a quiet, grim trio. The two boys digested what they’d just heard, what they’d seen, and what they’d allowed. Zeyn sat unsated astride Chisisi, but the calm of his Oath sat like a comforting weight on his shoulders.
