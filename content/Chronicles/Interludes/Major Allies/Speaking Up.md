# Speaking Up

*After the party leaves Tarwa'kem for Carinthia, Garreth faces the Kula elders to answer for Ariesa steering Beata and Bastien toward Laconia rather than home. He defends them — and pays a physical price.*

---

Speaking Up

*This story takes place after the party has left Tarwa’kem and started their travels for Carinthia. They have just encouraged the tieflings Beata and Bastien to visit Laconia for a possibility at a crime-free life outside of the kula. Ariesa took up this request from Garreth to help convince the tiefling youths to return to the kula, but of course Ariesa chose a new way–and now Garreth stands in a circle of tiefling elders to answer for it.*

	The funny thing about using curtains and beads for doors is that they do piss-all for stopping the sounds of conversation. Maybe that’s the point, Garreth reflected as the Elders took their turns rounding on him–they get multiple lessons for the price of one. Whichever of the kids were huddled outside the temporary meeting hall would feel the sympathetic stings of this berating–fertile soil for the restrictions and cautions that would be sure to follow. Garreth felt the bile tickle in his throat.

	“Lost in the world! Out there roaming with neither map nor sense, those two, from a home that shelters them to a world of, what, indifference at best? They have their problems, this Laconia, and you think they’ll drop them all for two of our children?” the mottled-purple tiefling ranted, each word poking a gnarled cane off in Garreth’s direction. “Two more mouths to feed and just attacked by goblins, he tells me of this village; stars and stones! Drop them in a ditch yourself, it would be faster!”

	Matron Zafira raised her own hand, calling for calm. “They come from your wagon train, Malusch; we feel your pain. But young Garreth here, he is a man of sense and good judgment. Let him speak his plan,” she said reasonably. “I know he has good cause to separate our children from loving wagons and ancient traditions. Garreth must tell us what insight he’s had that teaches him these two tieflings will be better in a far-off land, learning nothing of their people, isolated in a hostile world and far from the loving mentors and feeble elders that relied on them.” 

	The Patriarch’s nostrils made a slight flare and he leaned forward, smoke billowing from them as he shed the exhale from his pipe. The flood of scented smoke billowed about the Patriarch’s gnarled hands as he gestured to permit Garreth to speak.

	The spit had dried out of Garreth’s mouth. Prickles of acid roiled in his gut as bad as if he’d been working magic. Pushing down bile and chaos had been more or less a trained reflex since he had begun to work any sort of pneuma bigger than a cantrip. How quickly child-Garreth had reconciled the disapproval Ariesa faced with her own wild magic, after all, with the cost it took to swallow the natural consequences of those spells. Some part of him whispered that he could escape this council’s ire; all he had to do was give them the scapegoat he knew they wanted.

	Hadn’t he been tricked, after all? His methodology had been unorthodox–sending the inspiration for the youths’ leaving (Ariesa) to wrangle them back home–but odd problems sometimes required strange solutions. Ariesa had demonstrated signs of growth in magic as well, and she had a team of supportive friends, and it had been so nice to pretend she was back in a place where they could trust one another, and there had been the matter of Dagozog to consider, but at her core, whispered that well-honed personal reflex, Ariesa was a problem. She wanted to abandon him, her people, her responsibilities–and all for what?

	He could have spoken those thoughts and turned the collective eye of scrutiny away from him, but a twitch of some unfamiliar emotion opened his mouth and spoke for him. “Malusch has the right of it–and he should, shouldn’t he? Bastien was your direct apprentice, after all, and we have all heard you complain–early and often!--about his work ethic and focus. Madame Zafira is more familiar with Beata, aren’t you? She was the one you pointed out as being too hungry for the kula, wanting more than her share of goods and glories. These children are closer to twenty than the cribs, and if we are to be a community and not a prison, we have to begin treating them like adults and not children. Yes, they did not fit in perfectly with their ‘place’ here, and yes, we miss them dearly, but they have not just found a niche out in the world, they have found purpose. I know,” he tells the two elders, but now his gaze of challenge leaves them, sweeping around the meeting circle toward those others he has long held in deference, “They have met hardship, but I have seen first hand how happy she is.” A beat followed in which some of the stares from the council stifled his momentum, and he quickly corrected, “Beata, that is. And Bastien too.”

	Silence sat for a long time, and the weight of it on his chest emphasized the pounding heart therein. Garreth realized how much stress he was carrying in his body, but he couldn’t find a way to release the vise hold it had on him.

	Frustration broke the silence but not his bonds. From outside the tent a woman’s cry cut in as she did, fury spilling out as she accused, “That is my daughter! She is from our train, Teufeloaken, not yours! She is not yours to steer away from safety. When she bleeds out in a foreign land, it will be your fault her death comes so fast, instead of long years ahead with family and friends gathered around. She dreams of a different life because she **is **a child; she only chases them because of **you**!”

The tickle of the bile in his throat was more insistent, but the same rage he’d found earlier couldn’t be summoned to banish the dryness from his mouth. Instead the bile bubbled and took care of that problem, and Garreth buckled forward, vomiting over the floor of the wagon-turned-meeting-hall. The kula’s elders stood and hastened a step or two backward, the clamor turning to alarm as they realize just what it was he was throwing up–the viscous nature of that pitch black ooze, the grasping tendrils that reach up into the air and curling in short-lived want and animation. The vile emission distracted the group only a moment from the young man writhing on the ground and the feathery growths working their way out of his skin. Dark ooze bled from his eyes and ears before they finally realized the trouble Garreth was in.
