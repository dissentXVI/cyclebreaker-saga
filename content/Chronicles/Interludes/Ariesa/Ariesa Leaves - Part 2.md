# Ariesa Leaves — Part 2

*Ariesa's late-night confrontation with Pappy before she slips away from the Kula. Continues from [[Ariesa Runs Away]].*

---

“Going somewhere?” 
 Ariesa froze for a beat before slowly turning to face the wizened old Tiefling at the front 
of her tent. While clutching the rucksack to her chest, she heard herself offer an uncertain 
“Nnnnno?” 
 It wasn’t necessary to follow that up with a correction. It was too painfully obvious. 
 Pappy scowled at her as she squirmed in embarrassed silence. The scowl was right at 
home on his wrinkled, wine-purple face. His bright white hair rose messily around a single horn 
jutting up from his right temple. The stump of the other horn, which he had lost before Ariesa 
was born, was hidden by the snowy halo. A pair of thick spectacles testified to years of straining 
his eyes over delicate repair work, a tinker’s ultimate badge of experience.  
 Pappy shuffled over to a chair and, groaning the privileged groans of the aged, lowered 
himself into it. “Damn cold weather making my hip all…There. Ah, there we go. Now,” he said, 
turning his attention back to the blushing Ariesa, “Pull that junk out of your bag and get your ass 
back to bed.”   
 “Pappy, look –” 
 “Don’t you ‘Pappy, look’ me, girl! ‘Pappy, look.’ By the twelve, you whine like a busted 
cart wheel.” 
 Ariesa glared at him. He glared back. Without taking her eyes off his sour face, she 
reached for the candleholder on her nightstand.  
 “Hey! Hey, don’t you do that.” 
 She raised one eyebrow, hooked a finger through the handle. 
 “You better not—” 
 She tossed the candleholder into her rucksack. 
 “DAMN YOUR BROKEN EARS, YOU MULEHEADED BUFFOON. YOU. AIN’T. GOING. 
NOWHERE.” 
 “I absolutely am. And I’m going to need a jacket.” She began pulling heavy clothes out of 
a trunk while Pappy fumed, huffed, and crossed his arms. 
 “Reckon I shouldn’t have worried about you running off to try to kill a hag, huh?” He 
hocked a glob of phlegm to the floor and snorted. “Didn’t figure you to just give up and run 
away.” 
 Her attention grabbed, she paused her packing and sighed. 
 “I’ve heard ‘Garreth’s right’ from two separate people tonight, and I’m about to make it 
three. Garreth was right. If I pick a fight with a hag she’ll dismember me and whittle down my 
femurs for toothpicks.” 
 Pappy smiled, showing teeth. “That Garreth is a smart young man.” 
 Ariesa growled at him and resumed shoving winter clothes into the sack.  
 “The point is – these breeches are frayed – that I’ve obviously honed my magical abilities 
all I possibly can here, and I need to go elsewhere to truly develop my power.” 
 “Magical abilities? Power?” Pappy scoffed. “You supposed to have those? Girl, the last 
time you tried to speak to me in my mind I had tinnitus for two weeks.” 
 “Because you resisted!”  
 “Cuz I don’t care what you have to say!” 
 Sensing a shift in the mood, Ariesa stood to give the top of Pappy’s head a playful kiss. 
“Yes, you do.” 
 “No, I don’t,” He grumbled. But he took her hand and gave it a squeeze. “At least sleep 
on it. That critter scratched you up bad.” 
 “So you are going to let me go,” Ariesa said with a smile. 
 “I’d stop you if I could, but you’re bigger’n me. Stupid of em to deputize me for this. 
What am I supposed to do? Trip you with my cane?” 
 “You don’t have a cane.” 
 “See? Impossible. Well now what are you doing? Stand up. I said git.” 
 Ariesa knelt at Volkar’s feet, holding on tightly to his hand. “I’ll go in a minute, but I want 
to know you understand why I’m doing this. You do, don’t you?” 
 Volkar grunted. “Since when do you care what I think?” 
 “Please, Pappy.” 
 He closed his eyes and sighed. “We been around the Tarwa’kin long enough for me to 
learn that every bill comes due. When they came for your granny and me – ” A pained 
expression shot across his face and he absently rubbed his head. “Well, let’s not…Anyway, after 
it happened I didn’t want nothing but to send the whole lot of em to Siren. And not just the 
ones that did it. Everyone. Me, too.” He gulped. “Your mama, too. I had to let Zocrya take her—
our brand-new baby— cuz I wasn’t sure what I was gonna do from one minute to the next. But I 
never could quite figure out what really needed doing. It was like – it was like a leaky kettle 
without a leak to patch, you understand? Frustrating.” He paused, thinking, before continuing. “I 
was saying every bill comes due. Maybe that’s now, baby girl.” He leaned forward to wrap Ariesa 
in his thin arms. “But damn it I hope I’m doing the right thing.” 
 Ariesa hugged him back. “You are. I promise.” 
 “You gonna tell Garreth you’re going?” 
 Ariesa pulled back with a mirthless laugh. “And why would I do that? I already had to 
deal with you. I don’t want another lecture.” She added, mumbling, “He’ll be glad I’m gone, 
anyway. He just thinks I’m a menace.” 
 “You’re dumber than a leaky kettle.” 
 “You’re bringing up kettles a lot, old man.”  
 He swiped at her shoulder with his palm, but she dodged out of the way. “If I talked to 
my Pappy that way, he would’ve taken a switch to my backside.”  
 “Lucky for me I’m bigger than you, then.” She paused before leaving the tent, turning 
back to Volkar with an elated grin. “The next time you hear from me, I’ll be a powerful sorceress 
and a regional hero.”  
 “And still a pain in the ass.” 
 “I love you, Pappy.” 
 He sat in the darkness of her tent until morning, his mind wandering all the places, past 
and future, his age gave it the power to go, but his heart went with his stubborn granddaughter.  
 “I love you, too, Ariesa.” He finally told the empty air. “Be safe out there.”
