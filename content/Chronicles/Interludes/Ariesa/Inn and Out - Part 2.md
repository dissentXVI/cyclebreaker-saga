# Inn and Out — Part 2

*Ariesa and Tsua Zuberi share dinner at the Merry Mountain. Tsua has been searching for the Kula for years to make amends. Ariesa turns the evening into a lesson for the innkeeper. Continues from [[Inn and Out - Part 1]].*

---

The Merry Mountain’s proprietor himself seated them in the most lavish private room 
Ariesa had ever seen. Lush green curtains bordered the windows and rugs of a similar hue were 
artfully arranged across the polished floor. Jugs of spring flowers sat atop the carved mantle of a 
large fireplace. Ariesa gulped. After a lifetime of shopkeepers shooting her dirty looks and 
guards casually trailing her whenever she set foot in “nice” parts of town, places like this made 
her jumpy.  
She tried to disguise her discomfort by taking another sip of the cold, crisp well water in 
her cup. Tsua sat across from her, long-fingered hands folded delicately in her lap, the picture of 
refinement. Her eyes, though, betrayed emotion. They drank in Ariesa’s presence with an 
almost feverish delight.  
The tense silence was broken by the creak of the chamber door. A serving woman 
entered and approached them with a curtsy.  
“Good evening, ladies. May I bring you some supper?” 
Ariesa recognized the harried, sheet-carting worker she spoke with outside and smiled. 
“Hello, again! You’ve done quite a job of making the place palatial. I’m scared to touch 
anything.”  
The woman blushed and laughed. “Just don’t check under the rug by the fireplace and 
we can keep the illusion alive.” She paled immediately, casting a nervous look at Tsua.  
The noblewoman gave her an encouraging smile. “It really is lovely. I apologize for 
inconveniencing you so.” 
“It’s no inconvenience, milady. This is the most excitement the Mountain has seen in 
months. Your Captain said you’ve been on the road all day. You must be hungry. Our proprietor 
suggests the seared quail with fire-grilled forest mushrooms?” 
“That sounds wonder – ” 
“Actually,” Ariesa interrupted, “Could you let us know what the kitchen staff are having 
tonight?” 
“Oh!” The server laughed again. “Well, I can tell you what I’m eating. Slow-roasted 
winter vegetables and some stewed rabbit, all procured today,” She gave Ariesa a knowing look, 
“And taken straight to the kitchen for cooking.”  
“We’ll have that, thanks.” 
With a parting wink, the server was gone. 
Tsua glanced from Ariesa to the door, clearly aware she was missing something. 
“How…delightfully rustic. I don’t believe I’ve ever had stewed rabbit.”  
“Trust me, it will be better than anything fancy they could find in the cellar,” She 
grimaced, the angry squeaks of rats ringing in her ears. “If it makes you feel any better, I’ve 
never had stewed rabbit in the company of Tarwa’kin nobility. So…why am I having stewed 
rabbit in the company of Tarwa’kin nobility? Your guard dogs didn’t seem too happy about it.” 
“Captain Khaba? He’s quite protective of me, I admit. He’s been with my family since 
before I was born. But his displeasure notwithstanding, I desperately need to speak with you.” 
She rose to fetch an embroidered pouch from her travelling chest. Her movements flowed 
gracefully into one another, making each step or dip a small work of art. It struck Ariesa that this 
woman, with her grace and her silken black hair and her flawless complexion, could quite easily 
be considered beautiful.  
Tsua opened the embroidered bag before Ariesa. It was filled to the brim with gold, 
more money than Ariesa could earn in a whole year of endlessly clearing dire rat nests out of 
cellars. Ariesa snatched her hands back from the table as though she was going to be accused of 
stealing it.  
“Please take this back to your kula, as part of my apology for,” Her large eyes grew misty 
as she spoke, “For what happened.” She mistook Ariesa’s stunned silence for something darker 
and rushed on, “I know it’s nowhere near enough, but it’s what I’ve been able to save without 
anyone noticing. I’ll do more. I can’t imagine how awful it was to be cast from the city that way. 
I heard there were instances of property damage and…and injuries. Please,” she cried, clasping 
her hands to her chest, “You must tell me. Is Garreth well? Was he hurt?” 
Ariesa’s astonishment froze into something tight and unpleasant in her chest. Here was a 
Tarwa’kin noble tearfully asking her about Garreth’s well-being. And she was rich, and kind, and 
beautiful, and gentle, which was all beside the point (why had she thought about that?) because 
what mattered was that Tsua Zuberi really was the reason Ariesa lost her closest friend and 
that’s why a glacier of nameless dread was scraping against her ribs. 
Ariesa opened her mouth to snap that Garreth was no concern of some Tarwa’kin noble 
who had almost gotten him killed (even though that wasn’t what happened, was it?) Before she 
spoke, twin tears spilled out of Tsua’s eyes and Ariesa sighed instead. 
“No. He wasn’t hurt. And he’s as well as he can be considering the oppressive gloom that 
follows him everywhere.” 
Tsua closed her eyes and lifted her clasped hands in prayer. “Thank Tytos,” she 
murmured. She sniffled and wiped at her eyes. Somehow even her crying was delicate. “And 
thank you, for bringing me this blessed news. The Lord of Justice knows I don’t deserve it. I let 
Rah’lul and Badru threaten him, and if you hadn’t intervened, I can’t bear to think what would 
have happened.”  
The burning glacier in Ariesa’s chest reminded her that Camitho, Tsua’s brother, was the 
one goaded into physically attacking Garreth. She bit her bottom lip, hard, to keep that cruel 
thought from spilling out. “So, you want to give the kula money to make up for it?” 
Tsua nodded. “I’ve been searching for you for years. Whenever I’m in the countryside, I 
keep my eyes and ears primed for word of Tiefling encampments, with no luck until today. It’s 
been made more difficult by the fact that I can’t request news…” 
“I take it your family isn’t exactly pleased with your plan?” 
“I’m ashamed to admit that they are not. Siren sought my grandfather a few years ago, 
and my brother has taken over the family. Camitho…is not the person he once was. He would 
not approve at all.” She brightened. “But now that I’ve found you, it will be easier. Can you take 
this money back? And tell me what else I can do to help?” 
Warmth overwhelmed the icy distress lodged in Ariesa’s heart, and she took Tsua by the 
hand. “Thank you. It takes a strong person to admit their mistakes and a stronger one to seek to 
make amends. I can tell you where the kula was when I saw them last.” She cleared her throat, 
forced herself to say, “You’ll have to take it there yourself.” 
Tsua’s face fell. “I’ll try. I’m sorry for my disappointment, but why can’t you deliver it, 
with my good wishes and apologies?” 
“Oh, I’ve actually left the kula.” Ariesa tried to sound casual and failed, her voice 
brimming with pride. 
“You left? That’s quite a decision. Garreth impressed upon me the importance of 
community to your people, of your mutual responsibility for one another.” 
“He told you that we…That’s – what a – well, then,” Ariesa drained the final drops of 
water from her cup.  
“I’m sorry. It seems I’ve upset you.” 
“Upset me? No, no. It just surprised me, that’s all.” It certainly was surprising to learn 
that Garreth, champion of “don’t trust outsiders, Ariesa,” was gossiping about their way of life 
with some random girl. 
“Oh,” Tsua’s brows knit together with concern. “Garreth must be heartbroken that 
you’ve gone. He always spoke so fondly of you.”  
Ariesa’s jaw tightened. It was really getting under her skin how often Garreth’s name 
slipped out of Tsua’s mouth. 
Their conversation was interrupted by the arrival of the food. A large tureen of stew, 
steaming hot, and a hefty platter of seasoned vegetables were set between them. Ariesa’s 
mouth watered. She began hungrily spooning out the hearty stew and passed the first bowl to 
Tsua. The noblewoman took it between her hands with murmured thanks and paused as if 
unsure what to do next. Ariesa couldn’t help but grin. 
“It’s okay. There’s no one here you need to impress with your social graces. I probably 
already broke twenty-seven rules of etiquette.” 
Tsua laughed. “Only eight. But thank you. It’s a deeply ingrained habit, considering such 
things.” She dipped her spoon into the soup. “Isn’t it funny? I’m finding it more stressful not 
having edicts of decorum to follow. Like being in a strange place without a guidebook.” 
“No offense, but that doesn’t make any sense. Why don’t you just, you know, eat what 
you want to eat?” 
“Well, for example, how might that make you feel? What if you were particularly excited 
to share this stew with me, but I spurned it in favor of the parsnips? In such a case I may miss an 
opportunity to connect with you. What if it were a Tiefling custom to eat roasted potatoes by 
spearing them with a knife instead of a fork? I might offend you, giving the impression that I 
don’t care enough to learn your ways.” 
Ariesa stared at her, a savory hunk of turnip halfway to her mouth. “Sounds like you 
invited your entire family to live in your head and now there’s nowhere for you to pitch your 
tent.” 
“Pardon me?” 
“When you’re a kid, your family scolds you about that stuff – the rules you need to 
follow, how you should act. Some of it you let into your head; you start asking yourself “what’s 
appropriate” even when your mom isn’t around to box your ears. But if all you ever think about 
are the laws that somebody else made up, there’s nothing left of you.” She swallowed the last of 
her stew. “Nowhere to pitch your tent.”  
After a pause, the noblewoman quietly said, “That’s quite the philosophy, Ariesa.”  
“If you let your dinner sit any longer, I’m going to eat it before it gets cold.” 
Tsua took a bite of stew and closed her eyes in rapture. 
The door to the suite banged open and the Inn’s proprietor stumbled in, the serving 
woman hot on his heels. A pair of amused looking guards with rabbits emblazoned on their 
breastplates looked on through the entryway.  
“Lady Zuberi, I must apologize to you! I was told you were given the incorrect meal,” 
“It wasn’t incorrect. She said it’s what she wanted,” The serving woman snapped. 
“Hold your tongue before our esteemed guest!” His eyes swept across the table and 
widened, taking in Ariesa’s presence for the first time. “…guests. Our esteemed guests.” 
Ariesa’s smile was sharp enough to cut glass. “I’m sorry, are you suggesting there’s 
something wrong with the stew and the vegetables? Because I specifically requested a meal 
that hadn’t been stored in…” 
“Anywhere! Hadn’t been stored anywhere!” He rushed to interrupt. “Only the freshest 
of ingredients for such a patron. Absolutely. My mistake, my mistake.” 
Ariesa called to him as he tried to bow awkwardly out of the room. “Before you go, Lady 
Zuberi wants to have a word with you.” 
She paled. “I…I do?” 
“Absolutely, she does. About Tieflings.” Ariesa gave Tsua a look.  
“About…Tieflings?” The proprietor repeated. 
“Yes. She’s quite concerned that the Merry Mountain hasn’t been friendly to Tiefling 
guests and workers in the past.” 
The proprietor’s twitchy eyebrows shot up. “Oh! Well, I…I’m sure our Inn is welcoming to 
any traveler and…” 
“No, that’s not what we’ve heard at all. In fact, we’ve heard that you discourage Tieflings 
from coming near your Inn.” 
He looked thoroughly miserable. “You understand, Lady Zuberi, that I run a simple 
business, and people these days are so close-minded…” 
“No, I don’t think she does understand. Do you, Lady Zuberi?” 
Tsue straightened in her seat. She managed to give the proprietor a look that might be 
called stern. “No, my friend. I do not understand.”  
“I thought not. So Lady Zuberi would like you to alter how you treat Tieflings – patrons 
or workers – or else she’ll use her vast influence in a way you won’t find pleasant.”  
Tsua nearly jumped out of her seat, but Ariesa gave her a swift kick under the table. Tsua 
squeaked.  
 Ariesa plowed on, “Isn’t that right, Lady Zuberi?” 
 “Yes? Yes. That’s right.” 
 After a series of simpering apologies and promises to mend his practices, the Innkeeper 
managed to drag himself from the room. The serving woman followed him, laughing into her 
apron. 
 Ariesa felt long fingers wrap around her hand and looked up into Tsua’s brightened eyes. 
“That was…exhilarating!”  
 The Tiefling woman grinned. “You signed up to help the Tiefling cause. I think this was a 
fantastic first step.”
