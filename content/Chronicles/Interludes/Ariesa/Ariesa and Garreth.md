# Ariesa and Garreth

*Youth in the Kula — a lesson with Scholar Zorakas, and the night Garreth's powers first manifested while Ariesa's first spell went catastrophically wrong.*

---

*CRACK* 
The smack of a thin branch connecting to skin was punctuated by a pained yelp. Garreth 
blinked and looked up from his scrolls, his head turning automatically towards Ariesa, who was, 
without fail, always the source of commotion during their lessons. Sure enough, Ariesa was 
rubbing at her knuckles while Scholar Zorakas loomed over her with his switch.  
Though the day was fair, a shining sun cavorting with fluffy white clouds, their teacher’s 
expression was thunderous. “How you expect to make any progress in your spellcasting without 
expending any effort whatsoever is beyond even my prodigious ken.” Ariesa scowled at their 
teacher, who continued his lecture undeterred. “Wipe that look off your face. Cease your 
infernal daydreaming and read. Do you have any idea how difficult it was for me to procure 
copies of Spellcaster Oryx’s Rudimentary Magicks? And this is the thanks I get.”  
 Garreth’s breath caught in his throat as he hoped against hope that his friend would 
listen to the scholar, back down, and return to her reading. But today wasn’t a day for miracles. 
 “This book doesn’t even make any sense. I don’t think Oryx knew anything about magic 
at all.” Ariesa squared her shoulders while the teacher sputtered with rage. She soldiered on: 
“Or at least nothing about my magic.”  
 Garreth winced as Zorakas delivered another stroke of the switch across Ariesa’s hand. 
“Your magic? Impertinent whelp. If you would but attend to the masterful instructions 
contained in these priceless tombs, you wouldn’t be such a failure of a spellcaster.”  
Garreth could tell that Ariesa was fighting to hold back tears, whether from the stinging 
pain of the switch or their teacher’s cutting words he couldn’t tell. But instead of backing down, 
which would be sensible, she just got louder. “If books really were great at teaching, you 
wouldn’t have to hit me so much! And then you wouldn’t have anybody to push around!”  
Before Zorakas could respond, Garreth was surprised to hear his own voice interject. 
“Scholar Zorakas, as Ariesa said, Spellcaster Oryx’s methods do differ from your own. Her 
practical magic use does not often intersect with the lessons you’ve taught so far. Is there a 
superior approach, or are the differences artificial?”  
Zorakas’s chest noticeably puffed up when Garreth tacitly compared his abilities to the 
Spellcaster’s. Ariesa forgotten, the Scholar turned to his star pupil. “I wouldn’t be so bold as to 
claim that there is a more potent or a more natural way for practitioners to approach magic 
use. Indeed, as you know,” Emphasis on the “you,” with a cold, exclusionary glance at Ariesa, 
“there are not only various schools of magic, but various ways to tap into magic. Warlocks, for 
example, with their demonic pacts, and Wizards with their years of arduous study.” Their 
teacher droned on, reciting principles Garreth already knew by heart, until their lesson ended 
and they were allowed to scamper away.  
Garreth kept pace with Ariesa through their makeshift village, darting around carts and 
campfires, laughing mothers and scolding fathers, until they reached the woods outside of the 
circle of Kula tents. They slowed, panting with exertion, until a choked sound from Ariesa made 
Garreth steal a glance at her. He suspected she was crying but didn’t want her to think he 
noticed. Her curling black hair was frizzy and mussed and served to conceal her face for a few 
more moments before she threw her head back and fully released her laughter into the trees. 
 “If Zorakas gives me one more book to read,” She gasped through her chuckles, “I’m 
using it for kindling. Can you imagine? ‘Ariesa, where’s Snooty Master So-and-So’s Boring 
Treatise on Illusions? Oh, that’s a lovely cooking fire. Yes, I’ll have some tea. Sip. My, my. 
Tastes…wordy.’” 
Garreth felt his jaw drop and his heart race. He wished he could be like that – able to 
just laugh off a lecture or a switching. He couldn’t believe he was friends with someone so 
brave. 
He cleared his throat and turned away to hide his blush. “It’s no use getting mad at the 
Scholar, you know. Yelling at him won’t make him put down the switch.” 
“Who cares? I’d rather he smack me with that stupid thing a million times than lecture 
me about magic.”  
Garreth turned to face her again and found her hanging upside down from a tree. She 
was grinning.  
“But…maybe if you paid attention in class,” He fumbled his words as her smile faltered. 
She knew what he was going to say, and it apparently stung her. But all he could do was spit 
out, “Maybe it would be easier for you to cast your spells?”  
She flipped gracelessly out of the tree and gave him a playful shove, disguising her hurt. 
“I’m no wizard, and neither are you. Our magic doesn’t come from that dry-as-dust nonsense.”  
“Well, no, but…” He faltered again as her eyes blazed dangerously. He went quiet. 
Placated, her expression turned thoughtful. 
“Garreth, you don’t have to do something just because you’re told to.” 
He bit back his knee-jerk response, which was along the lines of, “Yes, I do.” He was the 
Kula’s great hope, his magic a blessing from the divine. He couldn’t let everyone down. He 
wasn’t like Ariesa. He couldn’t just say whatever he was thinking whenever he felt like it.  
“Now, come on,” she pulled on his arm, leading him deeper into the woods. “We’ve got 
to keep training for the next Cataclysm. Since the last Beast came from a lake, I bet the next 
one will be from the mountains. What kind of spells work best in the cold? And what if it can 
fly?”  
Garreth smiled and let her pull him along. Young as he was, he was certain of one thing: 
between the two of them, Ariesa, unafraid to break the rules, would be the one to leave the 
Kula, see the world, pursue heroics. The heart, after all, is never too young to anticipate 
breaking.
