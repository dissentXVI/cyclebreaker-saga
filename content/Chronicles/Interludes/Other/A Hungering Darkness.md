# A Hungering Darkness

*On the road between Serinvale and Aqualonde, an elven family's wagon breaks down in the Fauconwood at night. One by one the family disappears. A well-dressed stranger with glowing eyes offers the surviving daughter shelter.*

---

The road between Serinvale and Aqualonde proper marked the border between the Fauconwood and the fae territories proper, the sort of trip a seasoned traveler would keep as short as possible. The eerie sway of shadow, the particular cracking of branches, the rustling of the boughs all took on a more oppressive haunt beneath a moon too thin for comfort. Mr. Aldren’s lantern cast long shadows from the broken down wagon, up into the unseasonably thin foliage where not even moths seemed to be disturbed by the light. Mrs. Aldren clutched her two children near, holding her watch with anxiety and quietly whispered words. 

	

“It’ll be okay Ellisar, Daddy can fix anything. He’ll patch it right up and we’ll be on our way home.” Her eyes darted between the trees across from her, long ears straining to keep watch on those she could not openly scan, “Selphie, go see if Daddy needs help.”

	

The eldest pulled her shawl tighter around her shoulders and approached her father.

“You need any help Daddy?”, her voice quiet against the silent emptiness of the night.

Mr. Aldren cursed under his breath, on his back staring underneath the wagon. He grunted, “Just keep an eye on those horses;keep them calm. I need to check the axle, and the last thing we want is something in these godsforsaken woods spooking them and sending the wheels running over me. Tell Ellisar to come hold this lantern for me.”

Selphie moved to the horses, running a trembling hand along their manes in what might have been a soothing fashion. The beasts shuddered, restlessly shaking their heads as their ears twitched towards the trees: nostrils flaring, hooves shifting, breath quickening. From the other side of the wagon, the lantern’s glow trembled as little Ellisar adjusted his grip; the shaky light cast dancing shapes along the underbrush.

A loud crack rang through the forest.

Mrs. Aldren gasped sharply. Ice took Selphie’s breath as she froze beside the horses.. They already turned towards the noise–toward the trees.

Beyond the wagon’s front lantern branches swayed. The wind whispered through the dead leaves and skeletal limbs of the trees. Silence returned like a weight.

Selphie finally caught her breath. “What was that, Mama?”

Mrs. Aldren’s wide eyes scanned the shifting treetops, her elven eyes sharp but finding nothing amongst the shadows. “I-I’m sure it was just an opossum or raccoon. Nothing to worry about,” her voice wavered.

Slowly she turned and craned her neck to peer toward the back of the wagon, now dark.

*"*Elluin? Ellisar?*"*

Silence.

Mrs. Aldren unhooked the final lantern from the front of the wagon and hurried toward the rear. She ignited its wick with a small expenditure of pneuma,its dim light barely cutting through the gloom.  

Selphie followed, her heart pounding along with her feet. “What is it? Where’s Daddy and Ellis?”

“I-I don’t know.” Her mother spun in frantic circles, the lantern swinging wildly in her grip and casting dizzying shadows across the road. “It’s not possible. They were right here.” Selphie could hear the panic edging into her mother’s voice as she shouted, “Elluin! Ellisar! If this is another game, I’m not playing!” 

Selphie swallowed the lump in her throat and joined in with a cracking voice,, “Daddy! Ellis!” 

The forest swallowed their cries. Not even their echoes answered.

Mrs. Aldren marched to the back of the wagon and wrenched open the storage compartment. The lantern rattled in her grasp as she reached inside, fingers closing around the worn hilt of a short sword. She drew it from its scabbard with a hasty, unavoidable scrape of steel.

“Next to me, Selphie. Stay close.”

Selphie obeyed and pressed against her mother’s lantern-bearing side. Breath came in shallow gulps for each of them, pronounced as Selphie asked,. “What are we going to do? We have to go find them.”

Her mother’s grip tightened bone-white around the sword. Her gaze fixated on the towering trees beyond Selphie.. “Listen to me.” Mrs. Aldren swallowed hard, her voice shaky as she urged, “Some–something is out there. Whatever it is-it *took* your father and brother without a sound.”

The trees loomed as something in the darkness watched.

“We need to get help,” she whispered.

The horses shrieked in terror. Their bodies tensed, muscles twitching as though sensing an unseen predator. Mrs. Aldren and Selphie rushed to their side, murmuring frantic but soothing words, but the animals would not be calmed. They thrashed against their harnesses, hooves striking the dirt with frantic desperation.

The wagon lurched violently. The already weakened axle gave a final, splintering crack. Wood groaned in protest as the entire frame buckled and sent the wagon crashing down in a cloud of dust and debris. The tongue holding the horses snapped. Freed from their restraints, the terrified animals bolted into the night.

Selphie barely had time to cry out before one of the stampeding beasts slammed into her. The impact sent her sprawling onto the dirt road, breath knocked from her lungs. She coughed against the dust swirling around her.

She watched dazed as the horses’ silhouettes vanished into the darkness, hearing their galloping long after their shapes turned to shadow.

When they were gone, Selphie was left with just the sound of a pounding heart in her ears..

Selphie pressed herself up from the dirt and called out for her mother.

Mrs. Aldren didn’t answer at first. She stood rigid, her gaze locked on the blackened trees.

Finally, she swallowed and forced a steadying breath.

“We just need to get to Serinvale,” she said, her voice measured but tight. “We can’t be far. We just have to keep moving.”

She turned to Selphie, kneeling beside her, setting the lantern carefully on the ground. The flickering light cast long, jagged shadows across the dirt road. She placed a comforting hand on her daughter’s back, rubbing slow, deliberate circles.

The two sat there, breathing, trying to calm each other.

And then—

A voice.

Soft. Barely above a whisper.

“You’re lost, aren’t you?”

The words drifted from the treeline, curling like mist in the cold air.

Mrs. Aldren’s breath hitched. Her grip on Selphie tightened painfully.

Silence.

The trees stood motionless. The lantern’s weak glow couldn’t reach beyond the road.

Mrs. Aldren lifted her blade toward the darkness.

“Who’s there?” Her voice was firm, but Selphie heard the unease beneath it.

No response.

Selphie swallowed, rubbing at her arms, suddenly very cold.

“Mama… who are you talking to?”

Her mother didn’t answer. Didn’t even glance at her.

Selphie shivered. The forest felt closer now. The silence between the trees wasn’t empty—it was listening.

Then, in the distance—

At first, just the faintest creak of wood and metal. Then the steady rhythm of hooves against the dirt.

A carriage.

Selphie twisted toward the sound, her pulse leaping.

“Mama! Someone’s coming!”

She turned, expecting relief in her mother’s face. Instead, she found her standing rigid, blade lowered, head tilted slightly toward the woods. Listening. Her breath caught.

She took a sudden, staggering step forward, “Ellisar.”

Selphie blinked, heart pounding. “What?”

Mrs. Aldren’s grip on her sword loosened. Her expression twisted with something fragile.

“I can hear him.” She turned wild, desperate eyes toward Selphie. “Ellisar’s out there. He’s crying. He’s calling for me.”

Selphie shook her head. “Mama, there’s no one—”

“He’s hurt.” Mrs. Aldren's voice was raw. She turned toward the treeline, taking another step.

Selphie lunged forward, grabbing her sleeve.

“Mama, look!”

Down the road, around the bend—a faint orange glow flickered. The light was growing closer.

Mrs. Aldren barely glanced at it. Her gaze remained fixed on the trees, as though something unseen had already wrapped its fingers around her mind.

Selphie’s fingers tightened around her sleeve. “Someone’s coming! We can get help!”

A moment of hesitation before her mother turned to her with a look Selphie had never seen before - something between love and regret.

“You stay here. Stay in the light.”

Selphie couldn’t stop her. Her mother disappeared into the dark.

Two raven-haired horses came to a slow, deliberate halt before a now solitary Selphie. Their breath steamed in the cold night air, whinnying gently as they pawed at the dirt. The lantern’s glow flickered against the lacquered black and gold trim of the carriage—its pristine elegance standing in eerie contrast to the ruin of the broken wagon and the oppressive dark of the forest beyond.

The coachman remained unseen, a shadow perched in the driver’s seat, silent and unmoving. Selphie’s heart pounded in her chest. The door creaked open with an unnatural slowness, revealing a man stepping gracefully from the carriage.

He was clad in fine crimson leathers, a gentleman in dark elegance. His red hair framed cerulean eyes that shimmered like captured starlight. There was something unnatural about their glow, yet she found herself at ease in his gaze as though he had lifted all her cares off her shoulders himself.

“Little one, you’re trembling,” His voice was rich and warm, like honeyed wine. He took a measured look around the wreckage before his lips pressed into a somber line. “Haelaxa have mercy. What happened here?”

Selphie, frantic and shivering, poured out her story—the broken wagon, the sudden disappearances, the terrible silence that followed. The man listened with quiet intensity, nodding in sympathy, his expression composed, unreadable..

She waved toward the looming trees. “Something is out there! It–it took my family; we have to find them!”

She took a step toward him. He took a step toward her 

With careful, deliberate gentleness, he placed a gloved hand on her shoulder. His touch was cool, yet strangely grounding.

“No child should be left alone in the dark.”

Selphie swallowed hard. “My family—they’ve been…”

“I know.” His gaze flickered toward the treeline, his expression shadowed. His voice grew lower, edged with something almost mournful. “The woods are no place for a lost child. The fae are… unpredictable creatures. I fear what may have happened to your family.”

Selphie’s throat tightened. “But they could still be out th—”

“If your mother had called for you,” he asked, tilting his head ever so slightly, “would you have followed?”

The question caught her off guard. She stared at him, then at her own trembling hands. “I—I don’t know. I guess not.”

The man turned away, his crimson cloak swirling about his legs before falling still.

“Good girl.” His approval was quiet, measured. “That’s how they get you, you know. The fae. They play tricks on you—the voices, the cries.” His tone turned almost admiring, as if appreciating the skill behind the deception. “Quite convincing, I must confess. But you mustn’t follow them… or you may never be seen again.”

Selphie shuddered and stepped closer. “But you—you look like a man who can handle the fae.” Her words came fast, desperate. “Otherwise, you wouldn’t be out here at this hour. Please—I can help. We have to find them.”

The man smiled. A crooked, knowing smile.

“’Tis true that the lion shan’t fear the wolf.” He exhaled, shaking his head. “But that does not mean one rushes into dangerous territory under nightfall.”

He took another slow look around before clapping his hands once. The sharp sound made Selphie jump.

“Now, I don’t do this for every stranger I meet, but I’d like to offer you shelter.” He gestured toward the open carriage, its warm lantern light spilling out like an invitation. “I have a place nearby. Safe from whatever is out here.”

His voice dropped to a soothing cadence. “You look tired. Cold. Hungry. A warm meal. A soft bed. At first light, we’ll find your family. I promise.”

Selphie wavered. Every aching muscle in her body screamed for warmth. But guilt gnawed at her ribs.

“No, I’m sorry.” She shook her head. “I need to stay. I need to find my mother.”

The man sighed. Not in irritation, but in disappointment. He lowered his gaze, his voice heavy.

“You need warmth. You need safety. You need to live.”

He stepped closer.

Selphie didn’t pull away.

He lifted her chin with the barest touch of his gloved finger, guiding her gaze back to his. His eyes, glowing in the lantern light, felt impossibly deep.

“Your mother would have wanted that for you.”

Selphie’s breath hitched. Tears welled in her eyes, “She’s out there. I can’t just…” The thought crumbled before it could fully form.

The man nodded, “I know. You’ll be reunited with your family. You have my word.”

He reached out his hand.

“Come, child. ‘Tis time to leave this place.”

Selphie hesitated only a moment. Then, sniffling, she wiped her tears and took his hand. 

The carriage lurched forward, its wheels creaking over the uneven road. The shadowed coachman snapped the reins with practiced precision, and the raven-black horses obeyed, their hooves cutting through the silence of the night. As the road twisted through the dense forest, the lantern light inside the carriage flickered, casting shifting shadows along the crimson velvet interior.

Selphie sat rigid, her hands clenched tightly in her lap. The warmth of the carriage should have been comforting, but instead, it felt heavy—thick with something unspoken, something unseen. The walls felt too close, the air too still.

Opposite her, the red-haired man watched her with the same unreadable expression, his cerulean eyes gleaming unnaturally in the dim light. He smiled, the curve of his lips practiced, careful.

Then, in a voice smooth as silk, he asked, "Tell me, child. Do you know why wolves do not attack the lion?"

Selphie blinked, caught off guard by the strange question. She glanced down at her trembling hands, unable to shake the feeling that something about him had changed. He felt different now.

"No," she admitted softly.

The man leaned forward, his elbows resting on his knees, fingers loosely interlocked. His voice was gentle, coaxing. "Because they know, instinctively, that to do so would be futile. The lion is beyond them—stronger, swifter, inevitable."

His smile widened, though his eyes remained sharp, focused entirely on her.

"It is a lesson, my dear. A truth woven into the very fabric of this world. The weak will always fall to the strong. The lesser will always be consumed by the greater."

Selphie swallowed, a cold dread crawling up her spine. The carriage rocked gently, but the stillness inside felt suffocating.

"I-I don’t understand." Her voice was barely above a whisper.

The man sighed, almost sympathetically. He tilted his head, studying her as one might examine a delicate piece of art. "No, most don’t. Not until the moment comes."

His gloved hand rose, fingers curling under his chin in thought. Then, his voice dropped to a whisper, so soft she barely caught it.

"Tell me, Selphie… do you know what you are?"

She stared at him, confusion flickering across her face. "I—I’m just a girl."

His smile returned, but this time, there was something behind it—something ancient, something knowing.

"No, child. The body is merely a vessel." His fingers traced absently along his wrist as though recalling something distant, something long forgotten. "What truly matters… what has value… is what lies within."

Selphie’s breath caught in her throat. The lanternlight flickered—and for the first time, she noticed that the shadows weren’t moving as they should. They curled at the edges of the cabin, deepening, thickening, swallowing the dim glow.

Then, the man’s cerulean eyes darkened.

Not dimmed, not dulled—but blackened entirely.

A void where his gaze had once been.

Selphie opened her mouth to scream.

But before the sound could leave her lips, the lantern sputtered out as the darkness consumed her
