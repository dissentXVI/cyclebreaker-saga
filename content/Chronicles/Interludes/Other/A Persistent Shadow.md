# A Persistent Shadow

*Goblin priest Aag Gax broods over his wounded followers after a failed ritual. Kyrichos — the robed figure — haunts his shadow and refuses to simply solve his problems, insisting Aag Gax learn to make the necessary sacrifices himself.*

---

Aag Gax sat his chin on his fist and stared out over the camp. His perch on the ridge overlooking his fellow goblins was small, well suited for the priests ruminating; its size didn’t stop the robed figure from joining. The goblin’s ears twitched with the subtle crackle of pneuma being worked to his side. 

	“You’re wasting your time, Kyrichos,” Aag Gax grumbled. Screeches of birds wheeling overhead filled the sky above. Whether they were waiting for scraps or for his wounded to perish, the priest didn’t know. 

	“You’re wasting theirs,” Kyrichos answered calmly. It was effortless the way he cut so cruelly to the point. Aag Gax looked out over the wounded; they numbered even among those that could walk, tending to those that could not.

	Aag Gax could see images of the supply caches dance distractingly before him. “...You could solve all of this–or help, at least. You’re just haunting my shadow, but you have power. You know things–so many things.”

	The masked figure floated effortlessly, unchanging. 

	“Why are you holding back? My people are dying every second you do. Look at them–we spent moons accumulating breathstone, and now we’re limping along. The ritual did not work; we tried using the tower, we–”

	“Did I come to you promising to pull you  by mine own hand from the hole in which you find yourself? Did I ever suggest such was ever on offer–or even possible? I am offering the tools of your own salvation,” Kyrichos rumbled low, annoyed.

	“Tools that did not work!” Aag Gax protested as his hand slapped his perch and he pushed up to stand. “The tower was untappable; the rest of the breathstone? Not enough. We don’t have time to–”
	“To steal and scavenge enough breathstone, both to make up for what you lost,” Kyrichos seemed faintly amused, causing Aag Gax’s ears to twitch again. Anger bristled like the charge before a storm as Kyrichos continued, “And to make up for what you need. Already the regional powers have mobilized against you. Already you have the answer to your question, the solution to your problem, but you turn away from it as if there were any other choice.” 

	Aag Gax’s chest was tight, his mouth dry. Below the two, below the priest and the temptor milled those of his people that had chosen to follow, to serve the greater good of their fellow goblins. They had good hearts; they were willing to die to save any of their fellows. But, Aag Gax thought, “Not like that.”

	The robed man’s voice remained cold, logical. “Hired swords swarm these hills–better weapons and arms to swing them than your people. Guards protect their haul of breathstone. Axes close in from the north, and rest assured, they have lethal aim. You either learn how to sacrifice, or…”

	“I know! I know,” Aag Gax grumbled irritably, never letting his head turn from the valley before him–though he sorely wished to wash this stranger in a baleful look. As stormy an eye as the old priest had, there were not winds strong enough to buffet Kyrichos.
