# Strangers in a Strange Land

*Serena and Ignia lead a small Vociran strike force across the blighted wasteland of the former Mare Sticaea, hunting evidence of a new Exemplar ritual. Crusty the goblin tries to warn them. Things get complicated.*

---

Blighted earth and crumbling foundations had taken over the landscape of Halinorum, a dark turn from the prairie grasses they’d started in and the pleasant woodlands they recently left. Honestly the trip had stopped being delightful the second they’d found the dried up old Mare; Gallus had gone into a mood, and Recius had always been sour even before his daughter turned coat to traitor. The troops brought with them were *fine*, but they had been chosen for talent before charisma. Ignia was having more luck there, but neither Serena’s prowess nor her scars engendered much love.

	So the Tribunis Sicaria was glad when they finally got to kill something.

	Honestly, Serena reflected, it hadn’t hurt anybody’s mood once they had collected some local costumes from the dead of Halinorum. Iggy always liked to smash things and finally they could stop their slow crawl by night and travel a little more openly. Nobody was going into these sprawling villages to be sure–none of the troops spoke the tongue well enough anyway–but it was nice to travel by daylight, especially once the ground turned treacherous.

	“Mare Sticaea, the locals call it. Spooky,” Ignia claimed as she clasped her great hands over Serena’s shoulders. The shorter sister turned her head and caught the massive one’s grin and wrinkling nose. “Awfully dry for a sea.”

	“They drained it all, sweetling. You haven’t heard a word the old man’s said, have you?”

	Ignia seemed legitimately surprised. “That’s here? That? It’s huge!”

	Serena answered with a snort. “They tried to break the world in two; a little puddle wouldn’t be enough to slake their Exemplar’s thirst, would it?”

	“How should I know how thirsty a god is? Seems short-sighted, sacrificing their own waters for that. Didn’t even work,” Ignia noted, releasing Serena’s shoulders and pacing over the barren, treacherous ground. Ahead of them their men worked in small groups, fanning out and testing the area ahead. They were on the hunt for *proof*, and it wouldn’t do to break an ankle (or worse) on the road.

	“Because they don’t sacrifice water, Tribunis,” came the Engineer Prime’s smug voice, never losing its air of superiority even as he waxed into a frightening lecture. “They sacrifice their little magic rocks. They rip the life out of the land around them. They break the natural order, just to force abominations into life. Demons travel these lands, bartering madmen’s loot and witches’ trinkets; fires and storms spring into being at sorcerous whim; and despite all these heresies, despite the tricks and conjurings and shortcuts, it’s not enough. They turn to their religions to invent new horrors, preferring to rip this world apart than face their own weaknesses. This scar you see, ladies, is the birth scar of an aborted Exemplar. It works, by the Emperor’s own word.”

	Even Ignia didn’t have anything smart to say to that. Serena cleared her throat.

	“On all of your travels and time together,” the Sicaria began, “Did he ever say how he stopped it?”

	Recius shook his head no. “Do the calculus, Tribunis. Your father has never run from a fight–but he has delayed them.”
	“Fight on your terms, fight a fight you can win,” Ignia supplied at once, recognizing the lesson of war, “So he pushed the fight, obviously. So he thinks he’s got a counter.”

	Serena’s mind raced–if the Empire had anything in its arsenal to stop a world-ending blasphemy, she didn’t know it, but in fairness, who would? The Emperor of course–that almost went without thought. The prince? Doubtful–but her father would be a contender over even the Emperor’s own son. Recius seemed apparently likely, and that thought rankled. How could *he* know but neither her nor Ignia? For his part, if Recius knew, the Engineer Prime did not reveal his hand. She reached out to lay a hand on Ignia’s arm. Her sister had thought well, but she thought aloud, and now Recius knew the sisters knew nothing about the plan.

	Through the crevices and trenches of the wasted lakebed crept the little goblin, angling for a better vantage on the humans talking before him. They had been creeping over the terrain, careful with their long sticks to poke out any treacherous ground, but they hadn’t caught him. If they realized they’d been on the verge of finding the other goblins, they hadn’t really given that away. Treasure seekers hadn’t always been the nicest people, but since Laconia had turned to his friends for leadership, diplomacy had been made more viable. Crusty had to try.

	“Don’t make alarm!” he called from his hiding place, not yet revealing himself fully–just a raise of his little hands to show they were empty. His common had never been his best skill, but they had to be warned. Besides, he’d been improving! “Danger forward!”

	He had to forgive them the thrown dagger. It stuck in the dirt right in front of his head, sunk in the muck of the lip of the trench so forcefully he could see the tip emerge an inch from his face.

	“No alarm! Friend! No weapon,” he claimed, having already unclipped his blades from his belt. He ducked back down and made a show of dropping them flat on the ground in front of his trench. No second dagger came. He could hear them whisper amongst themselves, though, distant though they were. Not the common tongue of Halinor. Crusty’s heart sank.

	The call they made was soft, at least, lacking the edge of intended violence. It wasn’t until another voice called out in the common tongue, accented in a strange way, that Crusty allowed himself to peek over the lip of the trench. He survived, so far.

	“You alarmed us, stranger,” called the man. Crusty peeked; he had grey creeping into his hairline, a clean-shaven face. Despite the note of truce in his tone, Crusty had heard the steel of war in his own people before.

	“I come make warn, not alarm,” Crusty protested, still ducking behind the trench. “You am find trouble; bad goblin ahead. Not bad, but make trouble goblin.”

	They shared more words in their own tongue before their leader spoke up again, “I don’t think you’re trying to hurt us; you’ve given up the element of surprise.” It took concentration to follow his words, but Crusty was far better at parsing Common than he was speaking it. The older man continued, “We would apologize. Please share a meal and tell us your warning. We won’t kill you.”

	So be it. These men were well-armed; his worry was not for himself, but the paragon-twisted goblins ahead. His worry was for his son. So long lost and so recently found, Crusty had to figure out how to–another time. For now, he stepped carefully up, pushing himself over the lip of the trench and froze. One of them, the scar-faced woman, had crept up to him. She reached down, but only to offer a hand. Crusty took it and pulled himself up. When she lifted him, there was a faint grimace of effort as the dusky-skinned woman hauled him up. She noticed him notice the elongation of her canines.

	Her grip did not falter, try as he did to escape.

	“Why do we need so many?” grunted Ignia as they held down the angry litter of green limbs. This one had a small lightning charm and a clutch of glowing crystals he’d been guarding like a nest of eggs. They were small; they were squirmy. Worst of all, they fought without an ounce of self-preservation, and while Ignia’s armor was great, statistics demanded at least a few bites would make it through.

	Serena knew her sister could flex and break the neck of the one she held, no issue. But their mission was more delicate–and far more annoying–than wiping out the swarms of the guilty littered across Halinorum. When they captured the first ‘goblin’, Gallus confirmed they were indeed after the ones responsible for the most recent exemplar, and he’d surprised the lot by telling them that there were more recent disasters than Kris’narax. More surprising had been the fact that they were taking the responsible parties alive, them and the means of their rituals. 

	This was no strike force, Serena had learned, but a mission of prolonged and exacting precision–dispersal of justice, yes, but more importantly a proof of concept come straight from the Emperor and his top advisors. Neutralizing Halinorum’s dirtiest trick would be the prelude to Pax Vocirum, built atop the ruins of world-ending superstition. A sharp crack of a smile spread as the assassin slapped chains on the wrist of the goblin now passed out in Ignia’s grip. Just a few weeks and two more trips out into the wilds of this continent…

	One down; two to go.
