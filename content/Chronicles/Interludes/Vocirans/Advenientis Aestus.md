# Advenientis Aestus

*Ignia, Serena, and their father Legatus Gallus gen Quietus sail toward the eastern colonies. A pre-campaign scene establishing the Vociran mission to neutralize the Halinorum Exemplar threat — and what Gallus saw at Sticaea.*

---

At the prow of the ship Ignia gradually warmed from the not-so-distant memory of the frozen shores of home. Mare Ostrum was not known to be, well, very calm, but Ignia stood like a pale boulder against the uninhibited battering of the wind, the merciless tossing of the waves. Even against a ship like this one the waters were having an effect–but then again, the soldier reckoned, the water should not be able to hold up so much metal. Like her own self, the water was strong. 

	The pensive nature of her expression turned to a smile and the heavy rest of her broad hands relaxed when she heard shouted behind, “Iggy! Come away from there; if you fall, we don’t have enough men to haul you back up!”

	She turned to spy her sister, the two all grins. Serena’s was less pronounced–all that burn scarring made it tough for her dark cheeks to flex happily–but it was a familiar smile all the same. Ignia called, “We’re almost there! I see it on the horizon.”

	She turned back toward the southeast where–if one squinted–the hazy start of the eastern colonies started. Their father told them this was a land of tall grasses, plains of salt, and heretics aplenty. None of that scared Ignia.

	Serena looked out over the choppy silver waters, squinting, smirking. Funnily enough she held back the snark and nudged the marble giant next to her with her elbow. Barely anyone could make Serena tes Paulus feel small, but the Scourge of Temples next to her… “Don’t fall. You can’t swim and I can’t carry your gear off this ship even if you could.”

	They shared a brief snort of laughter. Colonia Diadum was beyond massive, and stretching out further than that was *the* existential threat. It had sent their father across a continent and an ocean and now back–

	“Tribinari!” came the sound of a call and a muffled salute behind the two sisters. They turned to find some messenger snuck up behind them–in the squall of the Mare, they had not heard his approach nor his salute. When he looked back up from his respects, he apologized to the soldiers–one pale, one dark, neither less bonded to the other for lack of blood, “Titles, my apology, but I knew not how to–”

	“You would threaten the vulnerable secrecy of this plan–the emperor’s own approved plan–with careless titles?” Serena asked dangerously, coldly, as she stared down at the man. His stammering platitudes were cut short by the faintest smirk, a relaxation of the micro-signs of aggression in her frame. “Calm yourself, man. If your crew couldn’t tell who we were before now, they’re not worth the herring you feed them.”

	The stiffness of the messenger amused her sister, but Ignia couldn’t help but feel for the flustered little thing. Still, she wasn’t going to ruin Serena’s fun. She goaded, “It’s fine. Call me by my name–most men wouldn’t live past the disrespect.”

	Coming from the rumbling mass of muscle in front of him, the danger of bypassing the decorated soldier’s titles versus the danger of disobeying her direct order seemed to make the messenger dissemble. He groped for some middle ground, but they did not pick shipmates for their cleverness, it seemed. “Ladies,” he pleaded.

	They answered with a guffaw and a snicker, bade him share his message, then lose himself from their sight.

	“My lad–tribuna–Your father, Gen–your father requests you join him and met Helv–”

	They had already walked past him before he could name the third Tribunis. He dare not breath. tes Metellus would squash you, but tes Paulus, he knew, could strike more deftly. Her poisons were probably more painful, too. When they had surely gone, when he was surely safe from revenge against an offense he was not sure he’d caused, he turned. The sooner these ranks got off the ship, the better.

* * * 

	“We can still hope it was death on both sides. The border patrol wouldn’t defect–”

	“The tesserari? No, I doubt it, not these. Still, it worries me what they ran into,” Quietus ruminated as he thumbed the shadow of hair growing on his chin. He spared a look toward the engineer and noted, “Not like a father, hoping for his child’s death.”

	Recius met Helvus, Engineer Prime, Tribunis Tertiari, and loyal Citizen, snorted. “I cannot claim a traitor. I have other children.”

	“None half so bright,” the Legatus noted as he turned back toward the map plastered on the wall. “Nor as fun. You should feel some remorse, Recius.”

	“Not all of us are so blessed a parent as yourself, Legatus,” was the dry remark that Serena and Ignia came into the room, to which the dark-skinned killer had to observe, “I think we’ve grown on him, Iggy.”

	“Tinker turned uncle; hug?” the giant woman offered Recius along with her grin. Her generosity was met with an unappreciative, dry face.

	It was the Legatus that brought order with a raise of his hand. “We’ll be docking in Fort Diadum soon. A few days at most–I want all bags to be ready for transport as soon as we touch down. We’ve got many miles ahead of us when we do and not much time.”

	“So why don’t we take an airship? Oh, right, ‘requisition unsuited’. Same as my other vehicular designs… and yet we march?” came Recius’s testy reply. 

	Quietus nodded. “And yet we march, we four and the fifteen soldiers come with us.”

	Ignia squinted, but this made some sense, even to her. “We join up with the cavalry in the south. Something already at Dagger Canyon?”

	Serena nodded in approval, and Quietus softly smiled. It touched his eyes, those little wrinkles showing. “Almost. We go east from Diadum, across salt and marsh and wood until we go forward no longer–then we go up.”

	“Up! Up he says, and not an airship in the supply!” griped the third Tribunis. His hands flung upward with some drama, leaden as they fell back to the table.

	“It’s good for a man to get a feel for the land,” the Legatus had already decided, that smile not fading despite met Helvus’s outburst. This was a private meeting, all three knew, and while he had earned their loyalty in public, more important still had he earned their trust in private. Gallus gen Quietus, Malleus Maleficarum, Hero of the East, Legatus, soldier, father, spoke further, “If we’re going to win, we’re going to have to fight as if more than our lives are at stake.”

	Serena volunteered what they were all surely thinking: “Your source shared some very, very bad news.”

	“Mm,” was the first and unindulgent answer the greying man gave. He was rarely so cagey. “Kyrichos was clear; the heretics in Halinorum are toying with dangerous forces again. What I saw at Sticaea, what they did there…”

	He had never quite said it all. Sure, Serena and Ignia both knew the tale–the witch-priest Velethoui, the murder of their countrymen to fuel the blood sacrifice to summon the Halinorum god, the disaster that had rocked the world. Gallus had been present long and ago, chasing Cassius in some foolhardy attempt at cavalry only to arrive too late–or too early, depending. The heretical invocation the Halinori people led was an aborted effort, their god terrible for all its brevity. The ensuing disaster had wrecked the battlelines on the eastern front–Emperor’s Fist, the whole world. Vociran forces had withdrawn to lick their wounds, fortify themselves, and only luck held that the heretics had gotten as bad as they gave. 

	The scene had changed Gallus. He’d always been driven, tough, loving–but Ignia did not remember seeing her father *worried*. It marked her back then, even as a teenaged, fresh-faced soldier–how eager she’d been to prove herself to the father that chose *her*!--that what had shaken him was something beyond meat, beyond stone walls, beyond even a nation. It was existential, inevitable. 

	And they were headed to face it at the source.
