# Pre-Cataclysm — Vociran Perspective

*Told by a Vociran soldier named Augustus, dying in a bunker after a betrayal. He recounts the secret mission under Legatus Cassius into Halinorum — and the nights he spent with Marius on the long march to the end of all things.*

---

If you only saw Marius you would understand why I went. I was already a lifetime away from home, but I’d started to acclimate to the dry heat of Colonia Diadum, and besides that had won much honor and renown, at least as far as a hastatus could claim. It would have been easy to remain with the Seventh Legion and hunt down the savages that would not bend–even if Legatus Cassius himself had asked me to go, it would have been easy to stay and tame the many wild places of this new land. The crags and crevices, the low brushy plains, the stretches of salt-rich rocky beaches–they swarmed with dangers and I had a fair hand for putting down danger. 

I could have stayed, made princeps in time; but *Marius *had answered the Legatus’s call. If he hadn’t, I wouldn’t be recalling this journey now as I am, dying in some foreign bunker. 

So we had marched. I followed in his footsteps all the way from the coast, across sandy fields that gave way to salt. Even the mountain’s foothills were loose, strange soil that favored our mission, as it let our footsteps fade to obscurity. We spent ages on this portion of the march, some few dozen men–boys–moving across foreign soil under equipped and undersupplied. I spent my days behind him in formation, each step inside the one he left behind and so for a time lengthening his mark on the world.

By night I followed him into his tent. 

We didn’t have much time before dawn each day. The Legatus pushed us like a man possessed. He had lost his sister at Dagger Canyon of course, and our people had bled for months after. I knew we could sit tight, keep what we had taken, but others had their doubts and dreaded further counteroffensive from the savages to the east. I had never known fear. Whether he pushed to quiet those doubts or steal decisive victory, I didn’t know. Great men led and we traveled in their wake.

So we had little time to ourselves–precious few moments as Augustus and Marius, just what part of the night we carved out for ourselves through hushed whispers inside each other's arms. In the morning light I memorized the hard lines of his shoulders and chest, the inked markers of time on his skin. The blocky Sixth Legion lay over his bicep; Mari’s heart held the symbol of our empire.

*If you don’t dress out*, came his voice, warmth like brandy, *You’ll have to march in your underclothes*.

I relished the playfulness in his voice, but he was right; the sun would be here any minute. I threw on my shirt, covering my own tattoos. All of us young soldiers were tattooed –numerical marks of the legions we served down the outside of one arm, family crests if you had one or hoped to establish it, comemmorative pieces of history over the back and shoulders. He had something else, something unique on the inside of his arm that his cloth manica covered while we marched. 

*What lie will you tell me tonight?* I asked as we scaled the twisted, narrow lines of rock up and over the vast mountains that kept us from our mission. I didn’t know what the locals call them, but I remember them as perils of toothy, sunblasted stone. The first week I’d been ashamed for the first time of not being able to read, so when the script on his bicep caught my eye (and he caught my eye being caught), I had played it off. Stupid. He’d laughed and I’d known at once that he knew that I didn’t know, and that was a blister I’d prodded in the back of my mind for two days. He’d only let me suffer a short while before he’d broken the ice.

*Do you remember your parents talking about the **Fati Imperium**? Our Father Meonus said, through fire nature is reborn whole. That is my belief, writ here on my arm. Like metal heated we are changed by our experiences. Don’t you remember being a boy, before all the drilling and this new world? Let me guess,* he had smiled, *you could see Zhikarr on a clear day. Your mother is stout, your father played rough, but he played. How do they feel about their firstborn being so far from home?*

*Did you get my enlistment papers or something?* It was shocking how close he’d been; over the following days we would grow like the crush I had. We spoke every night since, and I soon learned every night it was something different. 

* * *

One night after an exhausting march, he told me the tattoo said, *Either with shield or on shield. I told myself I could never go back any other way. You too, right? As much as you miss it, how could you? We’re not just saving ourselves now but the whole of Terra.*

* * *

Another night he added: *The stars incline us, they do not bind us. I don’t mean like these savages; I mean the circumstances of our birth. We are from nowhere, but we have crossed a world. I’ve traveled further than my village’s mayor ever has. We have nothing, *he had added sadly, *but I think we’ve lost so much too. I sometimes think…*

He had hushed. Our party kept guard of course and I’d thought he was listening for them. 

*I think we are lashed to the yoke of fate. We labor like the aurochs, stale air above, hard earth below. Only men like the Legatus are free, and even then…*

How I’d longed to have something witty or comforting to say, but he had been the thinker. I knew only the rote answer, *There are no gods or fates, only the works of men*, so I’d said it, but he hadn’t answered that and as another night communing had closed, I felt like I’d fallen short of devotion.

* * *

We’d covered more ground and started the descent. Forty-odd young men like me crept across the enemy’s land. We struck into the heart of Halonorum on a mission, but we had only known so much. Legatus Cassius had us on a secret mission and discussed it only with his Secundus. Cassius had led the Seventh to greatness and he weathered rebellion after rebellion. Maybe that’s what had been on Marius’s mind when he’d lied and said his arm’s tattoo read: *Let them hate so long as they fear.*

I had given up on guessing Marius’s game. I may not read, but there’s no way one arm can say so much. But we talked for hours every night based on his fancies. This last one we did not exhaust in one evening, either. As we marched in fluid ranks–in the approximation of ranks–as we climbed through rock; crept through hills; practically crawled through woods, these conversations gripped me. These savages through whose lands we snuck, whose lives we would liberate, they remained ignorant of the nights we shared.

*I hate to think Meonus’s line thinks so. Fear and hate brought low the people of this land, drew them to worship not themselves but distant gods. You served in the Seventh, Augie; you know some peregrini. Is it hopeless? Are we always going to hate each other?*

I’d had to teach a few of the local savages how we do things; no one is going to claim Augustus will ever make top marks at the scholarium, but I had been a marvel at laying caementicium–I’d worked concrete since I was ten until I went to Halonorum six years later. They had counted on me in Colonia Diadum to teach the more trainable savages, the peregrini, how to do so. 

*How could we?* I had answered. *We came here to save them, and we lifted them out of ignorance. Their roads will stand six wagons wide; their houses will last a century. Their gods will no longer rule them; they will–*

He hadn’t gone silent then; he’d reached out a hand, put it over my chest, and started it to race for the dozenth time. *I know what Cassius thinks. I want to know what you think, anaticula.* We had met at a spring outside the Colonia capital. Anaticulae were the little foreign birds that had not belonged in the water but dove in and thrived all the same. I had never lost the nickname, but I had found the affection in it.

He wanted to know what I thought, so for a long minute I thought. *Not this lifetime. The Sixth was… thorough. *I had been careful. How effective he and his legion had been in wiping out wave after wave of resistance… for some reason I had always gotten the feeling he’d felt more shame than valor. When the Seventh was called to action, we answered the threat of riots brewing in little farms. The Sixth had faced armies. *And we work them hard, I guess. I may get to go home someday, but I don’t think, you know, that they’ll call it home. Maybe their grandchildren. Until the natives no longer remember what came before Colonia Diadum, I think they will not love us.*

I’d felt him cry silently as we tried to sleep.

* * *

The mission was crucial to ending the war early and decisively. Cassius had a plan held close to his chest, something he spoke about only with his second in command. Secundus Persona–we didn’t know the man’s name or even what he looked like, so we named him after the mask he wore to hide his war wounds. Secrecy and speed were of utmost importance; Cassius and Persona hadn’t recruited even a full centurium; the Eighth numbered only about forty men. We were promised glory in return for our service; life everlasting in the annals of history.

And we had accepted; the leader of this new and free colony had picked us! So we had covered more leagues than what I thought possible, bereft of our regular equipment in return for haste. We marched, we scavenged, we slept. How many of our brothers had found more than marching in these past weeks, I would never know.

Every league and every conversation brings us here to the end of all things, bound and prone on the floor by Persona’s betrayal. I dream after the ambush, and my mind prefers revisiting the memories above rather than ruminating on Persona and his attack. I don’t know if he’s gotten to Cassius, but the sound of many voices tells me he trapped my brothers in arms with me in this dark, stone bunker. Beneath me, my mouth tastes dirt, stone, and iron. Mari sprawls before me on the ground, eyes locking on mine. I feel a flash of guilt; he will be the last thing I see, and my stupid brain is stuck still.

But so is his and I can feel the despair seep out of him; I can see blood leaking from his mouth and pooling in the grooves between the laid stones. When his eyes start to lose focus, I panic. “Mari,” I groan. “Stay awake. Look at me. Remember,” I plead, unable to stretch a hand out.

He meets me with his eyes.

“Remember,” I repeat, “When you told me you felt like an aurochs.”

It might be a poor choice of reference. Our hands were yoked, our bodies immovable. He groaned. They sacrifice their bulls in this land.

“You cursed the air and the earth; you lamented your lack of freedom,” I say, and honestly I start to worry I lost the thread. But he remembers; he listens. My voice cracks as I admit, “I still don’t know what your tattoo says; I just know each step with you has made me love the air above us, cherish the earth beneath our feet. You freed me and I didn’t know I had been shackled.”

The huffs he makes might be laughter. His swollen eye opens and waters. “I didn’t free you. I can’t even free me. Look at where we are.”

“No, it’s not ideal,” I admit. This is awful. Why couldn’t I have found these words weeks ago? “I wish… I wish it could have been back in the Colonia. Or back home.” The bonds behind my back are unshakeable. The sky above me is stale air trapped inside some dusty old structure; the earth beneath me is stone unyielding. I don’t know where we are or where I wish us to be, only any place but this. “I just know I never thought all the things we talked about before you. Meonus… he said better to die free than live a slave.”

“Better to live free,” he laughs dryly, but it’s not as bitter as he was a minute ago. “But I guess we might not get a choice. Still… I was struggling, and you–I saw clearly with you. I’m sorry,” he adds, and choked up as I am, I can’t imagine why. He reads my confusion in my silence and says, “The words. My tattoo, I never told you what it really says.”

Oh. I start to answer, to tell him it’s okay, that I get it–that I think I get it–but there’s a thousand things I want to say and he’s staring behind me, up and above, mouths the word Cassius. My head turns, scraping cheek against stone, and I see his boots first, the grim lines of his face next, and the knife in his hand last. 

Blessed Emperor, he found us before Persona! He steps forward and the croak of voices cascades, grateful to see him. He steps forward, surely to sever our bonds.
