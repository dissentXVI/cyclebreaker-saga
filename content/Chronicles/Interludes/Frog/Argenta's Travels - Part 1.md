# Argenta's Travels — Part 1

*Before Tadami's birth — Jewel, now Argenta, travels with Qamar and the Moonlit Revel. She confesses her past, deals with blackmailers in the night, and begins to reckon with who she was as Quiss.*

---

“Argenta…. Argenta!” called Qamar. 
 
The cloaked woman stirred from her troubled thoughts. The name Qamar had chosen for her 
was still new to her. The woman known as Quiss de Barghest was no more. For the first time 
since her teenage years, she felt wayward and listless, just as she was when she went by her 
birth name, Jewel Siltborn. This was the name she gave to Qamar. She was that scared girl 
again just having set out from home, but now she was going to be a mother. Her wanton 
lifestyle, passion, violence, and bravado as Quiss felt like a half-remembered dream from which 
she awoke from as Jewel again. 
 
Seeing that she had Jewel’s attention, Qamar motioned for Jewel to come to her side atop the 
hill while her followers filed by. Jewel ran her hand along her shaved novitiate scalp, her fingers 
expecting the texture and resistance of her thick wavy hair and feeling only the bristle. 
 
“You told me not to call you Jewel or Siltborn openly amongst the Moonlit Revel. So Argenta you 
will be,” Qamar patiently intoned when Jewel was close enough. “It is your name in the light of 
Petra.” 
 
“Sorry. I haven’t gotten used to it.” 
 
“In time. Look out there,” Qamar said, with a broad wave of her arm. Dusk was settling to the 
west, and down in the plains the desert met the grassy expanse, and in that field stood a dense 
settlement, a patch of small fires flickering to life amongst its shrouded shapes. 
 
“Darius’ Crossroads…,” Jewel said automatically. 
 
“Yes. We need to resupply. Petra has a shrine there. I know it is dangerous for you to show your 
face in public, but keep your hood close and stay near me. Petra has charged me with 
protecting you and I will. Your weapons and gear have been safely disposed of by my followers. 
You will not be able to defend yourself so readily should you be found but less identifying 
features is the safer bet, especially if what you say about your pursuers is true.” 
 
This was the first time Jewel had been in public in several weeks. She joined the train of 
celebrants with Qamar idling into the crossroads.  
 
Surrounding the town to its north and west, acres of shanties and rough tents had been set up 
with its settlers regularly moving in small groups off to the Sobekemsaf River to fetch water and 
clean sundries. The Moonlit Revel had to pass through this to reach the town. People 
bedraggled, in rags, hung outside these settlements caked by the dusty winds. Jewel had seen 
many of these faces before as if in a nightmare. It took her a moment to remember where: 
through the scope of her arbalest. Jewel clutched her robes tightly to her and close around her 
face. A torrent of emotion was tearing through her.  
 
A mother and young boy gently tugged her robes, their hands raised. “Please… Petra be 
blessed. Would you have some alms for us? My husband was badly beaten in the raid on the 
miners weeks back and cannot work till he recovers. We have had to sell everything to make 
ends meet.” 
 
A surge of nausea washed over Jewel, which she did everything to hold down. Her tears 
escaped instead. “Yes. Yes. Take my day rations. May Petra’s light shine upon you,” Jewel said, 
choking back sobs. 
 
In gracious approval, the parent and child set back to a fire in a small clearing surrounded by 
tents, where they set down on a log by a tent flap. A man’s feet hung out the opening.  
 
Once in the town proper, Jewel stayed with the line of parishioners passing through a 
marketplace where some of the Revel had broken off. Her foot crunched upon a washed out 
parchment torn in half with a rain-faded illustration. Jewel could not make it out. She kept with 
Qamar, however, who led her toward the town center just beyond the market, a crowded clinic 
stood next to the modest shrine with a guard who watched the door of the shrine. People on 
crutches, arms in slings, rounded the building. The jobs board was posted nearby and it was 
hard to distinguish those in the crowd for the clinic and those looking for work. Jewel’s nausea 
surged again. A memory flashed in her mind of Sharl taking her shopping for Tarwa’kem finery. 
Her guilt seeped bone deep and chilled her. 
 
Next to the jobs board stood a post with an illustrated parchment nailed to it. Two rough men 
stood by and observed it. “So the Cleavers’ bloody bitch flew the coop!” the first man spoke. 
 
“I’m not sure I would even keep her alive for the award if I got my hands around her neck,” the 
second replied. “It looks like I’ll have to return to those damned Kustara thugs if something 
doesn’t open up in the next couple days. Siren claim her! I hope she rots.” 
 
Jewel pulled the hood close around her features, keeping on Qamar’s heels. Qamar stopped 
before the guard, revealed her seal and handed him some coins from her satchel, and he 
stepped out of the way. She removed a necklace with an ornate key and unlocked the small 
door. A small alcove lit only by angular stone vents greeted them with Petra’s figure carved 
against the back wall. Qamar released a large purse from her back by buckle and handed it to 
Jewel. Qamar addressed Jewel: “Our alms supplement the Revel’s travel. Most of it comes from 
private donations, estates, and the parishioners, but a few times a year, we have to collect our 
alms. Much of this will be donated to midwives, clinics, and charities. Use that bag to get about 
half out of the collection box and take it to the clinic. Take it around the back so you’re not seen. 
I will dispense with the rest.”Jewel sighed easily, lifting the coins by the palmful into the purse. 
For the first time in weeks, a glimmer of satisfying warmth stirred within her.  
 
—————— 
 
Months had passed. The Revel had been making a counterclockwise circuit around the great 
Sticaean Lake, mostly keeping to wild areas except to occasionally distribute alms in small 
settlements. For Jewel, now feeling comfortable in her traveling cognomen, the time all passed 
as if in a beautiful daze of firelit dances and dream-visions of the child she would bring into the 
world. Petra was guiding this mother-to-be and had delivered her to safety. Jewel cradled her 
burgeoning bump. She had never been especially religious before Sharl swept her up in his 
fervor for Meninos, and even then she always felt more of an accessory to his rituals than an 
active dyed-in-the-wool devotee, as he and Chauni were, but now she felt a secure path, an 
easement from her tainted life by doing Petra’s work. Ever still, however, shadows of her past 
life haunted her conscience. Some dreams opened up to a wailing river of the dead that would 
pull her under if she attempted to ford it. The moon and a restful fire waited on the other side. 
Jewel would be pulled down by cold hands and awaken. 
 
At this point in the Moonlit Revel’s circuit, they skirted the Chillmire Bog and had passed the 
Birview Lumber Mill days ago on an alms mission, weeks away from Quickstall. The dark swept 
in from the dense forest, the last light a purpling shimmer atop the grassy plains. Jewel furtively 
glanced back. For days now three figures had followed the Revel from Birview, even fording the 
river. They kept to the tree line. It was not uncommon for the Revel to pick up stragglers or the 
destitute into its train until reaching the next town or nearest outpost, but these two kept their 
distance. Like a whisper from her last life, Jewel instinctively knew they were trouble, if not for 
her, at least the Revel. She ran her memory through everyone she had encountered at Birview 
for long glances, any moment of acknowledgment. Was it the suspicions of her mind producing 
the evidence or her memory? She suddenly pictured a group of three people who accepted 
alms from her who had lingered quizzically in their stares. Were they trying to place her? Her 
first thought was to stake out an ambush and capture, interrogate, then kill them. Only way to be 
sure. The thought came all so simple and automatic. But just as soon as the thought concluded 
she felt a pang for how a thought so rash and violent as this could have slipped into her mind 
now without skipping a beat.  
 
That night Jewel approached Qamar. “Something is on your mind. Speak, Argenta.” 
 
“Three people have followed us from the lumber mill. My worry is that they identified me at the 
mill or they have criminal aims. Probably see a bunch of women traveling with alms and 
ritualistically intoxicating themselves at night as easy prey,” Jewel replied and shortly thereafter 
winced for the admission. She paused. “I may have spoke rashly. I try to see the best in people 
now, but I fail and that old part of me sees the worst. I am worried for us and me.” 
 
“There is good in you. Petra sees it and so do I,” Qamar led on. She motioned for Jewel to 
follow her to a quiet place away from the other Revelers. “Do not be worried about the people 
following us. They may want to rob us, but if they do they must not know we are protected by a 
powerful spell of sanctuary. If they should get close with evil intent, nature itself will rise up to 
thwart them, and violently, as I have seen. Petra will preserve her followers, and the Revel 
especially.” 
 
“And what if they made out who I was?” Jewel asked. 
 
“Then I hope they do try to capture you and meet nature’s fury,” Qamar replied. “Consider the 
fact they are following us. They did not run off to reveal your location to collect the reward.” This 
did little to calm Jewel’s suspicions.  
 
Qamar continued, “When you joined us, Argenta, you only spoke briefly of the people after you 
by names, relationships and capabilities, the lengths they would go to find you. Besides what I 
have gathered from keeping open eyes and ears on our travels, Petra only showed me flashes 
of who you were, but perhaps you are finally ready to tell me about your past. Why is it your 
heart is still clouded? Though you now travel in Petra’s light, how was it you became the bloody 
Barghest?” 
 
Jewel softly settled onto a cushion, closed her eyes and inhaled deeply, then began: “There 
weren’t many of us when Sharl and I started off. We were simple soldiers of fortune aiming for 
just that: fortune, improving our livelihoods. Newly minted adults, but still kids, really. Fighting 
other warring people, hunting bounties. It was all above board. 
 
“But one day after a few years of this, Sharl took a job that called for a lone hunter to kill a 
notorious predator in the Fauconwood that had graduated to man-eating. He had some 
experience doing this from growing up next to the Gragatos Jungle. I thought the “lone hunter” 
stipulation was strange, but I knew if anyone could do it it was him. The hunt took over a week 
longer than expected. I waited at our group’s camp on the edge of the wood wracking my 
nerves in worry. When he did re-emerge, his appearance was shocking. He wore his jaguar skin 
around his waist with none of his gear or clothes on his chest. Sharl was painted in blood, 
animal blood it seemed, cleanly half of him from belly button to forehead, and all down the one 
arm; one red half, the other clean skin. I could immediately tell there was something different 
about him, his person, I mean, in his stature, his walk, how he looked at things: like he was 
above it all, not conceited, but on top, nothing was beyond him, top of the food chain, everything 
at his discretion or his appetites, and his charisma was enhanced by it. His presence had 
become magnetic. But that was not the only thing that came out of the murk of those woods. 
Chauni was with him, skulking closely behind. 
 
“Chauni Stag-Eye. Half-elf druid dressed in leather and animal furs, fashioned like a ceremonial 
warrior, the sign of Meninos stitched in red on her chest. Mousy bitch,” Jewel spat automatically. 
Qamar readjusted on her cushion. Jewel continued: “I knew immediately they had been 
together– her face impassive but for the way she goaded me with those big dark eyes of hers! 
But I was just so relieved to see Sharl alive that moment I rushed to him and threw myself onto 
his blood-painted body even. He held my chin up. For a moment his look was cold, appraising, 
then it flashed to warmth. His old self but different somehow, like what he presented to me was 
less a mask than merely a facet of him now. He said, “My Quiss, my Quiss…” as he streaked 
my face in the blood from his fingertips. Something changed within me, and between us, too, 
then, though I did not notice it at first. Less mutual love between us but more me happy to be 
loved, to be possessed, that my love could touch him at all. I was drifting into his thrall. Chauni’s 
eyes approved. 
 
“Sharl introduced me to Chauni. “This woman saved me in the Fauconwood. I would have bled 
out on the forest floor if not for her. My appearance now is part of her healing ritual. The job is 
done,” he said, kicking the blood-soaked sack of with the trophy for the bounty.”  
 
I tolerated her even as she insinuated herself between us. I could tell there was a side of him 
only they understood, a private shared comprehension. He only ever spoke of what happened 
to him in the Fauconwood vaguely when I pried: it sounded like a long fevered nightmare of 
violence, blood, fire, and shadows. Dream details mixed with the hunt. I would do anything for 
him and in time I pushed to understand that side of him he shared with Chauni. He was waiting 
for me to.” 
 
Jewel paused. “In this time, things changed for our mercenary band. At first in small ways. Small 
extra degrees of cruelty in Sharl’s actions that gradually grew into unchecked bloodlust. Others 
around him took on his qualities. He took a more active role in training, finding ways to bring out 
our best martial qualities; he made us harder, more ruthless, and we all took a peculiar pride in 
this growth. Egos flourished in this environment. A recipe for disaster, you would think, but Sharl 
knew exactly how to play people off each other, like he did me and Chauni for his affection, or 
find compromises to maintain team cohesion.” Jewel took another deep breath, “I was uneasy 
with the changes at first. The jobs we took had less scrupulous targets and with that real money 
flowed in. Our band grew. We became better equipped and seemed to draw in greater and more 
diversified talent as if by some hidden attraction. Our changed behavior became normalized, 
and the sense of common purpose was infectious. We were like a pack of wild dogs in a world 
of rabbits.” 
 
Qamar listened intently. Jewel trembled in anticipation for the next part and took a deep drink 
from her waterskin. “It was on one of these ‘less scrupulous’ missions I changed in no small 
way. I did something I thought I would never do.” 
 
Jewel stared into the distance for a long while. “What was it, Argenta?” Qamar broached. 
 
“I murdered… a civilian, a seemingly decent man… for a job,” Jewel cracked out. “It was a 
damned assassination. Political rival in Avosa Tesota…. I fought against it. He pressured. But 
ultimately, I wanted to… for Sharl. And it felt… good…. Sharl hung over my shoulder as I did it, 
encouraging me. His fingers tracing that mark… Meninos, in chalk on a rock, his other hand on 
my back. It was hard pulling the trigger, but after that it got easier. Everything did. Worse things, 
too. Unforgivable things. And yet… I believed I had found my purpose. A grand diva of the 
battlefield. The fashionable and deadly sniper, Quiss de Barghest.” 
 
Though Jewel spared Qamar the awful details, Qamar could sense their magnitude in her, how 
they weighed on her conscience. Qamar could see her contrition and felt deeply for her 
novitiate. The priestess knew that justice would have been to leave her to her fate, but she was 
Petra’s charge, and the goddess must have her reasons. Qamar wordlessly wrapped Jewel up 
in her arms and wept with her, until they parted to sleep. 
 
—————— 
 
Days passed. Jewel never eased from her anxiety of being followed. One night, she awoke to a 
chorus of feral howls that rung the camp. Newly settled into sleep from exhaustion, most 
Revelers stayed asleep, but Jewel strained to focus on the tree line where the howls emanated, 
almost as a moving cacophony that trailed off until it dissipated. She glanced behind her, yards 
back, and Qamar was also following the sounds. Her eyes met Jewel’s and gave her a 
reassuring smile.  
 
Daylight the next day, Qamar and a contingent of revelers accompanied Jewel to inspect the 
perimeter for what became of the would-be-intruders. The signs of struggle were vivid. Jewel 
could see the disturbances in the forest where creatures must have burst out of bushes and tree 
branches toward a common point. She followed the chaotic tracks dozens of yards until they 
terminated by a tree with patchily destroyed bark on one side. Bloody streaks and claw marks 
sketched the story on the tree and ground beneath. A trail of grue led off deeper into the forest, 
where the victims must have been dragged.  
 
“Whether it be wolves, bears, or wildcats, they cornered them here, slew them, and dragged the 
bodies off to feast on them,” Qamar stated. The evidence of fatality was convincing. Jewel was 
slightly relieved to see this. “Rest assured Petra has and will protect you.” 
 
More days passed uneventfully. Jewel no longer saw figures on the tree line keeping with them 
in the far distance. One night, the Moonlit Revel deep in a riot of drugged celebration, Jewel 
slipped off to relieve herself in the woods. She wandered out far, where the fires and noise 
became a murmur. The forest was peaceful. She knelt behind a mossy downed tree. The area 
was beautiful in the moonlight. She heard the susurrus of a forest stream and scanned to see a 
glimpse of the shuffling rapids sparkle deeper within. Shafts of lunar gloss beamed through the 
trees and pooled on the forest floor or on silent trunks. One broken trunk, cynosure in a clearing 
40 feet off, was wholly bathed in the moon’s shimmer. The vision of natural beauty was 
disrupted somehow, some unnatural element in its composition. It took Jewel seconds to realize 
a piece of parchment had been nailed to the trunk, lightly lifted with the early winter breeze.  
 
Jewel immediately stooped lower into cover and finished quickly. She needed to see what was 
written on that note. Precautions had to be taken, however. Jewel’s nerves spiraled in fear at the 
thought she was being watched, but her senses were heightened by it as she clung to the 
shadows and took the trunk from behind. She waited for a cloud to pass before the moon then 
sprung on the parchment, taking it, slithering back into the shaded forest and the safety of the 
encampment. Jewel swore she could hear taunting hisses of “Quissssss… Quissssss…” from 
somewhere hidden nearby. 
 
Once again among the rancor of the Revel, Jewel steeled herself to read the parchment. Her 
breath caught in her throat. It was bad news. 
 
“We know who you are. Consider it a great mercy we did not report who you were traveling with 
and under what disguise. We seek a greater prize. If you wish for your identity to remain a 
secret, you will bring the coin collection the Moonlit Revel travels with and dispenses for alms to 
the head of the forest stream in two days when the Revel nears it.” 
 
“But the bloody remains…?” Jewel puzzled to herself. “They must have been from just one of 
their party.” She folded the parchment away. Once her nerves calmed, she put together the 
particular logistics of their plan; her mind necessitated the raw arithmetic out of survival less 
than sang froid. “So they wouldn’t have reported it otherwise this plan for the coins would be 
pointless. If there are only the three following, and they did not know specifically I saw them 
following so that count is likely accurate, then there are likely two left after one died tripping the 
sanctuary protection. Did they not know? No, they all would have gotten caught. Not all of them 
knew then, and those that did needed to know the range of the ward. They needed to know to 
know where to put the note. I must have been observed for days for them to know how far off I 
might travel to relieve myself and when. I need to gather more information,” Jewel thought. Her 
mind was already outlining a plan and she was already revolted at the awful simplicity of her 
goal. The plotters would have to die. The only way to keep herself and her baby safe. 
 
Qamar watched Jewel struggle back to her bedroll and lay on her side. She could tell Jewel 
seemed deeply preoccupied. What had happened when she left to the woods for so long, 
Qamar wondered. Jewel was turning in and she would make a point to ask in the morning. 
 
Meanwhile, Jewel attempted to sleep on a growing nausea that would not find release. There 
would be no talking or safe exchange of goods. The men would have to die. She curled and 
lurched through restless sleep, a vivid dream played on repeat. Faces from the Birview Lumber 
Mill, bruised and dirtied by their lifestyle, looked up beseechingly to her. She would reach into 
her alms satchel, draw a knife from it, and run it along their pleading eyes. Blood ran down blind 
faces as they continued to beg. She would try to catch the blood and staunch it to no end, and 
then the dream would start again. It would not let her do it differently. 
 
The morning rays came as a relief on Jewel’s sweat-dappled brow. She wanted desperately to 
escape those dreams. Jewel shielded her eyes from the sun and saw the silhouette of Qamar 
standing in front of it. “Something happened to you in the forest last night, Argenta. Walk with 
me when you’re ready.” 
 
Jewel told her everything: the note, their likely plan, her dreams, and misgivings. Though the 
outlook looked just as dire to Jewel, she felt better having shared it. “I know you are loathe to do 
it,” Qamar said. “But as your protector I too believe these conspirators should be killed. It’s the 
only way to be sure.” Qamar encouraged: “We do not have much martial might among us, but 
we will support any way we can,” 
 
“I, I appreciate that but if possible I would still like to keep my circumstances concealed to the 
Revel at this moment. What’s more, if these people are to be killed… I must do it. I can’t live 
with another tainted for my sake,” Jewel replied. “I may need your help though…” 
 
That day and the next the two worked out a plan. Jewel would often back out at her revulsion to 
killing before logic and compunction would lead her back in with a bitter fervor, detailing the 
precise points. It would be simple. In the dark of the appointed night, Jewel would change into 
stealthy attire, and Qamar, being of similar height, would don Jewel’s robes, doing well to mask 
her face. Jewel would painstakingly take a long circuit around the drop off point at the head of 
the stream as a matter of reconnaissance on the extortionists. Qamar would proceed at the 
appointed time and deliver a large bag with only a small portion of the Revel’s currency. Once 
Qamar had departed, if Jewel did not spot them, she would wait for them to expose themselves 
when they went to retrieve the bag and dispose of them. Nothing she had not done before came 
the cold echo of her past life.  
 
Qamar voiced her objections to leaving a pregnant woman who was her sacred charge to 
protect to the killing of two, probably armed, rogues. “I can do more. You do not have to take this 
on by yourself. Let me cast the sanctuary spell on that area as soon as they reveal themselves.” 
 
“No! Only as a last resort,” Jewel replied. “If I scream. If it sounds like I’m in trouble, do it. Don’t 
forget…,” she paused. “Don’t forget, I was a notorious assassin not so long ago who could have 
made short work of a few ruffians.” 
 
Qamar conceded this point in speech, but privately would consult Petra whether to interfere. 
She was surprised when she felt the goddess’ assent to the plan settle in her mind as she 
dreamed. 
 
—————— 
 
On the day of the planned exchange, Jewel and Qamar proceeded with their day as if nothing 
had changed to not arouse suspicion. Upon nightfall, Jewel slipped into a tent with Qamar, 
donned black wrappings and was handed a dagger kept in her security. Qamar concurrently put 
on Jewel’s robes and exited the tent, being mindful to move as Jewel did. After Qamar had 
walked to where Jewel would regularly rest while she observed the Revel, Jewel slipped out the 
back at a crawl, silently inching unseen through the grass in the deep gloom, careful with her 
newly tumescent stomach. 
 
Jewel had no idea where the conspirators would be hiding if they were, but she knew they could 
not be close to the Revel itself. It took her 30 minutes of hard labor to crawl to a point of the tree 
line well beyond the limits of the Revel and feel safe enough to stand. She had a general 
understanding of where the stream ran but she took every care to move quiet and low from tree 
to tree, creeping in the shadows. It was hours work circumferencing the area to get behind the 
head of the stream. The stream burbled up from underground currents which emanated from 
the Chilneau Peaks. Most currents collected in the Chilmire Bog, but this one’s frothed forth out 
at a greater distance. She eventually heard the sound of waters in the east and made her 
soundless circuit to get north of it.  
 
Jewel fell into the rhythm and praxis of quiet infiltration easily again. She was only disquieted by 
this ease on a moment when she reflected on how recently she had done this exact behavior. 
The thought of the killing itself, however, caused more than disquiet. It shuddered her 
conscience, which she only tamped down by reminding herself of the necessity of her actions. 
“No traces. There can be no traces,” she repeated in her head. 
 
Jewel checked the position of the moon in the sky. The time of Qamar’s dropoff was coming in 
the next half hour. Jewel kept her geographic orientation to the sound of the stream keenly and 
began creeping southward. The heights of the trees were impossible to perceive and she could 
only discern the contents of their branches when directly underneath contrasted against the sky. 
Her eyes darted at shadows. Every movement of the forest was suspicious. The forest was 
silent but for the stream. Animal life would not peep for a human presence: the extortionists had 
to be here. In the distance she spied the head of the stream jetting forth from stones cracking 
through the mossy loam of the forest floor. Before it by 50 yards was a rocky mound shrouded in 
a copse and a thick low hanging tree. She focused on the bushy copse for a minute, the perfect 
location to spy on the delivery, and sure enough the bush lightly shuddered when there was no 
wind. She could faintly make out a large shadow within the bush. 
 
“That’s one,” Jewel thought. “But where is the other?” She saw there was only one planed way 
down the raised position and she moved at an inaudible crawl in complete shadow to a hidden 
ambush position at the foot of its incline from which she could still see much of the area. She 
squinted up at the stars from her hiding place, taking in the position of the near full moon. It was 
time.  
 
On cue, Qamar emerged from the darkness far to the south. The hood was pulled too low to see 
her face well and she carried a big canvas bag in front of her, looking warily around the forest. 
The bushes rustled at the top of the rocky mound. “Aye aye, there she is,” she could make out 
at a rasp coming from the top of the mound. Jewel did not move at all, her muscles primed. Her 
blood roiled in anticipation of the coming violence and wondered if her pounding heart could be 
heard.  
 
Qamar set the sack behind the stony spring and began retreating into the murky veil of the 
forest. Long minutes passed with no activity before the bushes rustled and a man began 
skulking down the backside of the mound. Jewel eyed him carefully, her self-loathing so strong it 
pinched her throat but she was determined to see this through. He was close enough she could 
barely make out his face: he was one of the ones she remembered. It vaguely disheartened her 
it was memory and not paranoid delusion that won out. He passed close enough to Jewel’s 
shadowy hollow she could touch him. His back was turned. She could see him thinking how he 
would collect the money safely in case he was attacked in the process. 
 
Jewel lunged forth, kicking out his knees and twisting his arm behind his back. She held the 
knife to his throat. He shuddered in surprise, as she gutturally whispered, “Where is your 
partner?” 
 
Something heavy landed on the forest floor with a ‘thwump’ from a neighboring tree. “Right 
behind you,” came a man’s voice from behind. Jewel was paralyzed for a moment. “What do 
you think I’m stupid?” the voice said, grunting from getting to his feet. “Dirty Dirk was dumb 
enough for the both of us breaching the sanctuary’s perimeter but that let us know its range. 
Dumb, but of—“ 
 
No time to think. Actions came immediately. Jewel planted the man she held down into a root 
hard with her knees and used the force of the impact to rebound hard in the opposite direction. 
The big man behind her was taken at an awkward footing and stumbled. Jewel maniacally 
stabbed him with a demon’s speed, clean arcs and straight jabs: ribs, ribs, neck, neck, face. 
She thought only of the knife fighting lessons Sharl had drilled her on; his voice in her ear again. 
By the time the big man had his bearings he was as good as dead, and looked up pitifully from 
his bloody wreck of a face at Jewel huffing above him with her dagger primed.  
 
By then, the other man had gained his footing again, stunned at the sudden turn of events. 
“Haelaxa’s twists! We never should have come. Sorry, Yeric.” He gave Jewel a wide berth and 
broke off in a stumbling sprint into the darkness between the trees away from her. 
 
Jewel’s focus was perfect in this moment. No thought perturbed her. The next man must now 
die. Her head followed his escape with a calm precision. She stood and pursued him in a low, 
even run, beelining after him.  
 
Qamar stayed hidden nearby. After minutes she heard a sharp cry from deeper in the woods, 
which was quickly muffled, and she proceeded in its direction. She found Jewel kneeling above 
the thief’s body, which bled out against a log, his face frozen in final fear. Qamar cautiously 
approached the prostrate woman. 
 
“Argenta… It’s okay. It’s me.” Qamar cautiously circumferenced Jewel at an arm’s length. The 
bloody dagger lay on the ground. The blood shone black in the moonlight. Jewel had a vacant 
stare, at the body, into it, beyond it, miles away. She seemed sad but otherwise calm. 
 
“I had to kill them. Knew who I was. Knew who I was. Imperiled me. My baby. My…baby…. 
Walked into a trap. Had to… had to act. The killing came so easy… so easy… natural, really. I 
thought I would revolt at the murder, but… I…” 
 
Qamar threw the knife deeper into the forest as she watched Jewel. 
 
Jewel buried her face in her hands. “I… I… I’m a killer.” 
 
“It’s okay, Argenta. Everything is okay,” Qamar gently knelt beside her, cradling her novitiate’s 
head in her hands. Qamar pulled Jewel’s hands down from her face and held them firmly 
between them, making eye contact. The priestess’ look was authoritative but nurturing. “To me, 
Petra has found a way to protect this mother, and child,” she said, placing all their hands on 
Jewel’s stomach.  
 
Single tears rolled down Jewel’s cheeks as she looked down to their hands. Jewel looked up 
again to Qamar with the same somber, certain expression, and spoke, “Yes. I can and will 
protect my baby. But this wretched thing I am… The things I see in my dreams… I’m not worthy. 
My child needs a better mother than a cursed killer. Can… can you find a home for her?” 
 
“So you have made up your mind, Argenta? Adoption?” Qamar asked, holding the young 
woman’s serious gaze.  
 
“… … yes…,” Jewel whimpered in reply. The admission hurt her. “With any luck, my child will 
have no share of my bloody life.”
