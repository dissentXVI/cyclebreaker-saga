# A Frog is Born

*The origin of Frog — formerly Jewel Siltborn, formerly Quiss de Barghest. Traveling with the Moonlit Revel as Argenta, she gives birth to Tadami and makes the agonizing decision to give her up. She arrives at a trapper's outpost and chooses her new name.*

---

Between the pressure and pain of the labor and the numbing needle Qamar kept injected in her 
lower back, Jewel pressed in waves through a twilight reality with singleminded dedication. The 
last thing she remembered before losing consciousness was the relief of delivery and cradling 
her baby on her chest, its gray eyes contrasting the slick of red hair and wailing mouth. 
“Argenta. It’s a girl,” she heard Qamar say. “A healthy girl. Petra be praised.” 
 
A chorus of croaking frogs amidst the patter of rain woke Jewel. The sound was overwhelming. 
Her memory hazily returned. The Revel hastened to Quagton when the contractions began. The 
curious looks of the halfling villagers, the creaking of the boardwalks, as she and a handful of 
Qamar’s most experienced midwives had rented a house on the outskirts of town. The day there 
she spent reclining on a dusty couch, the sound of Colwater Marsh ever present sloshing 
beneath her.  
 
She searched the room for her child. Of the two midwives still there, one rose cradling a basket 
with a swaddled infant inside. The woman eased the child into Jewel’s outstretched arms, who 
wrapped her in a gentle embrace. 
 
Her eyes welled and drops rolled down her cheeks. “Precious…. How could I give you up…? 
What kind of mother would I be to…” Her hug tightened. A flash burst through the boards. 
Thunder peeled outside. It summoned a memory of thunder, a village leader, his feet and hands 
bound to a post, blindfold lose around his neck, a burning house before him, his shouts drowned 
by the thunder. In the moments between strikes, a baby’s wails could now be heard from within 
the inferno. Quiss shuddered with the realization. She was careless, but it was too late.  
 
A voice came from behind Quiss. “You have outdone yourself this time, love. Our client will be 
pleased. There won’t be any more holdouts in the region.” Quiss slinked into Sharl’s embrace. 
She kept her cool. Owned it. She had done it after all. She smiled for her lover and meant it. Her 
last bastion of decency fell without a fight. 
 
Her baby’s cries shook Jewel from her reverie. Her once full heart curdled in revulsion. Qamar’s 
voice came from the doorway. “I am pleased to see you bonding with her. Are you still sure 
about delivering her into our orphanage?” 
 
Jewel was split in cold tremors. “Please take her. Ye–.” She choked on her words and 
swallowed. She shook as she handed the baby back to a midwife. “Yes. I am… unworthy. 
Unforgivable…!” Jewel clawed at her skin angrily before clutching her face, panting amidst the 
flue of tears. 
 
The room was silent for minutes except for the rain. Qamar ached for the young woman, and 
the baby. “If you are sure… at least you know where to find her, should you change your mind. 
Remember the name of the town? Laconia. We passed north of it a little over a month ago. We 
have an orphanage near there. Spirit guardians protect it. Your child will be safe. Raised well. 
This is my oath to you.” Qamar moved closer to Jewel’s bed flanked by her midwives. One still 
holding the baby. “Have you thought of a name?” 
 
The croaking symphony outside could be heard in a pause in the rain. “I have. An old family 
name. Tadami. Tadami Siltborn. Like my grandmother who looked after me after my mother left 
and when father was busy. She deserves this token of love. Sharl will not know it. I never told 
him.” 
 
“Tadami. It will be done, Argenta,” Qamar said. “We will give you a few days to recover before 
we depart. Our scattered agents have been gathering the parts of your gear we divided and hid 
in pieces upon your initiation in the Revel and should arrive in that time.” 
 
Jewel clinched her eyes so as not to see the child she would leave behind. Her own future 
would be a cold penance to the end of her days. 
 
Silent nights alone in the cabin with her daughter, the moon ribbed shadows above the brackish 
churn. It took a perverse resolve to only touch Tadami gently. Her breasts ached. A midwife told 
her it would pass with neglect. A wetnurse had been found a week back at her request. She 
could not bear to watch, roaming aimlessly throughout the creaky trade hub. The fog would roll 
in low off the lake and close around her legs, before rising around her. If not for the sounds of 
commercial life, the world would seem to have ended in a gauzing blankness. Blood came to 
nothing. Anguish choked on the tide. Jewel watched the rowed ships prow into the endless lake. 
The steady captains navigating the hulks crewed with burly men, like her, a mind for peace and 
absolution could only aim a body of violence into the wild, to crack the waves, to founder or find 
harbor. 
 
Time sifted from her grip, only a chain of Tadami’s cries pinned Jewel to this world. The day of 
their departure, Jewel sat on an embanked log watching tiny eddies stir beneath the gentle 
shore. Movement was barely visible from where she sat. Concentration revealed it was a clutch 
of tadpoles hatching from within sunken shrubbery, swimming dazedly in a dozen directions. 
She had come to this spot over the past days and had seen parent frogs watch over the eggs, 
but now she searched and could not find them. They had abandoned the eggs just as they 
hatched. Tadpoles would die. And tadpoles would live, grow, and learn to take care of 
themselves. It was the way of frogs and here they thrived. It gave her no comfort but a little faith 
to abide her turmoil. She followed the last tadpole to hatch. Its watery scamper ranged the 
protection of the shrubs till even the last stragglers had departed for other waters. In her heart 
Jewel wanted it to stay in the bramble despite the necessity of it branching out. Which it did, 
eventually, the runt cutting out into the open water disappearing beyond the waves and fog. It 
was the compromise, an uneasy peace, that settled on her. Her breasts began to ache again. 
She rose to leave.  
 
At the rental, Jewel encountered Qamar and a small party of the Revel who awaited her. They 
were packed for travel.  
 
“Here is your old gear,” Qamar gestured, as a Revel member came forward bearing in both 
arms a stuffed duffel which clamored before she set the sack at Jewel’s feet with a grunt. 
“Remember the orphanage near Laconia. We will keep records if Tadami is adopted. Do you 
want to see her one last time?” 
 
“I must,” Jewel resolved. Part of her, an essential pillar, was disappearing. A midwife with a 
papoose approached, a hood covering the baby’s head.  
 
Jewel lifted the hood. The baby was awake and alert and turned to look directly at Jewel. Their 
eyes met. Jewel touched her face gently. The baby raised her pudgy arm to grip the hand 
touching her, her eyes still locked with Jewel’s, almost intelligent in their circumspection of her 
mother. “Forgive me, Tadami. Your mother is a monster and now must live like one. You are less 
likely to be found in their care, and with me in exile…. But if ever I believe you are in danger, I 
will find you and lay down my life. But for now, when you need a mother most, I must become a 
stranger to you. Perhaps, I deserve it, but you don’t and never will. Goodbye, my little tadpole.” 
 
Tadami’s sharp eyes held her mother still. She did not cry, but she tried to keep the hand as its 
owner retracted it. Jewel stood and turned to Qamar. “With this, my old life is over. I am no 
longer Jewel or Quiss. I will forever be Argenta in the light of Petra. For everything, I thank you, 
holy mother. But out there, in the wilderness, I will be no greater than a beast. Perhaps, I will 
become someone new. Someone worthy of love. Or someone else altogether after isolation in 
nature with my grief has reshaped me. I do not know where I will settle. I may come back to 
Quagton occasionally for supplies. You may leave messages for me at the shrine.” 
 
Qamar exhaled, deflated. “Yes. Under your name, Argenta. Petra will see your child is raised in 
love and security. The boat you requested is leaving soon and will drop you off at a trappers’ 
camp on the northwest shore of the marsh after a couple days journey. You are a skilled ranger, 
but even you will need vigilance. I hear of worse things than beasts lurking in the depths of the 
Frostmarch Forest. Bundle up with that wool robe for the trip. Not least to hide yourself, but also 
because winter is coming soon.” 
 
The party of Revelers turned to depart, Tadami with them. Qamar was last to go. “May Petra’s 
light guide you back to your child. Farewell, Argenta.” The woman known as Jewel’s heart 
hollowed. She watched the Revelers round the waterfront and disappear into the fog near where 
the town meets the forest road and wondered if that was the last she would ever see of her 
daughter.  
 
The woman felt hopeless as she turned to the docks, wrapping the gray robe around herself. 
She picked up her gear. Another amphibious chorus began to crescendo and she sank into the 
white mist toward the sound of ships. A chamber deep within her had closed, and from it she 
began to cry. Without even knowing the feeling, she cried. Cried off and on for days, borne 
listlessly with the wind to brooding shores.

The trapper’s outpost bustled with life. Mud squelched and sunk into the crannies of latticed 
long planks that set upon the muck leading to small shacks, cabins, and the shore. Their groan 
was constant with the thud of leather boots. Chains clanked and fur rustled against tanned hide 
and rope. Voices broached the air between coughs and drunken doggerel. Scurrilous men and 
scabrous sounds dragged into a cacophony, to the moored boats and back. Blood, pine, the 
freshness of the sopping ground, and the stink of men and game mixed into a heady smell. Only 
one woman was in the whole settlement and she drew curious eyes and mutterings for those 
who spotted her figure beneath the heavy robes and hood. 
 
“Wat’s yer name, miss?” the grizzled camp supplier asked. 
 
The woman once known as Jewel did not know how to answer. Since the beginning of the trip, 
she thought only of herself as the discarded vestige of Tadami, where her heart lay. Her child’s 
opposite journey became irrevocable and ever-distant in her mind. She saw again the tadpole 
striking out into the waves and replied impulsively. The word escaped: “Frog.” 
 
“Frog? Yea, they’re all over. Point and yer’re liable to find one. But what’s yer name?” 
 
‘As good as any other,’ the woman thought in her low esteem. “No. You can call 
me Frog.” 
 
“Frog?? Yer a bit touched ain’t ya?” the supplier spit, clinking a brass jug on the ground. “But I 
‘spose you’d ‘ave to be to head off into those woods on yer lonesome.” In that moment, the 
shop boy came out of the back with a burlap sack. “Here’s the gear you requisitioned. Trap, 
rope, camping stuff, vittles, waterskin, sparker slate, winter layers. Won’t ye be needing a knife 
and bow for the huntin’, skinnin’, and dare say if Tyrachon be not too keen on ya’, protection?” 
 
“Already set on that,” Frog replied, tugging at the strap to the duffel on her back. Bits of her old 
gear and harnesses had been fashioned around her grey robe, the hood still high on her head. 
Frog set a stack of gold pieces on the ramshackle counter. The supplier inspected them to his 
satisfaction.  
 
“I’ve a good read on folk and can tell yer tougher than most of the boisterous lot here. Mebbe 
even martially. But jes’ one word of advice, miss,” the old mug intoned, leaning on the counter. 
“Iffin’ ye see movin' fires in those woods. Run or hide. Dark folk of a strange tongue gather in 
the deep. Pray ye don’t cross em.”  
 
Frog nodded but felt no mortal fear. Whether it was in a day or five decades, she believed only 
her death waited for her in the depths of the Frostmarch Forest. She hefted the burlap over her 
other shoulder and set off into the main trail due southwest. Few eyes in the village followed her 
course as she disappeared between the trees.
