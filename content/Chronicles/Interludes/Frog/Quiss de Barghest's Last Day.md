# Quiss de Barghest's Last Day

*The last job Quiss ran for Sharl van der Cleev and the Crimson Cleavers — breaking a miners' strike. Told from Quiss's perspective as sniper, this is the mission that finally broke something in her.*

---

Sharl released her from his kiss. 
“This is an important contract for us. I’m counting on you, Quiss.” His grip firmed on her 
shoulders. “My Quiss.”  
Quiss ’red lips uncurled. There was that possessive fire in his eyes again. Searing ice blue 
eyes, his face wreathed in a halo of flowing dark red hair. Quiss was bound by that fire, his 
insurmountable force of will. They were coming up from the gutter to the palace: once a ragged 
gang of sell swords, now a formidable mercenary regiment. There was no doubt. Power, 
prestige: it was all a question of when with him. She had never been anyone special. He made 
her believe, saw what she could be, what she deserved. He was her fire. She had never met a 
more dangerous duelist than Sharl van der Cleev, nor anyone more charismatic, or ambitious…. 
Quiss would do anything for him. The leaders of these striking miners were as good as dead. 
“Consider it done, Sharl,” Quiss spoke, smiling again. “Listen for my signal – or the shouts – at 
the height of the new moon.” 
He chuckled. “Quiss de Barghest. Show them why they call you Petra’s Shadow. The one who 
goes thunk in the night…” 
They paused, laughed, then Quiss replied. “That one will never catch on.” 
“Give them something to remember you by, Quiss. The survivors that is…” He showed his 
gleaming teeth. Quiss matched with her own savage smile. He pressed his hand down again on 
her shoulder. “Do not forget to form the mark of Meninos before you fire the first bolt. You have 
never failed me.” His fingers alighted then traced her cheek. “See you in two dawns.” 
His eyes stayed fixed on Quiss, but she could tell his mind was already turning elsewhere – to 
the future, his future, the map laid out behind him, the raid and scattering of the camp, the chest 
of Tarwa’kem gold waiting upon completion, purchased prestige, respect, dignity, the means to 
exact vengeances unforgotten, the family and world that spurned him.  
Quiss turned toward the door and found herself staring down the wide, dark, appraising eyes of 
Chauni Stag-Eye, mousy and pale, Sharl’s half-elf druid spy. Another starving whelp Sharl had 
collected, somewhere in the depths of the Fauconwood; the mission that changed him. He 
returned to Quiss and his handful of ruffians with her following behind. “The sneak,” Quiss 
thought to herself. She had skulked in without a sound, who knows how long ago. Quiss 
countered her cold glare with a gloating grin and sauntered out of the tent with a flourish, 
Chauni’s eyes following her.  
Sharl had been favoring Quiss more lately and Chauni knew it. He played his allies off each 
other as much as his enemies, how he maintained a tenuous order of egos and outsiders, 
managing to sharpen everyone to their destructive best, so long as they remained useful. He 
had a devilish knack for it.  
 
 
Quiss stepped into the night, almost unseen in her black leather getup and fur cloak. A nearby 
fire caught her face in its light, warming her cheeks against the chill of the desert night. She 
approached the fire to see some regulars milling about. Like most of the ranks, these were more 
D’yadyan refugees: ex-soldiers, hunters, guards, laborers, anyone looking for steady work after 
the Vociran invasion. There, half-cloaked in shadows flickering from the flame were the two 
resplendent elvish twins, Uriah and Ariel, leaning out of the opening fold of their tent. Once high 
society rich kids of Aqualonde with a sadistic streak a mile wide, now members of Sharl’s 
personal retinue and his de facto body guards, interrogators, and ambassadors. The two always 
fought side by side, more like dancers than hardened warriors, a gale of curved blades, 
throwing knives, and barbed whips. They brought the finest blades and fashions from their 
home, and in these things, they coordinated to complement the other. Sharl gave them the 
world as a playground after numerous scandals had them disowned and exiled.  
Uriah and Ariel, in one synchronous, bored motion acknowledged Quiss, then as one, spoke, 
“Don’t keep us waiting, Quissy,” before returning to their secret language of glances and 
gestures, between absently sharpening their blades. They were preparing for the raid, the 
agitation for violence curling inside them. Quiss moved on toward the camp center. She could 
hear Pierre Poinsard, the tall proud elf knight, marshalling the corps of armored and mounted 
regiments. A vainglorious and belligerent former captain of House Vaillant of Carinthia, ruthless 
in combat, yet moreso punctilious in candor and conduct. Pierre believed Carinthia should take 
a more offensive stance against Wodr’s Brood, and even to return Carinthia to its more 
expansive ancient martial presence in Halinor, a belief he attempted to prove one day manning 
the walls with his battalion when he spied a prowling dragon. Against the quizzical looks of his 
soldiers, Pierre had them sally forth against the dragon outside Carinthia’s walls. Once they 
charged it, the dragon did not seem surprised, in fact, it simply flew over them into the city 
where it wreaked a little havoc in a civilian area before being driven out. Pierre was banished for 
dereliction of duty: a hard blow for his ego. His sole joy is military command, but moreso victory, 
and he has now found a home for his skills with less institutional restraint, though his squires 
are rougher around the edges. More to work with, he thinks. 
Quiss spied Pierre’s polished armor through a gap in the tents, the point of his lance cast sparks 
of light above the tops as it waved; it was hard to catch him outside of that armor. She kept 
away from his torchlit clearing, avoiding him and whatever remarks he may have in reserve for 
her with his idle criticisms of everything from decorum to tactical efficiency. She needed to 
focus. She had a long day ahead.  
Nearing the edge of the camp she approached a richly adorned tent that gaudily stood out from 
the prevailing canvasses: a purple, black, and gold, trellised and velvet pavilion. From within 
Quiss could hear the clinking of coins. Inside was a man with a pointed beard and oiled black 
hair in matching robes to the tent, stacking coins from a purse next to an open chest. Quiss 
poked her head inside, coughing to catch the attention of the magician. “Megrind, you already 
know the plan. You already know about the fog bank to mask our troops ’approach, but I need a 
storm mid encampment, bolts, thunder, wind, something to get them disordered, and all on my 
signal.” Quiss held up her flute, which could carry great ranges at lower frequencies. Megrind 
cocked an eyebrow, rolling a coin across his fingers, “It will cost you, of course.” Quiss extended 
 
 
a small bag of coins. “And one more thing. Clear the sky for the new moon,” she said, tossing 
him the bag. Megrind nodded slightly, collecting the bag and adding the coins to his stacks.  
Megrind had once been a promising sorcerer out of Shurevaire but one who was avaricious to a 
fault. He got in to criminal schemes through visiting Avosan pirates to the island nation and by 
graduation he had dovetailed his studies of environmental pneuma toward these buccaneer 
outfits. Before long he was crewing his own boat and was known across the Serveaus Sea as 
Megrind the Monsoon, for his ability to concoct assaultive weather when assailing a shipping 
vessel. Megrind’s greed was such that he racked up a reputation of skimming shares of the 
booty from his crew, inventing fees, or delaying payments indefinitely, which eventually led to a 
mutiny. He fled Avosan society persona non grata. 
Quiss exited toward the last trace of flame she could see, lonely before the deep, darkening 
desert. She could see the banner of the Crimson Cleavers above it just touched by the fire’s 
light. Manned guard towers had been hastily erected around the banners. Most of the soldiers in 
this part of the camp had retired to their tents. Only two figures remained seated by the fire: a 
hairless orc with pearl white skin streaked in scars, taller than any orc Quiss had seen by at 
least a foot, stone silent but with a vaguely plaintive expression, a massive maul leaned against 
his shoulder as tall as a normal human; and across from him was a young halfling woman with 
wild blond hair, brass plates bedecked to her sooty work clothes as befit her role as the 
company’s armorer, weaponsmith, and more to her passion, chemist and bombardier. She 
spoke rapidly to herself as she sketched in a notebook, erasing and rescribbling.  
Quiss stopped a few feet from the halfling. “Have you got my weapons and arbalest tuned up for 
me?” The woman continued her scribbling and manic gibberish. “HAVE you got my arbalest 
tuned up for me?” She kept scribbling. “I heard you the first time. Just a moment. They’re on the 
workbench in my tent. Don’t touch anything. You might die. Haha.” 
Quiss entered, inhaling an acrid chemical air, and saw her heavy crossbow laid out on the 
bench, smoothly polished, lubricated, and finely tinkered to shoot with maximum power and 
precision. Her case of bolts leaned against the bench with a length of rope and grappling device 
nearby. “I took the liberty of adding serrated notches to your grapple. You’ll need it for the kind 
of stone cliffs you’re climbing.” The dwarf’s voice entered the tent flap before she did. “See any 
of my new work?” Quiss was still taking shallow breathes as she turned to examine the boiling 
decanters and tilted alembics, concocting who knows what. “These things when pressed to a 
catalytic medium will change the world!” the halfling spoke with unrestrained glee. Quiss replied, 
“Just make sure I’m far away if and when you use them, Dyna.” “Remarkable work as ever,” she 
said motioning toward her weapon. “Yes, yes, yes. Petty work. Any halfwit weaponsmith could 
do that,” Dyna replied with a wave.  
Like most of the Cleavers ’ranks, Dyna Dee had come from D’yadya, an orphan of the invasion, 
fostered under Vociran care and education, but in her case, she was selected for her special 
aptitude at an early age to be the servant of a prominent Vociran military engineer. He 
underestimated her aptitude, however, and secretly she picked up on Vociterra’s new 
frightening technologies they brought to Halinor, eventually learning how to better it to ghastly 
 
 
ends. Growing up near such an efficiency minded war engineer eroded her ethical grounding. 
When the scientist discovered her working on one of her private experiments, Dyna had no 
qualms testing the results on him. Little remained but what could be scraped off the ceiling. The 
Vocirans assumed his halfling assistant was caught in the blast as well, not having safely 
regained her freedom through the hole in the wall, new name and new aims in tow. 
Quiss strapped the heavy weapon and gear to her built in metal clips. “Sure you don’t want to 
take a taste of the future???” Dyna encouraged her, palming a glass globe of frothy orange-pink 
liquid with a small permeable bag tied to the stopper. Quiss cocked an eyebrow, “Maybe next 
time. Try not to blow up the camp.” Dyna rolled her eyes. “It would be hard to now they got me 
working on the perimeter. Soldier protests! Safety hazard! Blah blah blah. I still got Molko to 
keep me company!” 
Quiss had already departed Dyna’s lab. The hulking orc did not rouse as Quiss passed him, 
approaching the edge of the desert. From behind, a low murmur reached her, “Careful.” Quiss 
turned back but Molko still stared into the fire. 
Molko Beambearer. No one knew his real name or past. He was likely cast out from the 
Tartarr’kha clan that followed the old ways for his deformity, and thanks to his preternatural 
strength, he clung to life. Sharl had ‘freed ’him from a traveling sideshow where the barkers 
offered a purse to anyone who could best the albino orc in unarmed combat. Quiss had been 
there. She saw Sharl read Molko’s depth with that look of burning possession, then hop into the 
ring. He glided past most of Molko’s wide salvo of blows, but enough landed to bloody the man. 
At the decisive moment, the moment Sharl had been waiting for, Molko overcommitted his 
staggering weight to a strike, and before the orc could fully register what had happened, he had 
fallen hard on his stomach with his thrusting arm pulled behind him in Sharl’s grip, ceased in a 
bolt of pain. Molko could not move without breaking it. Sharl leaned down, to where he and 
Molko could be eye to eye, “And now you are mine.” Quiss delivered the key to his chains that 
night in his cell. Molko caught up with the traveling warriors down the road in the night. He never 
said a word.  
Quiss picked up her pace to a light jog, toward the miles of hills and sloping river beds hiding 
their fires from the strikers ’camp at the head of the mine. Rough terrain, even for a scout, but 
this was her time. Quiss felt a surge whenever she looked up and saw the waning sliver of the 
crescent moon, more luminous at its near eclipse, smiling down on her. She was taking the long 
way around, through the lowlands and the canyons, slower as she drew nearer, so as not to be 
seen. The miners ’camp was half encircled by hundreds of feet of a mountain face, their forces 
entrenched back to the entrance of the mine.  
Quiss circumferenced the mountain meticulously at a distance, minding every sound and 
movement, till she followed a river bed slope into a narrow canyon. The ground softened as the 
walls of the thin canyon scaled high above her. At her feet, she could see by the light of the 
moon, footsteps in the dirt, relatively fresh, a few days old. Quiss quietly traced them deeper 
into the canyon until she reached a shallow pool, a plate of silver in the gloom. Small shrubs 
grew on its borders converging into a blackened maw, a cave entrance, mostly boarded up. “A 
 
 
drainage shaft,” Quiss thought. “Smuggling in food through here must be how the strikers have 
held out this long.” She backtracked to examine the track patterns and could surmise by their 
relative faintness these secret deliveries happened once every two days or so, with the most 
recent yesterday. One set of tracks was smaller than the others. “A child?” This shaft had likely 
been a secret to most of the miners since there was no intelligence on this abandoned entrance. 
Quiss began to plot. 
The attack would be in a day. Undoubtedly, there would be strikers, likely its leaders, who would 
flee here as the situation turned. They had to know of its existence. And given the timetable 
of deliveries, a couple well hidden traps should go undetected in that period. Quiss diligently 
buried her bear trap in the low water at the foot of the entrance, and fastened a snare to a 
canyon wall obscured by a leaning timber. There was not much more she could do with the 
night  or materials left to her without making things too conspicuous. Quiss had after all arrived 
at the general area she intended to climb. With her traps set, she laced up her grapple and 
climbing pick and began her slow ascent to the top.  
The canyon was not more than 30 feet high but merged into a shaded crevice in the mountain 
from ages of erosion. Quiss was practically invisible within. The climb up the sheer face of rock 
was grueling for at least 150 feet before reclining into a steep slope, made worse by a sudden 
urge to pee she had to hold in. The sky purpled in the first dawning rays, but she was now able 
to shimmy up the sloped top at greater speed before anyone could find her. This mountain, 
more mesa really, was practically unassailable to an attacking force. No one would expect an 
attack from behind and above. Once on the flat top with the first rays peeking out over the 
horizon, Quiss took a look around for signs of human activity. Seeing nothing, she took a 
moment to finally relieve herself. She crawled toward the opposite side for good measure, 
crossing among the desert bushes until she reached a thicket along the ledge overlooking the 
camp. She could hear the bustle and calls from the camp below. People roused from their tents 
to dying fires. The night sentries retired with yawns. Once secure with a good view, Quiss 
stretched a net of brown leaves over her back and got comfortable for a long day of nothing at 
all, but first sleep, and gods was she tired. She bunched up her collar into a pillow and turned in.  
Quiss awoke to the sound of mass movement in the camp. The time was midafternoon. The sun 
was behind her and she could finally use the magnified scope on her heavy crossbow without 
risk of glare. She gazed out over the camp. The miners looked thin but determined, each 
clutching a ramshackle weapon or tool. Most made their way toward the exit where a crowd was 
gathering. Quiss skipped her aim up to the opening and saw a well-dressed entourage on the 
opposite side of the strikers ’barricade. She immediately recognized the representative of the 
Tarwa’kem mining concern in that entourage, who contacted the Cleavers to break the strike 
with prejudice. His boss would never show his face here. The representative was surrounded by 
heavily armed paladins for protection, imposing but not nearly enough to take the camp. The 
crowd parted for the leader of the strikers to come forward. A tall man with rugged good looks 
and bronzed skin, he moved with an inner nobility that belied his rough fashion. The company 
representative presented a big chest and a scroll to the leader, who after a cursory read, held 
up the scroll and turned to the assembled workers. He shouted something and tore the scroll in 
 
 
half to a chorus of cheers Quiss could hear from her position. Then the leader turned and kicked 
the chest over at the rep’s feet. A chant went up, “Safety, hours, leave, and pay! You won’t have 
your mine today!” Quiss enhanced the scope as best she could to clock the leader’s features. 
He was returning to a small circle of strikers who had not joined in the chant and seemed to 
deliberate things with them. “Must be his lieutenants,” Quiss thought. She clocked the ones 
turned to her. The inner circle dispersed and so did much of the camp after a salvo of rocks sent 
the company entourage into a quick retreat. Quiss tracked the leader back to his tent by the 
head of the mines. The entrance they had concealed with a pavilion that branched off to two of 
his lieutenants ’tents. She watched one of them, an older man, leave with a crate to the camp’s 
mess area, depositing it behind the cook’s quarters. He looked around but no one saw him. 
“Yep. It’s food they’re sneaking in, and they’re trying to keep the means secret just in case 
there’s a spy.” 
For hours Quiss continued her surveillance, noting patrols, locations, and routes. Night was 
rolling over the desert again. The fires shot to life throughout the camp. She scanned back to 
the open tent of the leader. He appeared to be occasionally looking up at a charcoal portrait of a 
woman and child while writing a letter. A din of drums erupted from the center of the camp as 
the night’s entertainment took up in song and dance around a bonfire. Her scope followed the 
leader as he got up to join the festivities. His other lieutenants all eventually meandered there, 
too. Quiss looked up and saw the clouds clearing unnaturally to wreath the shadowy face of the 
new moon in wan light. “Megrind,” she noted. She aimed out deep into the desert and saw a fog 
bank moving along the horizon from behind a long hill. “Almost time.” She took a red chalk piece 
from her pouch and drew the sign of Meninos on a rock near her. She set her bolts to her side 
and prepared her flute. Quiss loaded the first bolt, pulling back the firing mechanism. She 
quivered with excitement as she scanned the revelers for a high value target. The leader was 
surrounded by the miners, leading them in a dance, but toward the edge, leaning against a 
wooden beam was the lieutenant who secreted the food crate. Quiss took aim. Primed the 
trigger.  
THUNK. A whistling followed by seconds of silence. She kept her sights on the leaning old man, 
too old to dance, but smiling. In a moment his smile burst into a gored maw. His body was flung 
against the beam, pinned by the bolt through his mouth. A confused agony shocked his features 
before he collapsed, held up like a macabre scarecrow. Quiss grinned at her marksmanship and 
began loading another bolt. She looked again to see the shock of the crowd spread to take in 
the sudden death, and she brought the flute to her lips and played a long low note. She watched 
the crowd all react at once to the sound, then fired again at another lieutenant, a middle aged 
matronly figure, but the frenzy of bodies by then was so great by the time the bolt arrived that it 
only clipped her shoulder, sprawling her out. She quickly regained her feet, sprinting into the 
cover of the tents. By now, calls of “Attack! Attack!” had risen from the center of the camp, 
percolating out to the alarmed population. One shout Quiss heard in isolation, and savored, 
“The new moon! It’s Petra’s Shadow! Raid incoming!!” 
The wind picked up and dense clouds began to form directly over the camp. The new moon 
remained clear behind her. Lightning burst from the cloud, exploding the bonfire, sending the 
now alarmed revelers into all out panic. Gusts knocked over tents. Another fork of lightning 
 
 
ignited a makeshift barracks and strikers surged out with their weapons in hysteria. Quiss took 
aim again at the leader who was running to gather his frenzied miners. She took her time 
calibrating the bolt’s trajectory against the wind. The third released. She watched it follow the 
leader’s course before catching a random 
striker in the back who had just that moment passed behind him. The striker collapsed against 
his legs. He saw the bolt in the man’s back, then looked up scanning the cliff. His eyes seemed 
to stop exactly on Quiss ’somehow. He shouted in fury and disappeared into a row of tents 
violently shuddering in the wind.  
Quiss racked up another lieutenant kill and maiming in the ensuing minutes of chaos before she 
heard new shouts from the front of the camp as the Cleavers armored infantry breached the 
barricade with an explosion. They were armed with maces to injure and disperse the assembled 
miners who had finally massed toward the entrance with their weapons. The matronly lieutenant 
was leading them, a pickaxe in her good arm, the other drenched in blood. The strikers were 
being beaten down in swaths by Pierre Poinsard and his men. Molko appeared at a flank 
waving his maul, upending entire men and fortifications. He drove them deeper into the camp. 
Many began to throw down their arms and flee any which way they could. The leader of the 
strikers appeared again with a fresh group of hardy men lightly armored with swords and spears 
and ran with them toward the front. They moved as if they had some training. The wind was 
dying down now but the fires still raged. Quiss struck down one of the battalion’s rearguard. The 
leader marshaled them against panic. Back toward the front they marched. They matched the 
elite armored infantry in numbers and engaged them swiftly, charging their ranks. Quiss could 
make out the ensuing clash from her cliff lookout. She was content to keep her few remaining 
bolts in reserve and observe the battle.  
The entire camp was now in absolute anarchy. Cries of “It’s the Cleavers!” echoed throughout. 
More fled. The leader’s regiment was losing ground to Pierre’s armored troops. Masses of 
miners lay wailing in agony on the ground. Pierre was engaging the matronly lieutenant. She 
arced her pickaxe toward his chest. His armor made him too slow to completely dodge and the 
blow dented his chestplate. Even through his heavy plate Quiss saw the fury roil Pierre’s body 
as he drew his sword and viciously cut her down in an instant. He cleaned the blade 
immediately above her corpse and collected his mace off the ground. The armored regiments 
began to pinch the remaining strikers against Molko and the regulars accompanying him. 
Mysterious smoke appeared behind their forces, stymying anyone thinking to join the fight in 
coughing fits. Quiss scanned the skies to see how one of Dyna’s bombs had gotten there: a 
large hawk circled the camp. “Chauni,” she thought. “You’re not getting one over on me, bitch.” 
Quiss loaded a bolt and took aim at the leader again. He and a handful of his fighters had gotten 
a couple armored soldiers on the ground and were stabbing into the gaps in their armor. She 
fired as he was commanding them, anticipating his movement. But he didn’t move, he seemed 
transfixed by something in the burning barricade. The bolt landed in front of him. He did not 
notice it. Quiss followed his eyes in frustration. Walking through the burning remains of the 
barricade was Sharl, flanked by Uriah and Ariel, the two spinning batons and a whip, Megrind 
and Dyna pulled up the rear observing their work. Sharl locked eyes with the strike leader, his 
hand planted on the double rapiers on his belt. The elf twins broke off in a skip to join the 
 
 
fighting, impish smiles frozen on their faces. A shudder went through the leader. He broke 
Sharl’s gaze, looked out over the brutality on his fellow workers, and in a moment of 
desperation, turned and fled.  
Quiss followed the leader in her scope, streaking back to his tent. He gathered his effects, the 
letter and portrait, then bolted for the mine shaft entrance. She smiled at her perspicacity, then 
got up to head down the back slope of the mountain in pursuit. But suddenly, standing for the 
first time in nearly a day, looking out over the carnage, she began to feel slightly nauseous, and 
with it, a twinge of weakness in her conviction, but in a moment, it was gone. She powered 
through the nausea crossing the top of the mountain. Quiss secured her long length of rope with 
spikes to a solid incline of the slope, double wrapped around a tree on that side, and began her 
descent. Quiss had not minded the heights on the way up but going down she must have got 
slight vertigo to again feel a spike of nauseous feeling telling her to not go down. It took her a 
moment to regain her composure, and now facing the wall strictly, she descended again. 
Over halfway down she heard a short cry. The leader had exited the drainage shaft and 
encountered a fellow miner hanging unconscious from Quiss ’snare in the wall. The strike leader 
stopped in his tracks and looked around, hugging the canyon wall. Quiss had continued her 
descent and was now only a few dozen feet overhead. The leader could hear running coming 
from behind him in the shaft. Another fleeing miner had found his way out of the abandoned 
passage. He broke free of the mine into the shallow pool and no sooner than the leader 
recognized him as one of his lieutenants, the refugee halted and doubled over in a piercing 
scream. Quiss ’submerged bear trap had closed around his leg. His wailing was awful. Again, 
Quiss felt oddly pained to hear him scream. She fought back another wave of nausea, and 
before the strike leader could take another step to rescue his friend, Quiss bellowed into the 
canyon, “You’re next!!”, punctuating it with a dog’s howl. The leader was torn. In tears, he 
shouted to his friend, “I’m sorry!” and scrambled through the narrow canyon in a stumbling run.  
Quiss lowered herself onto the edge of the canyon and immediately joined pursuit. Separated 
from the noise of the raid, she could hear the strike leader’s harried breathing in the canyon 
below. With sickness pooling in her, the weight of her gear had her at a disadvantage to catch 
up, but Quiss was athletic and redoubled her efforts. Her commitment, her love to Sharl, was 
the steel pin holding her together. The land she ran across sloped down to meet the mouth of 
the canyon. The strike leader burst out of it not too far ahead. “You don’t get to escape!!” Quiss 
practically howled out of her gasping body. The strike leader threw his head around in despair, 
tears streaming down his face, and continued on his desperate sprint into the lowlands.  
The first rays of the sun tinted the high desert beyond the riverbed. In the half light, the 
landscape emerged from the darkness. In her haste Quiss misjudged an embankment and 
rolled to the valley floor. She struggled to stand again, but she could not fail, not now. Quiss 
loaded a bolt into her arbalest. She heaved it toward the fleeing labor leader. Her head rolled 
with her stomach. The sights spun. Quiss felt within her she wanted to be anywhere but in this 
moment. She did not understand, pushed the feeling down, squared her aim, and pressed the 
trigger. The bolt flew past him. He was now too far away to catch up in her state. Quiss loaded 
her final bolt. The tips of the tall mesas gleamed. The retch quaked through her limbs. Anything 
 
 
to get out of this situation. Quiss crunched her eyes close. Sharl stared back at her in her mind. 
Anger and fear mixed with self-loathing inside her. “Must… Must...” Her eyes focused for a 
moment. She pulled the trigger. Her soul fell out. From somewhere deep within, the words 
“Miss... Miss…,” escaped her throat. No more than ragged breaths. Quiss and the leader saw 
figures split from the mesa in the shimmering distance. Joy shot through him, then the flying 
bolt. The leader fell in a plume of dust. Quiss vomited onto the cracked earth, heavier and 
emptier than ever before. That feeling would never go away.
