# Theo and Gothmog — Part 1

*Two scenes from Gothmog and Theo's time at Shurevaire: their first magic tutoring session over tea, and Ochmaa catching Gothmog lying about where he'd been.*

---

Part 1

Theo was busy pouring tea into two cups when Gothmog entered the clearing in the Shurevarian forest where they had agreed to meet. He paused before joining Theo to take in the lush greenery of the place – the ground was blanketed in velvety emerald moss and vibrant springtime flowers bloomed all around them in blatant defiance of the fact that it was now midwinter. Theo hadn’t noticed him yet  – he hummed a tune and began to arrange something on a plate. His long silvery blonde hair kept spilling over his shoulders and threatening to dip into the tea. Gothmog took a few heavy steps on purpose so as not to alarm him. Theo looked up and smiled warmly, crinkling his long nose. 

“Hello, Gothmog. Would you like a cup of tea? It’s tannelin tea from Aqualonde, my home. It’s really quite delicious, particularly when paired with these orange rolls. Though I have to give M’ylaena credit for the orange roll recipe. Please have a seat.” 

“Did you do this?” Gothmog asked, gesturing toward all of the greenery. “You grew this in the middle of winter?” 

Theo’s face brightened. “This? Just an illusion – my specialty. But I’m pleased you thought they were real. I’ve been working on giving them a scent, but I can’t quite master it. Everything ends up smelling like burned toast for some reason.” 

Gothmog knelt on the smooth stone across from him. “Thank you for agreeing to tutor me. I am ready to begin, Master Theo. I will follow your commands unquestioningly and prove to you that I am worthy, and I vow to repay this favor or may Grond tear my flesh and crack my bones.” He bowed his head low and missed Theo’s wide-eyed, open-mouthed expression. 

“Umm, it’s truly no trouble at all, Gothmog. I don’t mind helping you catch up. Professor Neridove is an amazing sorceress, but she’s hardly a talented teacher. I imagine she’d rather be inventing new spells than taking on students.” 

Gothmog couldn’t meet Theo’s eyes as he said, “I am a failure and a disgrace. When I could not even learn to cast light, Neridove told me to give up. It may be hopeless.” 

Theo frowned, “Were I to tell you that I wished to learn combat, would you give me that giant sword of yours to swing around?” 

Gothmog snorted derisively, “I don’t wish to offend you, Theo, but I doubt you could lift it. With your build, you would be much better suited to a dueling blade, perhaps a rapier. You might also consider archery.”

“Exactly,” Theo said. “Magic is much the same. It’s highly personal. One must find the right approach for each individual practitioner, lest you pair, say, a robust red with a too mild cheese.” 

“Forgive me, but I’m not following you,” said Gothmog. 

“Here, try the tea,” said Theo and pushed him a cup. Gothmog delicately picked up the tea cup and took a sip. Flavors of mint, citrus, and honey blossomed on his tongue. He closed his eyes and let out an appreciative sigh. 

“This magic-enhancing brew tastes so much better than my mother’s strength-enhancing potions,” Gothmog exclaimed. “Magic is delicious.” Theo laughed, causing a blush to creep up Gothmog’s neck. 

“It’s not a magic-enhancing potion, Gothmog. It’s just tea, I promise.” 

“If it’s not part of the lesson, then what is the purpose of drinking it?” 

“The purpose is to enjoy it.” 

Part 2

Gothmog came home from one of his magic lessons to the sound of shattering steel. He walked around the outside of the stone cottage that he shared with his mother to find her picking up a sword from a pile of weapons and swinging it over her head in a downward arc against an anvil. With an ear-ringing clang, the sword shattered into pieces. The ground around her was littered with broken and bent steel. Gothmog felt Batuhan’s cold nose nuzzle his ear. He turned and hugged the dire wolf around his neck, breathing in the smell of the forest from the wolf’s thick coat. Batuhan sniffed one of Gothmog’s pockets and stared at him meaningfully with large amber eyes. 

“Okay, okay,” Gothmog said, pulling out the sticky, slightly squished orange roll. The wolf sniffed it, gingerly took it in his jaws, and disappeared once again into the forest beside their cottage. 

“Gothmog, is that you? Come bear witness to the inferiority of Elvish craftsmanship!” cried Occhma as she destroyed another blade with a quick diagonal slash against the anvil. “This is what happens when your forge is too hot. Your — CLANG—- blades — CLANG—- become — CLANG—- brittle!” Gothmog ducked as a piece of steel came whizzing by his head. He guessed this is why Batuhan had been taking refuge in the forest.  

“Take note. A brittle sword will shatter easily. Forging them at a steady heat will create a more durable blade. These are all worthless – get over here, we’re going to have to melt all of this down and start over.” Each flexion and extension of Occhma’s powerful arms as she swung the inferior weapons demonstrated the truth of her thesis: real steel was forged through strenuous, exacting effort. Gothmog began gathering the metal into a pile by the forge. 

“It’s funny,” he said offhandedly. “Making swords is a little like brewing tea. Theo says if the water is too hot, you’ll scorch the leaves, and it will taste bitter. You can make the water hotter for certain types of leaves, like different types of steel.” Gothmog stopped dead before picking up a bent sword — he realized Occhma was staring at him with narrowed eyes. 

“I was under the impression that you were missing hours of your training on magic lessons, not on Elvish tea parties,” she said icily. 

“They are magic lessons, mother,” Gothmog said quickly. “We just had tea today because I’m learning to purify poisoned food and drink. That’s all.” He couldn’t tell by her inscrutable expression if she was mollified by this or not. “You would approve. I have to … to purify the poisoned tea before drinking it. As with dueling, there is no room for error. It requires utmost focus and precision.” She didn’t say anything to this, but retrieved two blunted training swords and handed him one. 

“Attack stance. Show me your pommel grip.” Occhma assumed a defensive stance and waited. Gothmog took a calming breath and centered himself. Without warning, he feinted to the left, waiting for the critical moment when she shifted her weight to parry the blow before abruptly changing the angle of his attack. He saw his opening and loosened his grip on the sword to let his hand slip to the pommel, giving the swing an extra boost of power. Graceful as a dancer, Occhma nimbly dodged to the side before he could connect with her right thigh and slammed the hilt of her sword into his wrist. Searing pain shot through his arm and he dropped his sword. Occhma turned on her heel and kicked him in the chest, knocking the air from his lungs and dropping him to his knees. He gasped and cradled his broken wrist. 

“You announce every attack with your eyes. My son is a bad liar. Now go tell this magic teacher that your next lesson should be healing.”
