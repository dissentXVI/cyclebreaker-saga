## Overview
The Zhikk are a Fire Giant people native to the volcanic continent of [[Zhikarr]]. For millennia they were the dominant civilization of the known world's northern reaches — a slaveholding empire of enormous physical power, theological conviction, and geological endurance. Their empire is gone, broken by the [[The Howling Winds of Y'Set|Howling Winds of Y'Set]] and the [[The Fati Imperium|Fati Imperium]] rebellion that gave birth to [[Vociterra]]. What survives is a scattered collection of clans, each a fragment of something that was once terrifying in its wholeness.

## Physiology
The Zhikk are Fire Giants — towering, broad-framed humanoids standing between ten and thirteen feet tall, with dense heat-resistant hides that range from deep charcoal to volcanic obsidian black. Their skin carries a faint internal warmth, visible as a dull ember glow along the joints and jawline in darkness. Their eyes are uniformly amber or deep orange, adapted to seeing through ash and smoke rather than open light. They are long-lived by mortal standards, with lifespans extending several centuries, and physically they are among the most resilient beings in Aspiria — able to endure extreme heat, prolonged ash exposure, and cold that would kill lesser creatures. Their hearing and tremor-sense are exceptional; their long-distance vision, dulled by generations in ash-laden air, is comparatively poor.

## Culture & Society

##### The Old Empire
Before [[The Howling Winds of Y'Set|Y'Set]], the Zhikk were a unified civilization. Their empire was theocratic and rigidly hierarchical — organized around three castes: the **Keth** (warrior-nobles who ruled by strength and bloodline), the **Vorath** (a priestly caste that interpreted volcanic activity as divine communication), and the **Grul** (a laboring underclass that included both low-caste Zhikk and the enslaved peoples they took from beyond Zhikarr's shores). The Keth held absolute authority; the Vorath legitimized it; the Grul and enslaved sustained it. This structure persisted for over a thousand years.

##### Faith & The Burning Below
The Zhikk do not worship a named god in the conventional sense. Their theology centers on **Karath-Vul** — the Burning Below — a term for the volcanic force they believe animates all of Zhikarr. To the Zhikk, Karath-Vul is not a being but a will — the primal drive of the world to consume, reshape, and endure. Eruptions are not disasters; they are judgments. The empire grew in the highlands because the Vorath read the ash-fall and lava flows as instructions. Slavery was theological: those taken were the weak, and the weak existed to serve the chosen.

The Vorath still practice these rites in the surviving clans, though with less certainty than before. The cataclysm shook their faith — Karath-Vul had turned against them, or so it seemed. The more conservative elders hold that this was a test. The more pragmatic hold it in silence.

##### After Y'Set — The Clan Age
The [[The Howling Winds of Y'Set|Howling Winds of Y'Set]] did not destroy the Zhikk. They fractured them. The empire's centralized supply and governance collapsed as ash storms buried highland roads and disrupted volcanic reading for generations. Clan-states formed around the old Keth bloodlines, each claiming legitimacy from the empire's pre-cataclysm structure, none possessing the resources to enforce it across the whole continent. The Grul and enslaved populations were retained — divided among the clans rather than unified under a single master — but the coordination that had made the empire unbreakable was gone.

This fragmentation persisted for nine centuries. The clans feuded, forged short alliances, and collapsed back into rivalry. By the time [[Meonus Magnus]] arrived among the enslaved in 3920 ApN, no single Zhikk clan controlled enough of the continent to respond decisively to an uprising. The rebellion exploited the gaps between them, clan by clan, and by 3925 ApN the enslaved had escaped across the sea.

##### The Clans Today
The surviving Zhikk clans hold the interior highlands of Zhikarr. They are smaller in number, poorer in resources, and more insular than their imperial ancestors. The old caste structure survives in form if not in function — Keth elders still lead, Vorath still interpret the vents, Grul still labor — but without an empire to sustain, the distinctions have blurred at the edges. Some clans have enslaved other peoples again in small numbers. Most subsist on volcanic resource extraction, hunting, and intra-clan raiding.

They do not discuss the rebellion openly. In Zhikk oral tradition, the events of 3925 ApN are called **the Ashwalker's Treachery** — framed not as a defeat but as a poisoning, an unnatural event caused by a figure of uncanny and inhuman power who moved through their enslaved population like something older than memory. This framing preserves their pride while encoding a genuine instinct: whatever Meonus was, he was not mortal.

## Relations With the Wider World
The Zhikk are largely unknown to the peoples of [[Halinor]] and [[Eldamar]] in the current age. [[Vociterra]] knows what they are — their founding mythology is built on escaping them — but Vociran official history frames the Zhikk as a conquered obstacle rather than an ongoing threat, and the details of that history are state-controlled. The Zhikk themselves have no interest in diplomacy and no means of projecting power beyond Zhikarr's shores in their current state.

That could change if they unified.

## In Play
The Zhikk are not currently an active faction in the campaign. Their significance is historical and potential — as the origin point of Vociran civilization, as a surviving remnant with a grievance that spans a century, and as a people whose fractured pride makes them dangerous in ways a more stable enemy might not be.

> [!dm] DM Only — Not Revealed to Players
> The Zhikk's oral tradition of the Ashwalker is functionally accurate. Psyronos — one of the Archorans — orchestrated the rebellion for purposes that had nothing to do with liberating the enslaved. He shaped the Vociran people into an empire because he needed a godless, technology-driven civilization as a long-game instrument. The Zhikk were an obstacle to that project, not its target. If players ever encounter Zhikk and earn their confidence, the Ashwalker legend could become a thread that leads back toward the Archorans. The Zhikk do not know what Psyronos is, but they know what he was not.
